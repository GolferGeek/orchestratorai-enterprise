# Expert Witness Engagement Protocol for Technology Litigation

## Purpose and Scope

This protocol establishes comprehensive procedures for engaging, preparing, and presenting expert witnesses in technology litigation, with emphasis on Daubert challenges and the unique evidentiary issues arising from AI systems, software platforms, data security, and technology-related disputes. Expert testimony is frequently the decisive factor in technology cases, where the subject matter exceeds the understanding of lay jurors and generalist judges. The selection, preparation, and defense of expert witnesses requires a systematic approach that begins at the earliest stages of the litigation.

---

## 1. Expert Witness Identification and Selection

### 1.1 Types of Experts in Technology Litigation

**Technical Experts:**

| Specialty | Use Case | Typical Background |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| Software Engineering | Source code analysis, software defect identification, development practices | Senior software engineers, CTOs, computer science professors |
| AI/Machine Learning | Model behavior analysis, training data assessment, output reliability | AI researchers, ML engineers, data scientists with PhD-level credentials |
| Cybersecurity | Data breach causation, security standard compliance, penetration testing analysis | CISOs, security consultants, forensic analysts with certifications (CISSP, CISM, CEH) |
| Digital Forensics | Data recovery, evidence authentication, access log analysis, timeline reconstruction | Certified forensic examiners (EnCE, ACE, CFCE), law enforcement digital forensics specialists |
| Database and Data Management | Data integrity, database administration practices, data migration, backup procedures | Database architects, data engineers with enterprise experience |
| Cloud Computing | Cloud architecture, SaaS deployment, infrastructure security, service level compliance | Cloud architects with certifications (AWS Solutions Architect, Azure Solutions Architect) |
| Networking | Network security, traffic analysis, intrusion detection, VPN and access control | Network engineers with CCNA/CCNP or equivalent, security analysts |

**Damages Experts:**

| Specialty | Use Case | Typical Background |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| Lost Profits | Revenue impact analysis, market share erosion, customer churn quantification | Forensic accountants (CPA, CFF, ABV), financial analysts |
| Reasonable Royalty | Patent and trade secret damages, technology licensing valuation | Licensing professionals, IP valuation specialists, economists |
| Unjust Enrichment | Defendant's profits attributable to the wrongful conduct | Forensic accountants with experience tracing profits to specific products or features |
| Cost of Remediation | Data breach response costs, system rebuild costs, security upgrade costs | IT consultants with cost estimation experience, project managers |
| Business Valuation | Enterprise value diminution, startup valuation impact | Certified business appraisers (ASA, CVA), investment bankers |

**Industry Experts:**

| Specialty | Use Case | Typical Background |
|-----------|----------|-------------------|
| Standard of Care | Industry practices, reasonable security measures, development standards | Former executives in the relevant technology sector, industry association leaders |
| Technology Market | Competitive dynamics, market definition, technology adoption patterns | Market analysts, technology consultants, academic researchers |
| Regulatory Compliance | Data protection, industry-specific regulations, compliance frameworks | Former regulators, compliance consultants, privacy officers |

### 1.2 Selection Criteria

**Qualifications:**
- Advanced degree in the relevant technical field (PhD strongly preferred for academic experts; equivalent industry experience for practitioner experts)
- Demonstrated expertise through publications, patents, conference presentations, or industry recognition
- Hands-on experience with the specific technology at issue (not just general knowledge of the field)
- Prior expert witness experience (beneficial but not required; a first-time expert with superior qualifications may be more credible than a professional expert with lesser credentials)

**Credibility factors:**
- No significant professional controversies or disciplinary actions
- No history of opinions being excluded under Daubert
- Track record of testifying for both plaintiffs and defendants (reduces the appearance of bias)
- Clear and effective communication skills (the expert must be able to explain complex technology to a lay audience)
- Professional demeanor under cross-examination

**Disqualifying factors:**
- Conflict of interest (current or former relationship with the opposing party)
- Opinions that are inconsistent with positions taken in prior cases or publications
- Methodology that has been rejected by courts in prior Daubert challenges
- Inability to explain the basis for opinions in accessible terms
- Over-reliance on litigation work (courts and juries are suspicious of "professional witnesses")

### 1.3 Engagement Process

1. **Conflict check:** Before any substantive discussions, verify that the expert has no conflicts of interest with any party, witness, or counsel in the case.
2. **Preliminary assessment:** Provide the expert with a high-level summary of the case (without disclosing work product) and obtain a preliminary assessment of whether the expert can provide favorable opinions. This preliminary assessment may be covered by the consulting expert privilege if the expert is retained only as a consultant at this stage.
3. **Retention letter:** Execute a written engagement agreement specifying:
   - Scope of the engagement
   - Hourly rate and billing terms
   - Estimated budget for the engagement
   - Confidentiality obligations
   - Understanding that the expert may be designated as a testifying expert and subject to disclosure requirements
   - Understanding that the expert's opinions must be based on the expert's independent analysis, not directed by counsel
4. **Document production:** Provide the expert with the relevant documents, data, and materials needed to form opinions. Maintain a complete log of materials provided.

**Critical practice note:** Under Fed. R. Civ. P. 26(b)(4)(C), communications between counsel and testifying experts are generally protected from discovery, except for communications that:
- Relate to the expert's compensation
- Identify facts or data counsel provided that the expert considered in forming opinions
- Identify assumptions counsel provided that the expert relied upon in forming opinions

Draft communications with testifying experts carefully. Do not provide written advocacy materials or legal arguments to the expert. Do not draft or substantially edit the expert's report. The expert's opinions must be genuinely the expert's own.

## 2. Disclosure Requirements

### 2.1 Federal Rule 26(a)(2) Disclosures

**Expert report required (Rule 26(a)(2)(B))** for witnesses "retained or specially employed to provide expert testimony":

The report must contain:
1. A complete statement of all opinions the witness will express and the basis and reasons for them
2. The facts or data considered by the witness in forming them
3. Any exhibits that will be used to summarize or support them
4. The witness's qualifications, including all publications authored in the preceding 10 years
5. A list of all cases in which the witness testified as an expert at trial or by deposition during the preceding 4 years
6. A statement of the compensation to be paid for the study and testimony in the case

**Timing:** Expert disclosures must be made at the times directed by the court, typically 90 days before trial for the party bearing the burden of proof and 30 days after for rebuttal experts (Fed. R. Civ. P. 26(a)(2)(D)).

**Expert summary required (Rule 26(a)(2)(C))** for witnesses not retained or specially employed (e.g., an employee testifying about the company's technology practices):

The disclosure must state:
1. The subject matter on which the witness is expected to present evidence
2. A summary of the facts and opinions to which the witness is expected to testify

### 2.2 Supplementation Obligations

Under Fed. R. Civ. P. 26(e)(2), expert disclosures must be supplemented if the expert's opinions change or additional information becomes available. Supplementation must be made by the time the party's pretrial disclosures are due (typically 30 days before trial). Late supplementation may result in exclusion of the new or changed opinions.

### 2.3 Minnesota Disclosure Requirements

Minnesota Rule of Civil Procedure 26.02(f) requires disclosure of expert witnesses and their opinions. The specific requirements and timing are governed by the court's scheduling order. Minnesota does not require the same detailed written report as the federal rules, but many courts impose report requirements through scheduling orders.

## 3. Expert Report Preparation

### 3.1 Report Structure

A well-organized expert report should follow this structure:

**I. Introduction and Engagement**
- Identification of the expert and the engaging party
- Summary of the assignment
- Statement that the opinions are based on the expert's independent analysis

**II. Qualifications**
- Education, training, and professional experience
- Publications, patents, and presentations
- Prior expert testimony (list of cases)
- Attach full CV as an exhibit

**III. Materials Considered**
- Complete list of all documents, data, depositions, and other materials the expert reviewed
- Identification of any materials provided by counsel
- This list is discoverable and will be scrutinized — include everything considered, even materials that do not support your opinions

**IV. Technical Background**
- Explanation of the relevant technology in accessible terms
- Provide sufficient context for the reader (judge, opposing counsel, jury) to understand the opinions
- For AI cases: explain the relevant model architecture, training process, and deployment methodology
- For software cases: explain the relevant code architecture, development processes, and testing practices
- For cybersecurity cases: explain the relevant security frameworks, standards, and common attack vectors

**V. Analysis**
- Step-by-step analysis applying the expert's methodology to the facts
- Identify and discuss each piece of evidence considered
- Address alternative explanations and explain why they are less persuasive
- For damages experts: show all calculations with supporting data

**VI. Opinions**
- State each opinion clearly and specifically
- For each opinion, cross-reference the analysis that supports it
- Number the opinions for easy reference in depositions, motions, and trial

**VII. Exhibits**
- Source code comparisons (annotated side-by-side)
- System architecture diagrams
- Timeline reconstructions
- Damages calculations
- Statistical analyses
- Demonstrative charts and graphs

### 3.2 Technology-Specific Report Considerations

**AI and Machine Learning Reports:**
- Explain the specific model architecture and training methodology at issue
- If comparing AI models, use accepted comparison methodologies (not just anecdotal input/output comparisons)
- Address the stochastic nature of AI outputs — explain that identical inputs may produce different outputs, and that this does not undermine the analysis
- For training data disputes, apply statistical methods to demonstrate overlap or independence
- Document the expert's methodology for examining the AI system (what tools were used, what tests were run, what access was provided)

**Source Code Analysis Reports:**
- Use accepted code comparison tools (e.g., MOSS, Black Duck, Winmerge) and document the methodology
- Present comparisons at multiple levels: file structure, function organization, algorithm implementation, variable naming, comments, and idiom
- Address the significance of similarities (could they result from common industry practices, standard libraries, or independent development?)
- Quantify the degree of similarity using established metrics
- Address non-literal copying (structure, sequence, and organization) in addition to literal copying

**Cybersecurity Reports:**
- Reference applicable security standards and frameworks (NIST CSF, ISO 27001, CIS Controls, OWASP)
- Document the specific vulnerabilities or failures identified
- Establish causation between the security failures and the breach
- Address the reasonableness of the security measures at the time of the breach (not with the benefit of hindsight)
- Quantify the scope of the breach (records affected, data types exposed, duration of exposure)

**Damages Reports:**
- Present multiple damages scenarios if the outcome depends on disputed factual issues
- Use accepted valuation methodologies (comparable transactions, income approach, cost approach, market approach)
- For reasonable royalty calculations in technology cases, apply the Georgia-Pacific factors (Georgia-Pacific Corp. v. U.S. Plywood Corp., 318 F. Supp. 1116 (S.D.N.Y. 1970)) or their equivalents
- For lost profits, establish the causal connection between the wrongful conduct and the lost revenue with specificity
- Address the apportionment problem: what portion of the defendant's revenue or the plaintiff's losses is attributable to the specific technology at issue vs. other factors?

### 3.3 Common Report Mistakes

1. **Assuming facts not in evidence.** The report must identify the specific evidence supporting each factual assumption. An opinion based on unsupported assumptions is vulnerable to exclusion.
2. **Legal conclusions.** The expert opines on facts, not law. "The defendant's security measures were inadequate by industry standards" is appropriate. "The defendant was negligent" is a legal conclusion for the jury.
3. **Advocacy.** The report should read as an objective analysis, not a brief. Argumentative language, pejorative characterizations, and one-sided presentations damage credibility.
4. **Incomplete materials list.** If the expert reviewed a document but did not include it in the materials list, the omission will be exploited on cross-examination.
5. **Lack of methodology.** Opinions must be the product of reliable principles and methods (Daubert). If the report does not explain the methodology, the opinion is vulnerable to exclusion.
6. **Overly complex presentation.** The report must be understandable to non-experts. If the judge cannot understand the report, the opinions carry no weight.

## 4. Expert Deposition Preparation

### 4.1 Pre-Deposition Preparation

- [ ] Review the expert report thoroughly with the expert, ensuring the expert can explain every opinion and every element of the analysis
- [ ] Review all materials the expert considered (opposing counsel will test whether the expert actually read and understood every item on the materials list)
- [ ] Review the expert's prior testimony, publications, and public statements for potential impeachment material
- [ ] Conduct a mock deposition focusing on the areas most likely to be challenged
- [ ] Prepare the expert for common deposition tactics:
  - "Is it possible that..." questions (the answer is almost always yes; the expert should explain why the alternative is less likely)
  - "Did counsel tell you to say..." questions (the expert should confirm that all opinions are independently formed)
  - "Have you ever seen a case where..." questions designed to get the expert to overstate their experience
  - Rapid-fire questions designed to elicit inconsistencies or premature concessions

### 4.2 Deposition Conduct Rules for the Expert

1. **Listen to the complete question before answering.** Do not anticipate or assume what the questioner is asking.
2. **Answer only the question asked.** Do not volunteer additional information. Do not explain or elaborate unless the question asks for an explanation.
3. **If you do not understand the question, say so.** "I don't understand the question" is always an appropriate response.
4. **If you do not know the answer, say so.** "I don't know" is always an appropriate response. Do not speculate.
5. **Do not guess.** If the answer requires calculation, analysis, or review of documents, say so rather than guessing.
6. **Be precise.** Use the same terminology consistently. Do not use informal or imprecise language that could be mischaracterized.
7. **Do not argue with the questioning attorney.** Answer the question and wait for the next one.
8. **Review documents carefully before answering questions about them.** Do not rely on opposing counsel's characterization of a document.
9. **Maintain composure.** Cross-examination is designed to be uncomfortable. A composed, patient expert is far more credible than a defensive or argumentative one.

### 4.3 Deposition Scope

Opposing counsel may inquire into:
- Everything the expert considered in forming opinions (documents, data, conversations with counsel)
- The expert's qualifications, including the limits of their expertise
- The expert's methodology and its acceptance in the relevant field
- The expert's prior testimony and whether current opinions are consistent
- The expert's compensation and the proportion of income derived from expert witness work
- Alternative explanations that the expert considered and rejected
- Assumptions underlying the expert's opinions

## 5. Daubert Challenges

### 5.1 The Daubert Standard

Under Daubert v. Merrell Dow Pharmaceuticals, Inc., 509 U.S. 579 (1993), and its progeny (Joiner, Kumho Tire), the trial court serves as a "gatekeeper" to ensure that expert testimony is both relevant and reliable.

**Relevance (Fed. R. Evid. 702(a)):** The expert's testimony must "help the trier of fact to understand the evidence or to determine a fact in issue." The expert's specialized knowledge must connect to the facts of the case.

**Reliability (Fed. R. Evid. 702(b)-(d)):** The testimony must be:
- Based on sufficient facts or data
- The product of reliable principles and methods
- The result of reliably applying the principles and methods to the facts of the case

**Daubert factors (non-exclusive):**
1. Whether the theory or technique can be and has been tested
2. Whether it has been subjected to peer review and publication
3. Its known or potential rate of error
4. The existence and maintenance of standards controlling the technique's operation
5. Whether it is generally accepted in the relevant scientific community

### 5.2 Daubert Challenges Specific to Technology Experts

**AI/ML experts:**
- Challenge: the methodology for comparing AI models is novel and has not been subjected to peer review
- Challenge: the expert's conclusions about model behavior are based on black-box testing that cannot reliably determine the model's internal workings
- Challenge: the expert lacks hands-on experience with the specific type of AI system at issue (a natural language processing expert may not be qualified to opine on computer vision systems)
- Defense: the expert used accepted testing methodologies from the AI research community; the expert's credentials include hands-on experience building and deploying similar systems; the expert's conclusions are consistent with the documentary evidence

**Cybersecurity experts:**
- Challenge: the expert's opinion on the "reasonableness" of security measures is based on hindsight analysis rather than the standards in effect at the time
- Challenge: the expert's forensic methodology did not follow accepted digital forensics practices (e.g., did not create a verified forensic image before analysis)
- Challenge: the expert's causation opinion is speculative (the breach could have been caused by factors other than the identified vulnerability)
- Defense: the expert applied recognized security frameworks (NIST, CIS Controls) that were in effect at the relevant time; the expert's forensic methodology followed accepted practices and is documented; causation is established through timeline analysis and process of elimination

**Software engineering experts:**
- Challenge: source code comparison methodology is subjective and not a recognized scientific technique
- Challenge: the expert's opinion on "independent development" is based on the absence of evidence rather than affirmative evidence of copying
- Challenge: the similarities identified could result from common programming practices, standard libraries, or functional constraints (scenes a faire in the copyright context)
- Defense: the expert used accepted code comparison tools and quantitative similarity metrics; the expert identified non-functional similarities (variable names, comments, stylistic choices) that would not be expected in independent development; the expert controlled for common practices and standard libraries

**Damages experts:**
- Challenge: the lost profits calculation is speculative and relies on unsupported assumptions about but-for market performance
- Challenge: the reasonable royalty analysis does not properly apportion between the value of the patented/secret technology and the value of other factors
- Challenge: the damages model has not been tested or peer-reviewed
- Defense: the lost profits model is based on historical financial data and conservative growth assumptions; apportionment is addressed through multiple methodologies; the damages model follows accepted financial analysis practices

### 5.3 Defending Against Daubert Challenges

When your expert faces a Daubert challenge:

1. **File a thorough opposition.** Address each of the opponent's challenges specifically. Do not simply argue that the expert is qualified — address the methodology and application challenges.
2. **Submit a supplemental declaration from the expert.** Address the specific methodological criticisms and explain why the methodology is reliable and properly applied.
3. **Cite cases admitting similar expert testimony.** Find cases in which courts have admitted the type of expert testimony at issue under similar circumstances.
4. **Request a Daubert hearing.** If the motion is contested, a hearing gives the expert an opportunity to explain the methodology directly to the court. This is often more effective than written submissions.
5. **Distinguish the challenged aspects from the rest of the testimony.** Even if some opinions are excluded, argue that the remaining opinions should be admitted. Partial exclusion is preferable to complete exclusion.

### 5.4 Minnesota Standard

Minnesota follows the Frye-Mack standard for scientific evidence (State v. Mack, 292 N.W.2d 764 (Minn. 1980)), which requires that the scientific evidence be "generally accepted in the relevant scientific community." For non-scientific expert testimony, Minnesota applies the Rule 702 helpfulness standard. The distinction matters: for AI-related testimony that is characterized as scientific, the general acceptance requirement applies; for testimony characterized as technical or specialized knowledge, the more flexible helpfulness standard applies.

## 6. Trial Presentation

### 6.1 Direct Examination

**Qualification phase:**
- Walk through the expert's education, training, experience, and publications
- Establish specific expertise in the technology at issue
- Tender the witness as an expert (some courts skip the formal tender)
- Anticipate and address potential qualification challenges during direct rather than waiting for cross

**Opinion presentation:**
- Start with the bottom-line opinions, then walk through the supporting analysis
- Use demonstrative exhibits — diagrams, timelines, annotated code comparisons, system architecture charts
- Have the expert explain technical concepts in plain language
- Use analogies to connect complex technology to everyday experience
- Pace the examination to allow the jury to absorb the information
- Repeat key points through different framings

**For technology experts specifically:**
- Consider live demonstrations if the technology can be demonstrated safely and reliably
- Use screenshots and screen recordings to illustrate system behavior
- Present source code in annotated, highlighted excerpts rather than raw code
- For AI testimony, use concrete input/output examples rather than abstract descriptions of algorithms
- For damages testimony, use clear financial summaries and charts rather than complex spreadsheets

### 6.2 Cross-Examination Preparation

Prepare the expert for the most likely cross-examination attacks:

1. **Bias attack:** "You are being paid $X to testify, correct?" Prepare the expert to respond calmly and to distinguish compensation for time from compensation for opinions.
2. **Qualifications attack:** "You have never designed an AI system like the one at issue, have you?" Prepare the expert to explain why their qualifications are sufficient even if they have not built the exact system at issue.
3. **Methodology attack:** "No peer-reviewed study has validated your methodology, correct?" Prepare the expert to explain why the methodology is reliable despite the absence of a specific peer-reviewed validation study.
4. **Prior inconsistency attack:** "In [prior case], you testified that [X]. Today you are saying [Y]. Which is it?" Prepare the expert by reviewing all prior testimony and publications and ensuring that current opinions are consistent or that differences are explainable.
5. **Concession attack:** "You would agree that it is possible that [alternative explanation], correct?" Prepare the expert to acknowledge possibilities while explaining why they are less likely than the expert's conclusion.
6. **Omission attack:** "You did not consider [factor], did you?" Prepare the expert to explain either why the factor was considered (and address it) or why it was not relevant to the analysis.

## 7. Expert Engagement Checklist

**Selection Phase:**
- [ ] Conflict check completed
- [ ] Expert's qualifications reviewed (CV, publications, prior testimony)
- [ ] Preliminary assessment obtained
- [ ] Daubert history reviewed (has the expert been excluded before?)
- [ ] Prior testimony reviewed for consistency with anticipated opinions
- [ ] Retention letter executed
- [ ] Budget established and approved by client

**Report Preparation Phase:**
- [ ] All relevant materials provided to the expert (with complete log)
- [ ] Expert's independent analysis completed
- [ ] Report drafted by the expert (not by counsel)
- [ ] Report reviewed by counsel for legal sufficiency (without directing the expert's opinions)
- [ ] Report served within the deadline specified by the court's scheduling order
- [ ] Materials list verified for completeness

**Deposition Phase:**
- [ ] Preparation session conducted
- [ ] Mock cross-examination conducted on key issues
- [ ] Prior testimony and publications reviewed for potential impeachment
- [ ] Expert prepared for common deposition tactics
- [ ] Logistics arranged (location, videographer, exhibits)

**Daubert Phase (if challenged):**
- [ ] Opposition brief filed addressing each specific challenge
- [ ] Supplemental declaration from expert filed
- [ ] Supporting case law identified and cited
- [ ] Daubert hearing requested (if appropriate)
- [ ] Fallback positions identified (partial exclusion, narrowing of opinions)

**Trial Phase:**
- [ ] Direct examination outline prepared
- [ ] Demonstrative exhibits prepared and approved
- [ ] Expert prepared for cross-examination
- [ ] Technology demonstrations tested (if applicable)
- [ ] Rebuttal testimony planned (to address opponent's experts)
