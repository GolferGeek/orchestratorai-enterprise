# Revocable Living Trust Agreement — Annotated Template

**Document Type:** Template with Practice Notes  
**Jurisdiction:** Minnesota (primary), Florida (secondary)  
**Last Updated:** April 2026  
**Applicable Law:** Minn. Stat. Chapter 501C (Minnesota Trust Code); Fla. Stat. Chapter 736 (Florida Trust Code)  

---

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** This template creates a revocable living trust for a married grantor. It serves as the primary estate planning vehicle, designed to receive assets during life and at death via pour-over will. The trust avoids probate for funded assets, provides incapacity management, and allows for flexible post-death distribution. Both Minnesota and Florida considerations are addressed throughout, as many clients maintain connections to both jurisdictions.

---

## THE [GRANTOR LAST NAME] REVOCABLE LIVING TRUST AGREEMENT

**THIS TRUST AGREEMENT** is made and entered into this ______ day of __________________, 20____, by and between:

**[GRANTOR FULL LEGAL NAME]**, of [City], [County] County, State of Minnesota (hereinafter referred to as the "Grantor" or "Trustmaker"), and

**[GRANTOR FULL LEGAL NAME]**, as initial Trustee (hereinafter referred to as the "Trustee").

### RECITALS

**WHEREAS**, the Grantor desires to create a revocable trust for the management of the Grantor's property during the Grantor's lifetime, for the Grantor's care during any period of incapacity, and for the orderly distribution of the Grantor's property after death; and

**WHEREAS**, the Grantor desires to serve as the initial Trustee of this Trust with full power to manage, invest, and distribute the trust property during the Grantor's lifetime;

**NOW, THEREFORE**, in consideration of the mutual covenants herein and the transfer of property to the Trustee, the Grantor and Trustee agree as follows:

---

### ARTICLE I — TRUST CREATION AND FUNDING

**Section 1.1 — Creation.**

The Grantor hereby creates and establishes the **[GRANTOR LAST NAME] Revocable Living Trust** (hereinafter the "Trust"). The Trust shall be known by this name in all transactions, accounts, and documents.

**Section 1.2 — Initial Funding.**

The Grantor hereby transfers, assigns, and conveys to the Trustee the property described in **Schedule A** attached hereto and incorporated herein by reference. The Trustee hereby acknowledges receipt of such property and agrees to hold, manage, and distribute the same in accordance with the terms of this Trust Agreement.

**Section 1.3 — Additional Contributions.**

The Grantor, or any other person, may at any time transfer additional property to the Trustee to be held pursuant to this Trust Agreement. The Trustee shall not be required to accept any property that the Trustee reasonably determines would be unduly burdensome or inappropriate for the Trust to hold.

> **PRACTICE NOTE — TRUST FUNDING:** The trust is only effective for assets actually transferred to it. Trust funding is the most commonly neglected step in estate planning. Provide the client with a detailed funding instruction letter (see Section 1.4 below). Unfunded trusts provide no probate avoidance benefit — the pour-over will must then carry the load, which defeats the primary purpose of the living trust.

**Section 1.4 — Funding Instructions.**

The following categories of assets should be considered for transfer to the Trust:

| Asset Type | Transfer Method |
|-----------|----------------|
| Real property | Quit claim deed or warranty deed to Trustee; record with county recorder; update homeowner's insurance |
| Bank accounts | Change title to "[Trust Name], [Trustee Name], Trustee" or designate Trust as POD beneficiary |
| Brokerage accounts | Re-title to Trust or transfer to trust-titled account |
| Business interests | Assignment of membership/partnership interest; amend operating agreement |
| Life insurance | Change owner and/or beneficiary to Trust (consider irrevocable life insurance trust for estate tax planning) |
| Retirement accounts (IRA, 401k) | Do NOT transfer ownership — designate Trust as beneficiary only (see tax implications below) |
| Vehicles | Title transfer varies by state; Minnesota allows TOD designation on titles |
| Personal property | Assignment of personal property document |

> **CRITICAL — RETIREMENT ACCOUNTS:** Never transfer ownership of a qualified retirement account (IRA, 401k, 403b) to a revocable trust during the grantor's lifetime. This constitutes a taxable distribution of the entire account. Instead, name the trust as a beneficiary. Ensure the trust qualifies as a "see-through trust" under the SECURE Act regulations to preserve the 10-year distribution period for eligible designated beneficiaries. If the surviving spouse is the intended beneficiary, naming the spouse directly (rather than the trust) typically provides more favorable distribution options, including rollover.

> **FLORIDA HOMESTEAD CONSIDERATION:** Transferring Florida homestead property to a revocable trust does not, standing alone, destroy the homestead exemption from creditors (Fla. Stat. § 196.041), but the trust must be drafted carefully. The trust should contain provisions that restrict devise of the homestead property consistent with Article X, § 4 of the Florida Constitution. Additionally, the trust must be revocable and the grantor must be a beneficiary during their lifetime. File a new homestead exemption application after transfer.

### ARTICLE II — ADMINISTRATION DURING GRANTOR'S LIFETIME

**Section 2.1 — Grantor's Reserved Powers.**

During the Grantor's lifetime and so long as the Grantor has legal capacity:

(a) The Grantor reserves the right to amend, modify, or revoke this Trust in whole or in part by a written instrument delivered to the Trustee;

(b) The Grantor may withdraw any or all of the trust property at any time;

(c) The Grantor may direct the Trustee to make distributions of income or principal to such persons and in such amounts as the Grantor directs;

(d) The Grantor may remove and replace any Trustee, with or without cause;

(e) The Grantor may add or remove beneficiaries.

**Section 2.2 — Distributions During Grantor's Lifetime.**

During the Grantor's lifetime, the Trustee shall distribute to or for the benefit of the Grantor all of the net income of the Trust and such amounts of principal as the Grantor may request or as the Trustee determines necessary or advisable for the Grantor's health, education, maintenance, and support.

**Section 2.3 — Tax Treatment.**

During the Grantor's lifetime, this Trust shall be treated as a "grantor trust" under Sections 671-677 of the Internal Revenue Code. All items of income, deduction, and credit shall be reported on the Grantor's individual income tax return. The Trust shall use the Grantor's Social Security Number as its taxpayer identification number during the Grantor's lifetime.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** A revocable trust does not file a separate income tax return during the grantor's lifetime. After the grantor's death, the trust becomes irrevocable and must obtain its own EIN and file Form 1041.

---

### ARTICLE III — INCAPACITY PROVISIONS

**Section 3.1 — Determination of Incapacity.**

The Grantor shall be deemed to be incapacitated upon the occurrence of either of the following:

(a) **Medical Determination.** Two (2) licensed physicians, one of whom is the Grantor's primary care physician if reasonably available, provide written certification that the Grantor is unable to manage the Grantor's financial affairs by reason of mental or physical illness, injury, or deterioration; or

(b) **Court Determination.** A court of competent jurisdiction enters an order adjudicating the Grantor to be incapacitated or appointing a guardian or conservator for the Grantor.

> **DRAFTING TIP:** Some practitioners prefer a single physician certification to avoid the difficulty of coordinating two physicians. Others add a third option allowing a named family member to trigger incapacity upon physician confirmation. Consider adding a mechanism for the grantor to dispute a finding of incapacity and a process for restoration of capacity. HIPAA authorization must be included to allow the successor trustee to obtain medical information — see Section 3.4.

**Section 3.2 — Administration During Incapacity.**

Upon determination of the Grantor's incapacity:

(a) The Successor Trustee designated in Article V shall assume management of the Trust;

(b) The Grantor's powers under Section 2.1 shall be suspended for the duration of the incapacity;

(c) The Successor Trustee shall apply the income and principal of the Trust for the Grantor's benefit, considering the Grantor's accustomed standard of living, medical and care needs, and the needs of the Grantor's dependents;

(d) The Successor Trustee may pay the Grantor's debts, taxes, insurance premiums, and other obligations from trust assets;

(e) The Successor Trustee shall, to the extent feasible, maintain the Grantor in the Grantor's home and avoid institutional placement unless medically necessary.

**Section 3.3 — Restoration of Capacity.**

If the Grantor regains capacity, as certified in writing by one (1) licensed physician, the Grantor's powers under Section 2.1 shall be immediately restored, and the Successor Trustee shall transfer management of the Trust back to the Grantor.

**Section 3.4 — HIPAA Authorization.**

The Grantor hereby authorizes any physician, health care provider, hospital, clinic, laboratory, pharmacy, or other covered entity under the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (HIPAA) to release and disclose the Grantor's protected health information to the Successor Trustee and the individuals named as health care agents in the Grantor's Health Care Directive, for the purpose of determining the Grantor's capacity under this Article. This authorization shall survive the Grantor's incapacity.

---

### ARTICLE IV — ADMINISTRATION AFTER GRANTOR'S DEATH

**Section 4.1 — Trust Becomes Irrevocable.**

Upon the Grantor's death, this Trust shall become irrevocable and shall not be subject to amendment or revocation.

**Section 4.2 — Initial Administration.**

Upon the Grantor's death, the Trustee shall:

(a) Obtain a federal Employer Identification Number (EIN) for the Trust;

(b) Notify all beneficiaries of the Trust's existence and their right to a copy of the trust terms as required under Minn. Stat. § 501C.0813;

(c) Marshal and inventory all trust assets;

(d) Obtain date-of-death valuations for all trust assets;

(e) Pay or provide for the Grantor's debts, funeral expenses, and costs of administration, to the extent not payable from the probate estate;

(f) Cooperate with the Personal Representative of the Grantor's estate regarding tax elections and filings;

(g) File any required state and federal tax returns, including the federal estate tax return (Form 706) if applicable.

> **PRACTICE NOTE — MINNESOTA NOTICE REQUIREMENTS:** Under Minn. Stat. § 501C.0813, the trustee of a revocable trust must, within 60 days of the grantor's death, send notice to the qualified beneficiaries that the trust has become irrevocable, the trustee's name and address, and the beneficiary's right to request a copy of the trust instrument. Failure to provide this notice can result in personal liability for the trustee.

**Section 4.3 — Specific Distributions.**

Upon the Grantor's death, the Trustee shall distribute the following:

(a) All tangible personal property as directed by any written statement or memorandum signed by the Grantor and in existence at the Grantor's death, or as otherwise provided in the Grantor's Last Will and Testament;

(b) The sum of **$[AMOUNT]** to **[BENEFICIARY NAME]**, if [he/she/they] survive[s] the Grantor by sixty (60) days;

(c) [Additional specific distributions as applicable.]

**Section 4.4 — Residuary Trust Estate — Distribution to Spouse.**

After payment of all debts, expenses, taxes, and specific distributions:

(a) If the Grantor's spouse, **[SPOUSE FULL LEGAL NAME]**, survives the Grantor by sixty (60) days, the Trustee shall allocate the remaining trust estate as follows:

**(i) Marital Trust (Trust A).** [If applicable for estate tax planning, allocate the applicable exclusion amount to Trust B and the balance to the Marital Trust qualifying for the unlimited marital deduction.]

**(ii) Family Trust / Bypass Trust (Trust B).** [If applicable, hold in trust for the benefit of the surviving spouse and descendants, with distributions limited to an ascertainable standard.]

> **PRACTICE NOTE — ESTATE TAX PLANNING:** For estates below the federal estate tax exemption ($13.99 million for 2025, indexed for inflation), A/B trust planning may be unnecessary and may sacrifice the step-up in basis at the surviving spouse's death. Consider whether portability of the deceased spouse's unused exemption (DSUE) under IRC § 2010(c)(4) is a better approach. If using portability, a timely Form 706 must be filed to elect portability, even if no estate tax is owed. For most middle-market estates, a simple outright distribution to the surviving spouse with a disclaimer trust may provide the most flexibility.

(b) **Alternative: Outright Distribution to Spouse.** If the Grantor's spouse survives the Grantor by sixty (60) days, the Trustee shall distribute the entire remaining trust estate to the Grantor's spouse, outright and free of trust.

> **DRAFTING TIP:** Choose subsection (a) OR (b) based on the client's estate size, estate tax exposure, and planning objectives. For most estates under the federal exemption amount, (b) is simpler and preserves the full step-up in basis.

**Section 4.5 — Distribution if Spouse Does Not Survive.**

If the Grantor's spouse does not survive the Grantor by sixty (60) days, or if the Grantor's spouse has predeceased the Grantor, the Trustee shall distribute the remaining trust estate as follows:

(a) In equal shares to the Grantor's then-living children, subject to the provisions of Section 4.6 for any child who has not yet attained the age of [thirty-five (35)];

(b) If any child of the Grantor has predeceased the Grantor leaving descendants who survive the Grantor, such deceased child's share shall be distributed per stirpes to such deceased child's then-living descendants, subject to the age-based provisions of Section 4.6.

**Section 4.6 — Trusts for Younger Beneficiaries.**

If any beneficiary is under the age of [thirty-five (35)] at the time they are entitled to receive a distribution, such beneficiary's share shall be held in a separate trust under the following terms:

(a) **HEMS Standard.** The Trustee may distribute to or for the benefit of the beneficiary so much of the net income and principal as the Trustee determines necessary or advisable for the beneficiary's health, education, maintenance, and support.

(b) **Staged Distribution Schedule:**

| Age Attained | Distribution |
|-------------|-------------|
| 25 | One-third (1/3) of the then-remaining trust principal |
| 30 | One-half (1/2) of the then-remaining trust principal |
| 35 | Entire remaining trust principal |

(c) **Death of Beneficiary Before Full Distribution.** If the beneficiary dies before receiving the entire trust estate, the remaining trust estate shall be distributed to the beneficiary's then-living descendants, per stirpes, or if the beneficiary has no living descendants, to the Grantor's then-living descendants, per stirpes.

(d) **De Minimis Termination.** If the total value of a trust created under this Section falls below **$[50,000]**, the Trustee may, in the Trustee's discretion, terminate the trust and distribute the remaining assets to the beneficiary outright or to the beneficiary's guardian or custodian under the Minnesota Uniform Transfers to Minors Act (Minn. Stat. §§ 527.21–527.44).

### ARTICLE V — TRUSTEE PROVISIONS

**Section 5.1 — Initial Trustee.**

The Grantor shall serve as the initial Trustee of the Trust.

**Section 5.2 — Co-Trustee During Grantor's Lifetime.**

The Grantor's spouse, **[SPOUSE FULL LEGAL NAME]**, shall serve as Co-Trustee with the Grantor. Either Co-Trustee may act independently on behalf of the Trust during the Grantor's lifetime and capacity.

**Section 5.3 — Successor Trustees.**

Upon the death, incapacity, resignation, or removal of both Co-Trustees, the following individuals shall serve as Successor Trustee in the order listed:

(a) **First Successor Trustee:** [SUCCESSOR TRUSTEE 1 NAME], of [City, State];

(b) **Second Successor Trustee:** [SUCCESSOR TRUSTEE 2 NAME], of [City, State];

(c) **Corporate Trustee:** If no individual named above is willing and able to serve, [CORPORATE TRUSTEE NAME], or its successor, shall serve as Trustee.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Always include a corporate trustee as the final successor. Individual trustees may predecease the grantor, become incapacitated, or decline to serve. A corporate trustee ensures continuity. For smaller trusts, consider that corporate trustee minimum fees (typically $3,000-$10,000/year) may be disproportionate to the trust value.

**Section 5.4 — Trustee Powers.**

In addition to all powers conferred by the Minnesota Trust Code (Minn. Stat. Chapter 501C) and the Minnesota Trustees' Powers Act, the Trustee shall have the following powers, exercisable without court order:

(a) To invest and reinvest trust assets in any kind of property, real or personal, including stocks, bonds, mutual funds, real estate, limited partnerships, and other investments, without regard to any law restricting investments by fiduciaries, subject to the prudent investor rule of Minn. Stat. § 501C.0901;

(b) To retain any property received by the Trust, including property that does not produce income and property that would not otherwise satisfy the prudent investor standard, for such period as the Trustee deems advisable;

(c) To sell, exchange, lease, mortgage, pledge, option, or otherwise dispose of trust property, at public or private sale, for cash or credit, upon such terms as the Trustee deems advisable;

(d) To borrow money for any trust purpose, and to encumber or pledge trust assets as security;

(e) To participate in any merger, reorganization, recapitalization, or other corporate transaction affecting trust assets;

(f) To employ investment advisors, attorneys, accountants, tax preparers, and other agents, and to delegate investment and management functions to the extent permitted under Minn. Stat. § 501C.0807;

(g) To compromise, settle, or abandon claims for or against the Trust;

(h) To distribute trust property in cash or in kind, or partly in each;

(i) To establish reserves for taxes, assessments, or other charges;

(j) To make tax elections and allocations between income and principal as the Trustee deems appropriate;

(k) To exercise all rights with respect to life insurance policies held by the Trust, including borrowing against, surrendering, or converting such policies;

(l) To hold trust property in the Trustee's name, in a nominee name, or in bearer form;

(m) To continue or participate in the operation of any business interest held by the Trust.

**Section 5.5 — Trustee Compensation.**

(a) Any Trustee who is a beneficiary of the Trust may serve without compensation, unless the Trustee elects to receive reasonable compensation.

(b) Any Trustee who is not a beneficiary shall be entitled to reasonable compensation commensurate with the services rendered.

(c) Any corporate Trustee shall be entitled to compensation in accordance with its published fee schedule in effect at the time services are rendered.

**Section 5.6 — Bond Waiver.**

No Trustee shall be required to furnish bond or other security in any jurisdiction.

**Section 5.7 — Trustee Resignation.**

Any Trustee may resign upon thirty (30) days' written notice to the Grantor (if living and competent) and to all current adult beneficiaries. A resigning Trustee shall continue to serve until a successor Trustee accepts the trusteeship.

**Section 5.8 — Trustee Liability.**

No Trustee shall be personally liable for any loss or depreciation in value of trust property, except for losses resulting from the Trustee's willful misconduct, bad faith, or gross negligence.

### ARTICLE VI — TRUST PROTECTOR

**Section 6.1 — Appointment.**

The Grantor designates **[TRUST PROTECTOR NAME]**, of [City, State], as Trust Protector. If [he/she/they] is unable or unwilling to serve, **[SUCCESSOR TRUST PROTECTOR NAME]** shall serve as Trust Protector.

**Section 6.2 — Powers of Trust Protector.**

The Trust Protector shall have the following powers, exercisable in a non-fiduciary capacity:

(a) To amend the administrative or dispositive provisions of this Trust to reflect changes in tax law, trust law, or the circumstances of the beneficiaries, provided that no amendment shall benefit the Trust Protector personally;

(b) To remove and replace a Trustee (other than the Grantor) with or without cause;

(c) To resolve any dispute between a Trustee and a beneficiary without resort to court proceedings;

(d) To change the situs (governing law jurisdiction) of the Trust;

(e) To convert the Trust or any separate trust created hereunder from a grantor trust to a non-grantor trust, or vice versa, for tax purposes;

(f) To add or modify spendthrift provisions;

(g) To grant a power of appointment to a beneficiary.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** The Trust Protector is an increasingly common role in modern estate planning. The Trust Protector provides a mechanism for adapting the trust to changed circumstances without judicial modification. Minnesota does not have a specific trust protector statute, but the Minnesota Trust Code (Minn. Stat. § 501C.0808) permits the trust instrument to grant powers to persons other than the trustee. Draft the trust protector's powers carefully — overly broad powers may jeopardize the trust's estate tax treatment (e.g., if the trust protector could redirect distributions for their own benefit). The trust protector should generally be independent of the trustee and the beneficiaries.

**Section 6.3 — Compensation and Liability.**

The Trust Protector shall serve without compensation unless the Trust Protector and the Trustee agree upon reasonable compensation. The Trust Protector shall not be liable for any action taken in good faith.

### ARTICLE VII — SPENDTHRIFT PROVISIONS

**Section 7.1 — Spendthrift Clause.**

Except as otherwise provided herein, no beneficiary shall have the right to anticipate, pledge, assign, sell, transfer, alienate, or encumber [his/her/their] interest in the Trust, and no interest of any beneficiary shall be subject to [his/her/their] debts, contracts, or liabilities, or to attachment, garnishment, execution, or other legal or equitable process, including claims for alimony, maintenance, or child support, except to the extent required by applicable law.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Minnesota enforces spendthrift provisions under Minn. Stat. § 501C.0502, with exceptions for child support obligations and claims by the state. Florida similarly enforces spendthrift provisions under Fla. Stat. § 736.0502 but recognizes exceptions for child support, alimony, and necessities. Note that under both states' laws, spendthrift protection does not protect the grantor's interest in a self-settled trust (the grantor's creditors can reach trust assets to the extent of the grantor's retained interest).

### ARTICLE VIII — GENERAL PROVISIONS

**Section 8.1 — Governing Law.**

This Trust shall be governed by and construed in accordance with the laws of the State of Minnesota, specifically the Minnesota Trust Code (Minn. Stat. Chapter 501C), regardless of any change in the domicile of the Grantor, any Trustee, or any beneficiary. The Trust Protector may change the governing law of the Trust under Section 6.2(d).

**Section 8.2 — Severability.**

If any provision of this Trust Agreement is held to be invalid, illegal, or unenforceable, the remaining provisions shall remain in full force and effect.

**Section 8.3 — Headings.**

Article and section headings are for convenience only and shall not affect the interpretation of this Trust Agreement.

**Section 8.4 — Counterparts.**

This Trust Agreement may be executed in counterparts, each of which shall be deemed an original.

**Section 8.5 — Entire Agreement.**

This Trust Agreement, together with any Schedules attached hereto and any amendments executed in accordance with its terms, constitutes the entire agreement between the parties with respect to the trust created herein.

**Section 8.6 — Rule Against Perpetuities.**

Unless sooner terminated under other provisions of this Trust Agreement, each trust created hereunder shall terminate no later than twenty-one (21) years after the death of the last survivor of the Grantor and the Grantor's descendants who are living on the date of this Trust Agreement. Upon such termination, the Trustee shall distribute the remaining trust estate to the then-income beneficiaries in proportion to their respective income interests.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Minnesota's common law rule against perpetuities has been replaced by a statutory rule under the Uniform Statutory Rule Against Perpetuities (Minn. Stat. § 501C.0409), which provides a 360-year perpetuity period for trusts created after January 1, 2024, or the common law period (lives in being plus 21 years). Some practitioners prefer to use the common law formulation as a conservative approach. Florida has a 360-year perpetuity period under Fla. Stat. § 689.225.

**Section 8.7 — No-Contest Clause.**

If any beneficiary of this Trust, directly or indirectly, contests the validity of this Trust Agreement or any of its provisions, or seeks to impair or invalidate any of its provisions, such beneficiary's interest in this Trust shall be revoked and such beneficiary shall be treated as having predeceased the Grantor without surviving descendants. This provision shall not apply if the beneficiary had probable cause for the contest.

**Section 8.8 — Merger.**

The Trustee may merge any trust created under this Trust Agreement with any other trust, wherever created, having substantially identical terms and beneficiaries.

## APPENDIX: FLORIDA-SPECIFIC CONSIDERATIONS FOR DUAL-STATE CLIENTS

### Homestead

- Florida homestead property (Art. X, § 4, Fla. Const.) receives protection from forced sale by creditors and exemption from certain taxes.
- If held in a revocable trust, the property retains its homestead character if the trust is for the benefit of the owner or the owner's dependents (Fla. Stat. § 196.041).
- Constitutional restrictions on devise apply: if survived by spouse or minor child, the homestead can only pass to the spouse (life estate with remainder to descendants, or outright to spouse). These restrictions apply even when the property is held in trust.
- Draft specific provisions in the trust that comply with Florida's homestead devise restrictions.

### Elective Share

- Florida's elective share (Fla. Stat. § 732.2065) applies to the "elective estate," which includes revocable trust assets. Minnesota's elective share (Minn. Stat. § 524.2-202) similarly reaches revocable trust assets.
- For clients with property in both states, analyze which state's elective share law applies and ensure the surviving spouse's rights are addressed.

### Creditor Claims

- In Florida, a revocable trust becomes subject to claims of the grantor's creditors after death (Fla. Stat. § 736.05053). The trustee must publish a notice to creditors, similar to probate.
- In Minnesota, creditor claims against trust assets are governed by Minn. Stat. § 501C.0505.

### Income Tax

- Florida has no state income tax, which can be advantageous for trust administration. If the trust situs is changed to Florida, trust income may escape state income tax (subject to the home state's rules on taxing trusts with in-state beneficiaries or in-state trustees).
