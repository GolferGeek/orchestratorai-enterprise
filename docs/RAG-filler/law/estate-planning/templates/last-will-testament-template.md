# Last Will and Testament — Annotated Template

**Document Type:** Template with Practice Notes  
**Jurisdiction:** Minnesota (primary), Florida (secondary)  
**Last Updated:** April 2026  
**Applicable Law:** Minn. Stat. Chapter 524 (Uniform Probate Code); Fla. Stat. Chapter 732  

---

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** This template is designed for a married testator with minor children. It includes pour-over provisions to a revocable living trust, specific bequests, a residuary clause, executor appointment, guardian designations, and a self-proving affidavit compliant with Minnesota execution requirements under Minn. Stat. § 524.2-502. Adapt as needed for unmarried testators, testators without children, or non-pour-over wills. All bracketed text requires customization.

---

## LAST WILL AND TESTAMENT OF [TESTATOR FULL LEGAL NAME]

I, **[TESTATOR FULL LEGAL NAME]**, of [City], [County] County, State of Minnesota, being of legal age and of sound and disposing mind and memory, and not acting under duress, menace, fraud, or the undue influence of any person, do hereby make, publish, and declare this to be my Last Will and Testament, hereby revoking all prior wills and codicils made by me at any time.

---

### ARTICLE I — DECLARATIONS

**Section 1.1 — Family Declarations.**

I declare that I am [married to / the domestic partner of] **[SPOUSE FULL LEGAL NAME]** (hereinafter referred to as "my [Spouse/Partner]"). All references in this Will to "my [Spouse/Partner]" are to [him/her/them].

I declare that I have the following [living] children:

| Name | Date of Birth |
|------|---------------|
| [CHILD 1 FULL LEGAL NAME] | [DOB] |
| [CHILD 2 FULL LEGAL NAME] | [DOB] |
| [CHILD 3 FULL LEGAL NAME] | [DOB] |

All references in this Will to "my children" include the above-named children and any children hereafter born to or legally adopted by me.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Under Minn. Stat. § 524.2-302, a child born after execution of this Will who is not provided for may take an intestate share unless the omission was intentional. Always include an after-born child provision or update the will promptly upon the birth or adoption of a new child.

**Section 1.2 — Definitions.**

(a) "Survive" or "surviving" means living for at least sixty (60) days after my death. Any beneficiary who does not survive me by sixty (60) days shall be deemed to have predeceased me.

> **DRAFTING TIP:** The 60-day survivorship requirement prevents assets from passing through two estates in rapid succession (e.g., common disaster). Minnesota's UPC default under Minn. Stat. § 524.2-702 requires 120-hour survival, but practitioners commonly extend this to 30-60 days. Coordinate this period with the trust survivorship clause.

(b) "Personal Representative" means the individual or corporate fiduciary serving from time to time as the personal representative of my estate.

(c) "Trust" or "Living Trust" refers to the **[TESTATOR FULL LEGAL NAME] Revocable Living Trust**, dated [DATE], as the same may be amended or restated from time to time prior to my death.

---

### ARTICLE II — PAYMENT OF DEBTS, EXPENSES, AND TAXES

**Section 2.1 — Debts and Expenses.**

I direct my Personal Representative to pay from my residuary estate all of my legally enforceable debts, my funeral and burial expenses, and the expenses of administering my estate, including but not limited to court costs, attorney fees, accounting fees, and appraisal fees.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Under Minnesota's UPC (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-805), priority of claims follows a statutory order. Secured debts follow the collateral unless the will directs otherwise. Consider whether the testator wants the estate to pay off mortgages on specifically devised real property — absent direction, the devisee takes subject to the mortgage under Minn. Stat. § 524.2-607 (exoneration of liens abolished under UPC).

**Section 2.2 — Taxes.**

I direct that all estate, inheritance, and succession taxes (including any interest and penalties thereon) assessed by reason of my death, whether attributable to assets passing under this Will, by beneficiary designation, by joint tenancy, by transfer on death designation, or by any other means, shall be paid from my residuary estate without apportionment or reimbursement from any recipient thereof.

> **DRAFTING TIP:** Tax apportionment is a critical planning decision. This clause equitably burdens the residuary estate. For clients with large non-probate transfers (life insurance, retirement accounts), consider whether the residuary estate can bear the full tax burden. Minnesota follows the Uniform Estate Tax Apportionment Act (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-916) if the will is silent. Always state the testator's intent explicitly to avoid litigation.

---

### ARTICLE III — SPECIFIC BEQUESTS

**Section 3.1 — Tangible Personal Property.**

(a) I give all of my tangible personal property, including but not limited to household furnishings, clothing, jewelry, automobiles, recreational vehicles, collections, sporting equipment, and all other articles of personal or household use, together with any applicable insurance policies thereon, to my [Spouse/Partner], if [he/she/they] survive[s] me.

(b) If my [Spouse/Partner] does not survive me, I give such tangible personal property to my children who survive me, in equal shares, to be divided among them by agreement or, if they cannot agree, by my Personal Representative in [his/her/their] sole and absolute discretion. Items unsuitable for distribution to minor children shall be held by the guardian of such child's property.

**Section 3.2 — Personal Property Memorandum.**

I may leave a written statement or list disposing of certain items of tangible personal property not otherwise specifically disposed of by this Will. Any such statement or list in existence at the time of my death shall be given effect to the extent permitted under Minn. Stat. § 524.2-513 (Separate Writing Identifying Devise of Certain Types of Tangible Personal Property).

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Under Minn. Stat. § 524.2-513, a writing referenced in the will may direct disposition of tangible personal property not otherwise specifically devised. The writing must be signed by the testator and must describe the items and devisees with reasonable certainty. It need not be in existence at the time of execution and may be altered after execution. This is an extremely useful tool — advise clients to maintain and update such a list. Florida has a similar provision under Fla. Stat. § 732.515.

**Section 3.3 — Specific Cash Bequests.**

I make the following specific cash bequests:

(a) I give the sum of **$[AMOUNT]** to **[BENEFICIARY NAME]**, of [City, State], if [he/she/they] survive[s] me.

(b) I give the sum of **$[AMOUNT]** to **[CHARITABLE ORGANIZATION]**, a [state] [nonprofit corporation / charitable organization], Tax ID [EIN], to be used for its general charitable purposes.

> **DRAFTING TIP:** For charitable bequests, always include the organization's full legal name, state of incorporation, and EIN to prevent ambiguity. Consider whether the bequest should be a specific dollar amount or a percentage of the estate. Percentage bequests automatically adjust for estate size changes.

**Section 3.4 — Specific Real Property Bequests.**

[Include if real property is not held in trust or if specific devises of real property are desired.]

I give my interest in the real property located at **[ADDRESS]**, legally described as **[LEGAL DESCRIPTION]**, [County] County, Minnesota, to **[BENEFICIARY NAME]**, if [he/she/they] survive[s] me.

> **PRACTICE NOTE — FLORIDA HOMESTEAD:** If the testator owns Florida homestead property, exercise extreme caution. Under Art. X, § 4 of the Florida Constitution, homestead property cannot be devised if the owner is survived by a spouse or minor child, except that it may be devised to the spouse. If the testator is a Minnesota resident who owns Florida real property, consult Florida counsel regarding homestead restrictions, which apply to the property regardless of the owner's domicile. Fla. Stat. § 732.4015 provides that the homestead restriction on devise applies to property in Florida regardless of the decedent's residence.

### ARTICLE IV — RESIDUARY ESTATE AND POUR-OVER PROVISION

**Section 4.1 — Pour-Over to Trust.**

I give all of the rest, residue, and remainder of my estate, of whatever kind and nature and wherever situated, whether real, personal, or mixed, whether acquired before or after the execution of this Will (my "residuary estate"), to the then-acting Trustee of the **[TESTATOR FULL LEGAL NAME] Revocable Living Trust**, dated [DATE], as the same may be amended or restated from time to time prior to my death, to be added to, held, administered, and distributed as a part of said Trust in accordance with its terms and provisions, including any amendments made before my death.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** This pour-over provision is authorized under Minn. Stat. § 524.2-511 (Testamentary Additions to Trusts). The trust must be identified in the will and must be in existence at or before the testator's death. The trust may be amended after the will is executed. The pour-over will serves as a "safety net" to capture any assets not transferred to the trust during the testator's lifetime. If the trust is revoked or invalid at death, the pour-over fails and the residuary passes by intestacy — always include the alternative distribution in Section 4.2.

**Section 4.2 — Alternative Residuary Distribution.**

If the Trust referred to in Section 4.1 is not in existence at my death, has been revoked prior to my death, or is held invalid by a court of competent jurisdiction, then I give my residuary estate as follows:

(a) To my [Spouse/Partner], if [he/she/they] survive[s] me; or

(b) If my [Spouse/Partner] does not survive me, to my children who survive me, in equal shares, per stirpes.

> **DRAFTING TIP:** "Per stirpes" means that if a child predeceases the testator, that child's share passes to their descendants. Under Minn. Stat. § 524.2-709, Minnesota follows the "modern per stirpes" (per capita at each generation) approach unless the will specifies otherwise. If you intend strict per stirpes (representation starting at the first generation), state: "to my then-living descendants, by right of representation, with the division to be made at the first generational level below me at which there are living descendants."

### ARTICLE V — APPOINTMENT OF PERSONAL REPRESENTATIVE

**Section 5.1 — Primary Personal Representative.**

I nominate and appoint **[PRIMARY PR NAME]**, of [City, State], as Personal Representative of my estate.

**Section 5.2 — Successor Personal Representative.**

If **[PRIMARY PR NAME]** is unable or unwilling to serve, or ceases to serve for any reason, I nominate and appoint **[SUCCESSOR PR NAME]**, of [City, State], as successor Personal Representative.

**Section 5.3 — Bond Waiver.**

I direct that no bond or other security shall be required of any Personal Representative serving under this Will, whether acting in a supervised or unsupervised administration.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Under Minn. Stat. § 524.3-603, a personal representative must furnish bond unless the will waives it or all interested persons waive it. Always include a bond waiver clause to save the estate the cost of bond premiums. If there are concerns about the PR's fiduciary conduct, interested persons may petition the court to require bond notwithstanding the waiver.

**Section 5.4 — Powers of Personal Representative.**

In addition to all powers conferred by law, including those set forth in Minn. Stat. § 524.3-715 and the Minnesota Trustees' Powers Act (Minn. Stat. § 501C.0815), I grant my Personal Representative the following powers, exercisable without court order:

(a) To retain any assets of my estate for such time as my Personal Representative deems advisable, without regard to diversification requirements;

(b) To sell, exchange, lease, or otherwise dispose of any real or personal property at public or private sale, upon such terms and conditions as my Personal Representative deems advisable;

(c) To borrow money, pledge or mortgage estate assets, and to execute promissory notes, mortgages, and security agreements;

(d) To employ attorneys, accountants, investment advisors, and other professional agents and to compensate them from the estate;

(e) To make distributions in cash or in kind, or partly in each, and to allocate assets among beneficiaries at fair market values as of the date of distribution;

(f) To continue any business in which I have an interest at the time of my death, in such form and for such period as my Personal Representative deems advisable;

(g) To make elections for federal and state tax purposes, including the election to file joint returns with my surviving [Spouse/Partner];

(h) To settle, compromise, or abandon any claim by or against my estate.

**Section 5.5 — Compensation.**

My Personal Representative shall be entitled to reasonable compensation for services rendered, as provided under Minn. Stat. § 524.3-719.

---

### ARTICLE VI — GUARDIAN DESIGNATIONS

**Section 6.1 — Guardian of Minor Children.**

If my [Spouse/Partner] does not survive me or is unable to serve as guardian, I nominate and appoint **[GUARDIAN NAME]**, of [City, State], as guardian of the person of each of my minor children.

**Section 6.2 — Successor Guardian.**

If **[GUARDIAN NAME]** is unable or unwilling to serve, I nominate and appoint **[SUCCESSOR GUARDIAN NAME]**, of [City, State], as successor guardian.

**Section 6.3 — Guardian of Minor Children's Property.**

I nominate and appoint **[PROPERTY GUARDIAN NAME]**, of [City, State], as guardian of the property and estate of each of my minor children, to serve without bond.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Under Minn. Stat. § 524.5-202, the court must appoint a guardian in the best interest of the child but gives significant weight to the testator's nomination. Consider naming different persons as guardian of the person and guardian of the property to create accountability. Where a pour-over trust is used, most property should flow to the trust and be managed by the trustee rather than the property guardian — coordinate these designations.

**Section 6.4 — Guardianship Preferences.**

Without limiting the discretion of the court, I express the following preferences for the care of my minor children:

(a) I prefer that my children remain together in one household;

(b) I prefer that my children continue to attend their current schools if reasonably possible;

(c) I [do / do not] wish my children to be raised in the [faith/tradition] in which they are currently being raised.

> **DRAFTING TIP:** While these expressions of preference are not legally binding, Minnesota courts consider them as evidence of the testator's intent. They can be particularly useful in contested guardianship proceedings. Discuss with the client whether to include religious upbringing preferences, educational preferences, and geographic preferences.

### ARTICLE VII — TRUSTS FOR MINOR BENEFICIARIES

**Section 7.1 — Minor's Trust.**

If any beneficiary under this Will is under the age of [twenty-five (25)] years at the time of distribution, my Personal Representative shall not distribute such beneficiary's share outright but shall instead hold such share in a separate trust for such beneficiary under the following terms:

(a) **Distributions for Health, Education, Maintenance, and Support.** The Trustee may distribute to or for the benefit of the beneficiary so much of the net income and principal of the trust as the Trustee determines necessary or advisable for the beneficiary's health, education, maintenance, and support, considering all other resources reasonably available to the beneficiary.

(b) **Staged Distribution.**

- One-third (1/3) of the then-remaining trust estate upon the beneficiary attaining age [twenty-five (25)];
- One-half (1/2) of the then-remaining trust estate upon the beneficiary attaining age [thirty (30)];
- The entire remaining trust estate upon the beneficiary attaining age [thirty-five (35)].

(c) **Early Termination.** If the trust estate is valued at less than **$[50,000]** at any time, the Trustee may, in the Trustee's discretion, terminate the trust and distribute the remaining assets to the beneficiary outright.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Staged distributions are a common planning technique to avoid giving a young beneficiary access to a large inheritance before they have sufficient maturity to manage it. The ages and fractions are customizable. For clients with pour-over trusts, these provisions are typically in the trust rather than the will — include them here only if the will serves as the primary dispositive instrument or as a backup if the trust fails.

### ARTICLE VIII — GENERAL PROVISIONS

**Section 8.1 — Simultaneous Death.**

If my [Spouse/Partner] and I die under circumstances where the order of our deaths cannot be established by sufficient evidence, my [Spouse/Partner] shall be deemed to have predeceased me for all purposes of this Will. This provision applies notwithstanding the provisions of the Uniform Simultaneous Death Act (Minn. Stat. § 524.2-702).

**Section 8.2 — No-Contest Clause.**

If any beneficiary under this Will, directly or indirectly, contests or attacks this Will or any of its provisions, or conspires with or assists anyone in any such contest, any share or interest given to the contesting beneficiary under this Will is revoked and shall be disposed of as if the contesting beneficiary had predeceased me without surviving descendants.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Minnesota courts enforce no-contest (in terrorem) clauses, but they are strictly construed. Under Minn. Stat. § 524.2-517, a no-contest clause is unenforceable if the person who filed the contest had probable cause for doing so. Advise clients that a no-contest clause is not a guarantee against challenges and may have limited effectiveness. Florida law is similar — Fla. Stat. § 732.517 enforces no-contest clauses except when probable cause exists.

**Section 8.3 — Spendthrift Provision.**

No interest in income or principal of any trust created under this Will shall be voluntarily or involuntarily transferred, assigned, or pledged by any beneficiary, or be subject to any beneficiary's liabilities or creditor claims, or to attachment, garnishment, or other legal process, before actual receipt by the beneficiary. This provision shall not restrict the Trustee's exercise of any discretionary power to distribute income or principal.

**Section 8.4 — Severability.**

If any provision of this Will is held to be invalid, illegal, or unenforceable, such invalidity, illegality, or unenforceability shall not affect any other provision of this Will, and this Will shall be construed as if such invalid, illegal, or unenforceable provision had never been contained herein.

**Section 8.5 — Governing Law.**

This Will shall be governed by and construed in accordance with the laws of the State of Minnesota.

> **PRACTICE NOTE — FLORIDA SNOWBIRDS:** For clients who maintain residences in both Minnesota and Florida, domicile determination is critical. Only one state can be the testator's domicile for probate purposes. Clients should file a Declaration of Domicile in their intended home state. Florida's Declaration of Domicile is filed with the county clerk under Fla. Stat. § 222.17. Inconsistent domicile indicators (voter registration, driver's license, homestead exemption) can lead to both states asserting jurisdiction over the estate.

**Section 8.6 — Fiduciary Discretion.**

Whenever a fiduciary is granted discretion under this Will, such discretion shall be exercised in good faith and in the best interests of the beneficiaries, and shall not be subject to judicial review except for abuse of discretion or bad faith.

> **EXECUTION REQUIREMENTS — MINNESOTA (Minn. Stat. § 524.2-502):**
> 1. The will must be in writing.
> 2. The will must be signed by the testator (or by another individual in the testator's conscious presence and at the testator's direction).
> 3. The will must be signed by at least two individuals, each of whom signed within a reasonable time after witnessing either the testator's signing or the testator's acknowledgment of the signature or the will.
> 4. Minnesota does NOT require notarization for a valid will, but a self-proving affidavit (below) eliminates the need for witness testimony at probate.
> 5. Minnesota recognizes holographic (handwritten) wills under Minn. Stat. § 524.2-502(b) if the signature and material portions are in the testator's handwriting.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** The self-proving affidavit is authorized by Minn. Stat. § 524.2-504. When attached to or included in the will, it creates a presumption that the execution requirements were satisfied, eliminating the need for witness testimony during probate. This is strongly recommended for all wills. The affidavit must be executed before an officer authorized to administer oaths (typically a notary public).

## APPENDIX: COMMON PITFALLS AND DRAFTING CHECKLIST

### Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Uncoordinated beneficiary designations.** The will does not control assets with beneficiary designations (life insurance, retirement accounts, POD/TOD accounts). These pass outside probate. Review all designations alongside the will.

2. **Stale guardian nominations.** Guardian designations should be reviewed every 2-3 years. Circumstances change — the named guardian may have moved, divorced, developed health issues, or become estranged from the family.

3. **Missing pour-over trust.** A pour-over will without the corresponding trust is useless for the pour-over provision. Ensure the trust is executed the same day as the will, or before.

4. **Florida homestead trap.** Minnesota clients who own Florida real property cannot freely devise that property if survived by a spouse or minor child. The Florida homestead exemption from creditors comes with constitutional restrictions on devise. Consider holding Florida property in a trust (though trusts do not eliminate homestead devise restrictions — only life estate to surviving spouse with remainder to descendants, or outright to spouse, satisfies the constitutional requirement).

5. **Digital assets.** Consider whether the will or trust should address digital assets (email accounts, social media, cryptocurrency, digital photo libraries). Minnesota has adopted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (Minn. Stat. §§ 521A.01–521A.17), which governs fiduciary access to digital assets.

6. **Tax clause interaction with non-probate assets.** A tax apportionment clause in the will that burdens the residuary estate with all taxes can inadvertently deplete the probate estate when large non-probate transfers (e.g., life insurance) trigger estate tax liability.

7. **Failure to update after life events.** Under Minnesota's UPC, divorce automatically revokes dispositions to the former spouse (Minn. Stat. § 524.2-804), but marriage does not revoke the will. A new child may have rights under the pretermitted heir statute (Minn. Stat. § 524.2-302).

### Drafting Checklist

- [ ] Verify testator's full legal name and domicile
- [ ] Confirm marital status and identify all children (including adopted, stepchildren if intended)
- [ ] Review and coordinate all beneficiary designations
- [ ] Review and coordinate with existing or contemporaneous trust
- [ ] Confirm executor/PR willingness and capacity to serve
- [ ] Confirm guardian willingness (for minor children)
- [ ] Review real property in all states (especially Florida homestead)
- [ ] Address digital asset access
- [ ] Include personal property memorandum reference
- [ ] Select tax apportionment approach and coordinate with trust
- [ ] Execute with two witnesses and self-proving affidavit
- [ ] Provide client with safekeeping instructions (original in fireproof location, copies to PR and attorney)
- [ ] Calendar annual review reminder
