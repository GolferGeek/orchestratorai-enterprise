# Advance Healthcare Directive / Living Will — Annotated Template

**Document Type:** Template with Practice Notes  
**Jurisdiction:** Minnesota (primary), Florida (secondary)  
**Last Updated:** April 2026  
**Applicable Law:** Minn. Stat. §§ 145C.01–145C.16 (Minnesota Health Care Directive Act); Fla. Stat. Chapter 765 (Florida Health Care Advance Directives)  

---

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** This stand-alone living will / advance directive template is designed for clients who want to document treatment preferences without appointing a health care agent, or as a supplement to a healthcare power of attorney. Under Minnesota law, a "health care directive" can include agent appointment, treatment instructions, or both (Minn. Stat. § 145C.01, subd. 5a). This template focuses exclusively on the treatment instruction component. For clients who also need agent appointment, use the combined Healthcare Power of Attorney template. For clients who have already appointed an agent but want a more detailed treatment instruction document, this template provides comprehensive end-of-life and treatment preference documentation.

---

## ADVANCE DIRECTIVE FOR HEALTH CARE  
## (LIVING WILL)

### PART 1 — IDENTIFICATION AND DECLARATIONS

**Section 1.1 — Principal.**

I, **[PRINCIPAL FULL LEGAL NAME]**, of [City], [County] County, State of Minnesota (Date of Birth: [DOB]), being an adult of sound mind, make this Advance Directive to express my wishes regarding medical treatment in the event I become unable to make or communicate my own health care decisions.

**Section 1.2 — Declaration of Intent.**

I understand that modern medical technology has made it possible to extend the dying process and to maintain biological functions long after the natural process of dying has begun. I believe that each person has a fundamental right to determine the extent of medical treatment they wish to receive. I make this Directive to ensure that my wishes are known and followed if I am unable to speak for myself.

**Section 1.3 — Relationship to Other Documents.**

This Advance Directive is intended to [supplement / replace] my Health Care Directive dated [DATE], in which I appointed **[AGENT NAME]** as my Health Care Agent.

*(Select one)*

- [ ] **SUPPLEMENTAL:** This Advance Directive provides detailed treatment instructions to guide my Health Care Agent's decisions. In the event of any conflict between this Advance Directive and my agent's judgment, [my agent's judgment shall control / this Advance Directive shall control].

- [ ] **STAND-ALONE:** I do not have a currently effective health care agent appointment. This Advance Directive provides instructions directly to my health care providers.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** A stand-alone living will without an agent appointment can create problems. When situations arise that are not specifically addressed in the written instructions, there is no designated person with authority to make decisions. The patient's family and physicians must then navigate the decision, which can lead to delay, disagreement, and even litigation. Best practice is to always combine treatment instructions with an agent appointment. If the client refuses to appoint an agent, document that refusal and explain the risks.

### PART 2 — CLINICAL CONDITIONS AND TREATMENT PREFERENCES

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** This section is organized by clinical condition rather than by treatment type. This approach is more intuitive for clients and more useful for physicians, who must first diagnose the condition before determining the appropriate treatment. Each condition includes a medical definition to ensure the client understands what they are consenting to or refusing.

---

#### CONDITION 2: PERMANENT UNCONSCIOUSNESS

**Definition:** Permanent unconsciousness includes persistent vegetative state and irreversible coma. It is a condition in which, to a reasonable degree of medical certainty: (a) I am unaware of myself and my environment; (b) there is total loss of cerebral cortical functioning, resulting in no capacity for voluntary action, social interaction, or awareness; and (c) the condition is irreversible with no reasonable prospect of recovery.

**My instructions if I am permanently unconscious:**

**A. Life-Sustaining Treatment Generally**

- [ ] I WANT life-sustaining treatment to be provided indefinitely.
- [ ] I want life-sustaining treatment to be provided for a trial period of [30/60/90] days to determine with reasonable medical certainty whether the condition is permanent. After that period, if two physicians agree the condition is permanent, I want life-sustaining treatment withdrawn.

#### CONDITION 3: ADVANCED PROGRESSIVE ILLNESS

**Definition:** Advanced progressive illness includes conditions such as advanced dementia (Alzheimer's disease or similar), end-stage neurological diseases (ALS, advanced Parkinson's disease, advanced multiple sclerosis), end-stage organ failure (heart, lung, liver, kidney) not amenable to transplant, and other conditions in which my cognitive and physical functioning have deteriorated to the point where I am unable to recognize family members, communicate meaningfully, or perform basic activities of daily living, and there is no reasonable prospect of meaningful recovery.

**My instructions if I have an advanced progressive illness:**

**A. Life-Sustaining Treatment Generally**

- [ ] I WANT all available life-sustaining treatment, including treatment of secondary illnesses and infections.
- [ ] I want treatment for comfort and symptom management, but I do NOT want life-sustaining treatment that would prolong the disease process. Specifically, I do not want treatment for secondary conditions (such as pneumonia or urinary tract infections) that may hasten death.
- [ ] I leave this decision to my Health Care Agent, with the guidance that I value [quality of life / quantity of life].

> **DRAFTING TIP:** The advanced progressive illness category is not universally included in living will templates but is increasingly important. Many clients fear prolonged cognitive decline more than rapid death. Without explicit instructions for this condition, care providers default to treating secondary infections and conditions, potentially extending a situation the patient would not have wanted. Discuss this scenario carefully with each client.

### PART 3 — ARTIFICIAL NUTRITION AND HYDRATION

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Artificial nutrition and hydration (ANH) is treated separately because it raises distinct ethical, medical, and legal considerations. Some clients and practitioners distinguish between "treatment" and "basic care," with nutrition and hydration falling into the latter category. Under Minnesota law (Minn. Stat. § 145C.01, subd. 7a), "life-sustaining treatment" includes treatment that "utilizes mechanical or artificial means to sustain a vital bodily function." ANH delivered by tube or IV qualifies as life-sustaining treatment. A general instruction to withhold life-sustaining treatment includes ANH unless the directive states otherwise.

**Section 3.1 — Artificial Nutrition (Feeding Tube).**

A feeding tube delivers nutrition directly to the stomach or intestine. It may be placed through the nose (nasogastric tube), through the abdominal wall (PEG tube), or intravenously (total parenteral nutrition / TPN).

*(Select one for EACH condition)*

**If I have a terminal illness:**
- [ ] I WANT artificial nutrition.
- [ ] I do NOT want artificial nutrition.
- [ ] I want a time-limited trial of [______] days, then reassess.
- [ ] Agent decides.

**If I am permanently unconscious:**
- [ ] I WANT artificial nutrition.
- [ ] I do NOT want artificial nutrition.
- [ ] I want a time-limited trial of [______] days, then reassess.
- [ ] Agent decides.

**If I have an advanced progressive illness:**
- [ ] I WANT artificial nutrition.
- [ ] I do NOT want artificial nutrition, but I DO want food and fluids offered by mouth for as long as I can accept them.
- [ ] Agent decides.

**Section 3.2 — Artificial Hydration (IV Fluids).**

*(Select one for EACH condition)*

**If I have a terminal illness:**
- [ ] I WANT IV fluids.
- [ ] I do NOT want IV fluids except as needed for medication delivery and comfort.
- [ ] Agent decides.

**If I am permanently unconscious:**
- [ ] I WANT IV fluids.
- [ ] I do NOT want IV fluids.
- [ ] Agent decides.

**If I have an advanced progressive illness:**
- [ ] I WANT IV fluids.
- [ ] I do NOT want IV fluids, but I DO want fluids offered by mouth.
- [ ] Agent decides.

> **PRACTICE NOTE — FLORIDA COMPARISON:** Florida's Life-Prolonging Procedure Act (Fla. Stat. § 765.302) requires that the refusal of artificial nutrition and hydration be separately and specifically stated. A general refusal of life-prolonging procedures does NOT include refusal of ANH in Florida unless expressly stated. This is a critical distinction for snowbird clients who may receive treatment in Florida. Ensure the client's directive explicitly addresses ANH if the client wants it withheld.

### PART 4 — COMFORT CARE AND PAIN MANAGEMENT

**Section 4.1 — Comfort Care.**

Regardless of any other provision of this Directive, I ALWAYS want the following comfort measures:

(a) **Pain Management.** I want adequate medication to alleviate pain and suffering, even if such medication may hasten my death, cause sedation, or reduce my level of consciousness. I understand that palliative care focuses on comfort rather than cure.

(b) **Hygiene and Dignity.** I want personal hygiene care, including bathing, oral care, skin care, and positioning, to be maintained at all times.

(c) **Emotional Support.** I want the presence and support of family, friends, spiritual advisors, and social workers as appropriate.

(d) **Oral Nutrition and Hydration.** Even if I refuse artificial nutrition and hydration, I want food and fluids offered by mouth for comfort, to the extent I am able to accept them safely.

(e) **Environmental Comfort.** I want a clean, quiet, comfortable environment with appropriate temperature, lighting, and minimal unnecessary medical equipment.

**Section 4.2 — Palliative Sedation.**

If my pain or suffering cannot be adequately controlled through conventional means, and I am in a terminal condition or near the end of life:

- [ ] I authorize palliative sedation (continuous deep sedation) to the extent necessary to relieve my suffering, even if such sedation results in my being unconscious until death.
- [ ] I do NOT want palliative sedation. I prefer to maintain consciousness even if it means enduring some degree of pain or discomfort.
- [ ] I leave this decision to my Health Care Agent.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Palliative sedation is the use of sedative medications to reduce consciousness in a terminally ill patient whose suffering is intractable by other means. It is ethically and legally distinct from euthanasia or physician-assisted suicide, both of which are illegal in Minnesota. The intent is to relieve suffering, not to cause death, though death may be a foreseeable side effect. The ethical principle of "double effect" applies. Discuss this option candidly with clients who have strong feelings about end-of-life comfort.

### PART 5 — PHYSICIAN CERTIFICATION REQUIREMENTS

**Section 5.1 — Required Certifications.**

The treatment instructions in this Directive regarding withholding or withdrawing life-sustaining treatment shall become operative only when the following certifications are obtained:

(a) **Terminal Illness:** My attending physician and one additional physician must certify in writing that I have a terminal condition, as defined in Part 2, Condition 1.

(b) **Permanent Unconsciousness:** My attending physician and one consulting physician (preferably a neurologist) must certify in writing that I am in a state of permanent unconsciousness with no reasonable prospect of recovering consciousness or cognitive function.

(c) **Advanced Progressive Illness:** My attending physician must certify in writing that my condition is advanced, progressive, and irreversible, and that I have permanently lost the capacity to make or communicate health care decisions.

**Section 5.2 — Documentation.**

All physician certifications must be documented in my medical record. The certifying physicians must personally examine me and must base their certifications on their examination and my medical history, rather than solely on the existence of this Directive.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** The two-physician requirement is a safeguard against premature withdrawal of treatment based on an incorrect diagnosis. It is not required by Minnesota statute for all conditions, but it is standard practice and provides additional legal protection for the health care providers. For permanent unconsciousness, involving a neurologist as the consulting physician is strongly recommended.

### PART 6 — PREGNANCY PROVISION

**Section 6.1 — Effect of Pregnancy.**

*(Select one — applicable only if the Principal is of childbearing potential)*

- [ ] **OPTION A.** If I am pregnant, the treatment instructions in this Directive SHALL still apply, regardless of the stage of pregnancy. My wishes regarding my own treatment take priority.

- [ ] **OPTION B.** If I am pregnant, the treatment instructions in this Directive shall NOT be followed if, in the judgment of my attending physician, it is possible that the fetus could develop to the point of live birth with continued life-sustaining treatment. Life-sustaining treatment shall be continued until delivery, if medically possible.

- [ ] **OPTION C.** If I am pregnant, I leave this decision to my Health Care Agent, in consultation with my physicians and family.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Pregnancy provisions in living wills are a sensitive but necessary consideration for clients of childbearing potential. Several states (not Minnesota) have statutes that automatically invalidate a living will during pregnancy. Minnesota does not have such a statute, meaning the principal's directive controls. However, the interaction between a pregnant patient's directive and the rights of the fetus is a complex ethical and legal area. Discuss all options with the client and document the client's informed decision.

### PART 7 — GENERAL PROVISIONS

**Section 7.1 — Provider Obligations and Conscientious Objection.**

I understand that under Minn. Stat. § 145C.09, a health care provider who is unable or unwilling to comply with this Directive based on the provider's conscientious beliefs must inform me (or my agent) and make reasonable efforts to assist in transferring my care to a provider who is willing to comply.

**Section 7.2 — Provider Immunity.**

I understand that health care providers who comply in good faith with this Directive or with the decisions of my Health Care Agent are protected from civil and criminal liability under Minn. Stat. § 145C.11.

**Section 7.3 — Revocation.**

I may revoke this Advance Directive at any time by any of the methods permitted under Minn. Stat. § 145C.08, including executing a new directive, destroying this document with the intent to revoke it, or by written or oral statement.

**Section 7.4 — Supersession.**

This Advance Directive supersedes all prior living wills, advance directives, and health care instructions I have previously executed, except to the extent that this Directive expressly supplements a separate Health Care Agent appointment.

**Section 7.5 — Severability.**

If any provision of this Directive is found invalid or unenforceable, all other provisions remain in full force and effect.

**Section 7.6 — Copies.**

Copies of this Directive, including photocopies, faxed copies, and electronic copies, shall have the same force and effect as the original, as authorized by Minn. Stat. § 145C.07.

### PART 8 — EXECUTION

**EXECUTION REQUIREMENTS — MINNESOTA:**

Under Minn. Stat. § 145C.03, this Advance Directive must be:
1. In writing;
2. Signed by the Principal (or by another at the Principal's direction, in the Principal's presence);
3. Signed by at least one witness OR acknowledged before a notary public.

The witness may NOT be the health care agent (if one is appointed in a separate document).

> **BEST PRACTICE:** Include both a witness and notary acknowledgment for maximum portability and acceptance, particularly if the client spends time in other states. Florida requires two witnesses for a living will (Fla. Stat. § 765.302).

## APPENDIX: CLINICAL DEFINITIONS REFERENCE

For the benefit of the client and non-medical readers, the following definitions are provided:

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Life-sustaining treatment** | Any medical treatment, procedure, or intervention that uses mechanical or artificial means to sustain, restore, or supplant a vital bodily function, and without which the patient will die. Includes mechanical ventilation, CPR, dialysis, artificial nutrition and hydration, blood transfusions, and some medications (e.g., vasopressors). |
| **Comfort care / palliative care** | Medical care focused on relieving pain and symptoms rather than curing the underlying disease. Includes pain medication, positioning, hygiene, emotional support, and hospice services. |
| **Hospice care** | A coordinated program of palliative and supportive services for terminally ill patients and their families, typically provided when curative treatment is no longer pursued. May be provided at home, in a nursing facility, or in a dedicated hospice facility. |
| **Cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR)** | Emergency procedures including chest compressions, defibrillation, intubation, and medication to restore heartbeat and breathing after cardiac or respiratory arrest. |
| **Mechanical ventilation** | A machine (ventilator or respirator) that mechanically assists or replaces spontaneous breathing through a tube inserted in the windpipe (intubation) or through a tracheostomy. |
| **Dialysis** | A procedure that mechanically filters waste products from the blood when the kidneys can no longer perform this function. |
| **Artificial nutrition** | Delivery of nutrients through a tube (nasogastric, PEG, or TPN) rather than through normal eating. |
| **Artificial hydration** | Delivery of fluids intravenously or subcutaneously rather than through normal drinking. |
| **DNR (Do Not Resuscitate)** | A medical order, written by a physician, directing that CPR not be attempted if the patient's heart stops or breathing ceases. A DNR order is separate from this Advance Directive but may be based upon the wishes expressed herein. |
| **POLST (Provider Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment)** | A standardized, portable medical order form signed by a physician that documents a patient's wishes regarding life-sustaining treatment. Minnesota recognizes POLST forms. |

## APPENDIX: COMMON PITFALLS

1. **Generic language that fails to address specific conditions.** A living will that says only "I don't want to be kept alive by machines" is ambiguous and difficult to apply. Specificity by clinical condition and treatment type is far more useful.

2. **Failure to address artificial nutrition and hydration separately.** Many clients and practitioners assume that a general refusal of life-sustaining treatment includes ANH. In some states, including Florida, it does not unless specifically stated.

3. **No agent appointment.** A living will without an agent appointment leaves treatment decisions to the medical team and family, who may disagree or be paralyzed by guilt and uncertainty. Always pair with an agent appointment.

4. **Failure to share the document.** A living will locked in a safe deposit box is useless in an emergency. Distribute copies widely.

5. **Failure to update after medical changes.** A client diagnosed with cancer, dementia, or another serious illness may have very different treatment preferences than they did when healthy. Prompt review after major diagnoses is essential.

6. **Conflicting documents.** Clients who have executed multiple directives, living wills, and POLST forms over the years may have contradictory instructions in effect. Always include a supersession clause and revoke prior documents explicitly.
