# Durable Power of Attorney for Financial Matters — Annotated Template

**Document Type:** Template with Practice Notes  
**Jurisdiction:** Minnesota  
**Last Updated:** April 2026  
**Applicable Law:** Minnesota Uniform Power of Attorney Act, Minn. Stat. §§ 523.01–523.24  

---

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** This template creates a durable power of attorney for financial matters under the Minnesota Uniform Power of Attorney Act (MUPAA), effective August 1, 2014. The MUPAA significantly changed Minnesota POA law, expanding agent authority categories, adding default rules, requiring specific grant language for certain powers, and imposing duties on agents and third parties. All practitioners should be thoroughly familiar with the MUPAA before drafting financial powers of attorney.

---

## STATUTORY DURABLE POWER OF ATTORNEY FOR FINANCIAL MATTERS

### NOTICE TO PRINCIPAL

> **YOU SHOULD SELECT SOMEONE YOU TRUST TO SERVE AS YOUR AGENT. UNLESS YOU SPECIFY OTHERWISE, GENERALLY THE AGENT'S AUTHORITY WILL CONTINUE UNTIL YOU DIE OR REVOKE THE POWER OF ATTORNEY OR THE AGENT RESIGNS OR IS UNABLE TO ACT FOR YOU.**
>
> **YOUR AGENT IS ENTITLED TO REASONABLE COMPENSATION UNLESS YOU STATE OTHERWISE IN THE SPECIAL INSTRUCTIONS.**
>
> **THIS FORM PROVIDES FOR DESIGNATION OF ONE AGENT. IF YOU WISH TO NAME MORE THAN ONE AGENT, YOU MAY NAME A CO-AGENT IN THE SPECIAL INSTRUCTIONS. CO-AGENTS ARE NOT REQUIRED TO ACT TOGETHER UNLESS YOU INCLUDE THAT REQUIREMENT IN THE SPECIAL INSTRUCTIONS.**
>
> **IF YOUR AGENT IS UNABLE OR UNWILLING TO ACT FOR YOU, YOUR POWER OF ATTORNEY WILL END UNLESS YOU HAVE NAMED A SUCCESSOR AGENT. YOU MAY ALSO NAME A SECOND SUCCESSOR AGENT.**

*(This notice is required by Minn. Stat. § 523.20)*

### PART 2 — DURABILITY AND EFFECTIVE DATE

**Section 2.1 — Durability.**

This Power of Attorney is DURABLE. It shall not be terminated or affected by my subsequent disability or incapacity. This power of attorney is durable under Minn. Stat. § 523.07.

**Section 2.2 — Effective Date.**

*(Select one)*

- [ ] **OPTION A — IMMEDIATELY EFFECTIVE.** This Power of Attorney is effective immediately upon my execution hereof and shall continue until revoked.

- [ ] **OPTION B — SPRINGING POWER.** This Power of Attorney shall become effective only upon my incapacity, as determined under Section 2.3 below.

> **PRACTICE NOTE — SPRINGING VS. IMMEDIATE:** There are significant practical tradeoffs:
>
> **Immediately effective:** Easier for the agent to use; no need to prove incapacity to skeptical third parties; allows the principal to have the agent handle routine matters even when the principal is merely unavailable (traveling, busy). Risk: the agent has authority from day one, which requires high trust.
>
> **Springing:** Provides psychological comfort that the agent cannot act until needed. Risk: third parties may refuse to honor the POA until they are satisfied that the triggering condition has occurred, causing delay. Financial institutions are particularly problematic — even with clear triggering language, many will involve their legal departments. Under MUPAA, Minn. Stat. § 523.18 provides that a third party may request an agent's certification and an opinion of counsel but may not require additional evidence.
>
> **Recommendation:** For most clients, an immediately effective POA with a trusted agent is the better practical choice. If the client insists on a springing power, draft the triggering mechanism clearly and include a physician certification process.

**Section 2.3 — Determination of Incapacity (for Springing Power only).**

For purposes of this Power of Attorney, I shall be deemed incapacitated when [one/two] licensed physician(s) certify in writing that I am unable to manage my financial affairs due to mental or physical illness, injury, or deterioration. I authorize any physician to release information regarding my capacity to my Agent or Successor Agent for this purpose, and I waive any applicable HIPAA restrictions to the extent necessary for this determination.

| _______ | Commodities and options | Minn. Stat. § 523.11, subd. 2 |
| _______ | Banks and other financial institutions | Minn. Stat. § 523.12 |
| _______ | Operation of entity or business | Minn. Stat. § 523.13 |
| _______ | Insurance and annuities | Minn. Stat. § 523.14, subd. 1 |
| _______ | Estates, trusts, and other beneficial interests | Minn. Stat. § 523.14, subd. 2 |
| _______ | Claims and litigation | Minn. Stat. § 523.14, subd. 3 |
| _______ | Personal and family maintenance | Minn. Stat. § 523.14, subd. 4 |
| _______ | Benefits from governmental programs or civil or military service | Minn. Stat. § 523.14, subd. 5 |
| _______ | Retirement plans | Minn. Stat. § 523.14, subd. 6 |
| _______ | Taxes | Minn. Stat. § 523.14, subd. 7 |
| _______ | Digital assets | Minn. Stat. § 521A (RUFADAA) |
| _______ | ALL OF THE ABOVE | (Initial here to grant all categories) |

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Under MUPAA, checking "all of the above" grants the agent all of the general authorities listed. However, certain extraordinary powers require a SEPARATE, SPECIFIC grant under Section 3.2. A general grant does NOT include the authority to make gifts, change beneficiary designations, create or amend trusts, delegate authority, waive the principal's right as beneficiary, or exercise fiduciary powers.

**Section 3.2 — Grant of Specific Authority.**

> **IMPORTANT:** Under Minn. Stat. § 523.15, the following powers are NOT included in a general grant. Each must be specifically and expressly granted. I grant my Agent the following specific powers (initial each to grant):

| Initial | Specific Authority |
|---------|-------------------|
| _______ | Make gifts of the Principal's property, subject to the limitations in Section 3.3 below |
| _______ | Create, amend, revoke, or terminate an inter vivos trust |
| _______ | Create or change rights of survivorship |
| _______ | Create or change a beneficiary designation |
| _______ | Delegate authority granted under this Power of Attorney |
| _______ | Waive the Principal's right to be a beneficiary of a joint and survivor annuity, including a survivor benefit under a retirement plan |
| _______ | Exercise fiduciary powers that the Principal has authority to delegate |
| _______ | Authorize another person to exercise authority under this Power of Attorney |
| _______ | Disclaim or refuse an interest in property, including a power of appointment |
| _______ | Exercise powers of appointment |

> **CRITICAL DRAFTING NOTE:** The specific authority to "create, amend, revoke, or terminate an inter vivos trust" is essential when the principal has a revocable living trust. Without this specific grant, the agent cannot fund the trust, amend it for tax purposes, or exercise the principal's reserved powers under the trust. Similarly, the authority to "create or change a beneficiary designation" is needed if the agent must update retirement account or life insurance beneficiaries. ALWAYS discuss each of these specific powers with the client individually.

**Section 3.3 — Gifting Authority and Limitations.**

If I have granted gifting authority in Section 3.2 above, such authority is subject to the following limitations:

(a) My Agent may make gifts to the following persons and in the following amounts:

- Gifts to any individual in an amount not to exceed the federal annual gift tax exclusion (currently $18,000 per donee per year, as adjusted for inflation under IRC § 2503(b));
- Gifts to my [Spouse/Partner] not to exceed the federal annual gift tax exclusion amount;
- Contributions to IRC § 529 education savings plans for the benefit of my children and grandchildren, not to exceed [$ amount] per beneficiary per year;

(b) No gift shall be made to my Agent unless specifically authorized in the Special Instructions below.

(c) Gifting authority shall be exercised only in a manner consistent with my established pattern of giving, my estate plan, and my anticipated future financial needs.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Under Minn. Stat. § 523.15(b), a grant of gifting authority without limitation allows gifts of any amount to any person, including the agent. This can be disastrous. ALWAYS include specific limitations on gifting authority. Consider whether gifts to the agent should be permitted (e.g., for Medicaid planning purposes) and, if so, limit them to an ascertainable standard.

### PART 5 — AGENT'S DUTIES AND OBLIGATIONS

**Section 5.1 — Statutory Duties.**

My Agent shall comply with all duties imposed by Minn. Stat. § 523.16, including the duty to:

(a) Act in accordance with my reasonable expectations to the extent actually known by the Agent; otherwise, in my best interest;

(b) Act in good faith;

(c) Act only within the scope of authority granted in this Power of Attorney;

(d) Keep my property separate from the Agent's property (except to the extent the Agent is a co-owner);

(e) Keep adequate records of all receipts, disbursements, and significant actions taken on my behalf.

**Section 5.2 — Accounting.**

My Agent shall maintain complete and accurate records of all transactions conducted on my behalf and shall make such records available for inspection upon the request of:

(a) Me, if I have capacity;

(b) My guardian or conservator, if one is appointed;

(c) My Successor Agent;

(d) A governmental agency having authority to protect my welfare;

(e) Any other person authorized by Minn. Stat. § 523.16 to request an accounting.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** MUPAA (Minn. Stat. § 523.16, subd. 2) provides that the Agent must preserve the principal's estate plan to the extent actually known by the Agent. This is a significant duty — the agent should be informed of the principal's estate plan to avoid inadvertently undermining it (e.g., by changing beneficiary designations or making gifts inconsistent with the plan).

### PART 6 — THIRD-PARTY ACCEPTANCE

**Section 6.1 — Third-Party Obligations.**

Under Minn. Stat. § 523.18, a third party that receives a properly executed power of attorney or an Agent's certification shall accept the power of attorney, except in limited circumstances specified by statute.

**Section 6.2 — Liability for Refusal.**

Under Minn. Stat. § 523.18, subd. 3, a third party that refuses to accept an acknowledged power of attorney without a statutory basis for refusal is subject to:

(a) A court order mandating acceptance;
(b) Liability for reasonable attorney fees and costs incurred to compel acceptance;
(c) Liability for damages, including consequential damages.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** One of the most important MUPAA provisions is the mandatory acceptance requirement. Before MUPAA, financial institutions routinely refused to honor POAs, particularly those more than a few years old. Under MUPAA, a third party must accept the POA within four business days of receiving the Agent's certification (Minn. Stat. § 523.18, subd. 1). The third party may request, but may not require, an opinion of counsel. Despite this statutory protection, some institutions still resist. Advise clients to:
>
> 1. Present the POA to financial institutions immediately upon execution to establish it on file;
> 2. Consider executing the institution's own POA form in addition to the statutory form;
> 3. Carry copies of Minn. Stat. § 523.18 when presenting the POA.

---

### PART 7 — REVOCATION AND TERMINATION

**Section 7.1 — Revocation.**

I may revoke this Power of Attorney at any time by providing written notice to my Agent and to any third party that has been presented with this Power of Attorney.

**Section 7.2 — Termination.**

This Power of Attorney terminates upon:

(a) My death;
(b) My revocation of this Power of Attorney;
(c) The occurrence of a terminating event specified in this document;
(d) The Agent's death, incapacity, or resignation (unless a Successor Agent is designated and willing to serve);
(e) The appointment of a guardian or conservator for me (the power continues, but the guardian/conservator may revoke or amend under Minn. Stat. § 523.06).

**Section 7.3 — Effect of Divorce.**

Under Minn. Stat. § 523.08, if my Agent is my spouse and our marriage is dissolved or annulled after the execution of this Power of Attorney, the Agent's authority is automatically revoked.

### PART 8 — EXECUTION

**IMPORTANT INFORMATION ABOUT EXECUTION:**
Under Minn. Stat. § 523.01, this Power of Attorney must be:
1. Signed by the Principal (or by another individual in the Principal's conscious presence and at the Principal's direction);
2. Acknowledged before a notary public or other individual authorized to take acknowledgments.

> **NOTE:** Minnesota does NOT require witnesses for a POA, but notarization IS required.

## APPENDIX: COMMON PITFALLS AND PRACTICE CONSIDERATIONS

### Pitfalls

1. **Failing to grant specific authority under § 523.15.** A general grant covers routine transactions but does NOT include gifting, trust amendments, beneficiary designation changes, or delegation. Omitting specific grants where needed is the most common MUPAA drafting error.

2. **Unlimited gifting authority.** Granting unrestricted gifting authority invites abuse and can result in depletion of the principal's estate. Always limit gifting scope, amounts, and recipients.

3. **No successor agent.** If the sole agent cannot serve and no successor is named, the POA terminates. Always name at least one successor.

4. **Springing power with impractical triggering mechanism.** Requiring two physicians to certify incapacity sounds reasonable but can be logistically difficult and time-consuming when immediate action is needed (e.g., paying the principal's mortgage while they are in the hospital).

5. **Failure to coordinate with estate plan.** The POA agent must know about and be empowered to work with the principal's revocable trust, including funding the trust during incapacity. Without the specific authority to amend/fund trusts, the agent may be unable to execute the estate plan.

6. **Stale POA.** While MUPAA prohibits third parties from rejecting a POA solely based on its age, practical compliance varies. Consider re-executing the POA every 5-7 years to minimize resistance.

7. **Out-of-state use.** A Minnesota POA is valid in Minnesota but may face resistance in other states. For clients with property in multiple states (e.g., Florida), consider executing a POA in each state where the client owns property or maintains financial accounts.

### Practice Recommendations

- Execute the POA at the same time as the will, trust, and healthcare directives.
- Provide the agent with a copy and the original to the principal for safekeeping.
- Have the agent present the POA to major financial institutions immediately upon execution.
- Include a letter to the agent explaining their duties and providing a list of the principal's assets, accounts, and advisors.
- Review the POA at least every 3-5 years or upon any significant life event.
