# Healthcare Power of Attorney / Healthcare Directive — Annotated Template

**Document Type:** Template with Practice Notes  
**Jurisdiction:** Minnesota  
**Last Updated:** April 2026  
**Applicable Law:** Minnesota Health Care Directive Act, Minn. Stat. §§ 145C.01–145C.16  

---

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Minnesota uses a unified "Health Care Directive" that combines the appointment of a health care agent (healthcare power of attorney) with instructions regarding treatment preferences (living will). This template follows the statutory framework established by the Minnesota Health Care Directive Act (Minn. Stat. Chapter 145C). A single document can serve both functions. This template is structured to address agent appointment, treatment preferences, HIPAA authorization, organ donation, and mental health treatment provisions.

---

## HEALTH CARE DIRECTIVE

### PART 1 — IDENTIFICATION

I, **[PRINCIPAL FULL LEGAL NAME]**, of [City], [County] County, State of Minnesota (Date of Birth: [DOB]), make this Health Care Directive to exercise my right to determine my own health care and to provide instructions and authority to my appointed health care agent(s) to make health care decisions on my behalf if I become unable to make or communicate those decisions myself.

---

I appoint the following person as my Health Care Agent, with the authority to make any and all health care decisions on my behalf that I could make if I had the capacity to do so, subject to any limitations stated in this Directive:

Name: **[AGENT FULL LEGAL NAME]**  
Address: [Agent Address]  
Phone (Day): [Phone]  
Phone (Evening/Cell): [Phone]  
Email: [Agent Email]  
Relationship: [Relationship to Principal]

**Section 2.2 — First Alternate Health Care Agent.**

If my primary Health Care Agent is unable, unwilling, or unavailable to serve, I appoint:

Name: **[ALTERNATE 1 FULL LEGAL NAME]**  
Address: [Address]  
Phone: [Phone]  
Relationship: [Relationship to Principal]

**Section 2.3 — Second Alternate Health Care Agent.**

If neither my primary nor first alternate Health Care Agent is able to serve, I appoint:

Name: **[ALTERNATE 2 FULL LEGAL NAME]**  
Address: [Address]  
Phone: [Phone]  
Relationship: [Relationship to Principal]

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Under Minn. Stat. § 145C.03, the health care agent must be at least 18 years old. A health care provider or employee of a health care provider may not be appointed as agent unless the person is related to the principal by blood, marriage, or adoption, or is a registered domestic partner. The agent should be someone who (1) knows the principal's values and wishes, (2) is willing to carry them out even if the agent personally disagrees, and (3) is available and able to advocate effectively with medical providers.

---

### PART 3 — AUTHORITY OF HEALTH CARE AGENT

**Section 3.1 — Scope of Authority.**

My Health Care Agent has the authority to make all health care decisions on my behalf, including but not limited to:

(a) Consent to, refuse, or withdraw consent for any type of health care, treatment, service, or procedure, including life-sustaining treatment;

(b) Select and discharge health care providers and institutions;

(c) Approve or disapprove diagnostic tests, surgical procedures, and programs of medication;

(d) Direct the provision, withholding, or withdrawal of nutrition and hydration delivered by artificial means;

(e) Consent to admission to or discharge from any hospital, nursing home, assisted living facility, adult foster care, or similar institution;

(f) Access my medical records and health care information, and authorize the release thereof;

(g) Take any legal action necessary to carry out my wishes regarding health care, including granting releases or waivers to health care providers;

(h) Authorize pain management and comfort care, including aggressive pain management even if such treatment may hasten death;

(i) Make decisions regarding autopsy, disposition of my body, and organ or tissue donation, subject to Part 7 below;

(j) Contract for health care services, including home health care, hospice care, and palliative care, and obligate me financially for such services.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Under Minn. Stat. § 145C.06, the agent's authority is as broad as the principal would have had, unless specifically limited. If the principal wants to restrict the agent's authority (e.g., prohibiting withdrawal of artificial nutrition), those limitations must be stated explicitly. The default is full authority.

**Section 3.2 — When Authority Becomes Effective.**

My Health Care Agent's authority to make health care decisions on my behalf becomes effective when my attending physician determines that I lack the capacity to make or communicate my own health care decisions. This determination shall be made in accordance with Minn. Stat. § 145C.01, subd. 4 (definition of "lack of decision-making capacity").

> **DRAFTING TIP:** Some clients want the agent to be able to act immediately (e.g., to access medical records, communicate with physicians) even before the principal loses capacity. Consider adding language such as: "Notwithstanding the foregoing, my Health Care Agent is authorized to access my medical records, communicate with my health care providers, and participate in treatment discussions at any time, whether or not I have decision-making capacity."

**Section 3.3 — Limitations on Agent's Authority.**

[State any limitations. If none, state: "I do not place any limitations on my Health Care Agent's authority beyond those imposed by law."]

(a) [Example: My Health Care Agent may not authorize the withdrawal of artificial nutrition and hydration unless my attending physician and one consulting physician determine that death is imminent regardless of treatment.]

(b) [Example: My Health Care Agent may not consent to my admission to a facility for the treatment of mental illness under Part 8 of this Directive unless the conditions stated therein are met.]

**Section 3.4 — Guidance to Health Care Agent.**

I provide the following guidance to my Health Care Agent regarding my values and wishes:

(a) **Quality of Life.** I value [describe — e.g., independence, ability to communicate, ability to recognize family, ability to live at home] more than mere prolongation of life.

(b) **Pain Management.** I [want/do not want] aggressive pain management, even if it may hasten my death or render me unconscious.

(c) **Spiritual/Religious Considerations.** [Describe any faith-based considerations that should guide health care decisions, or state "none."]

(d) **Location of Care.** I prefer to [remain at home / be in a facility / have no preference] during serious illness or end of life, if medically appropriate.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** This guidance section is not legally required but is extremely valuable. The health care agent may face difficult decisions not specifically addressed in the directive. Knowing the principal's values, priorities, and preferences helps the agent make decisions consistent with what the principal would have wanted. Encourage clients to have thorough conversations with their agents about these topics, not just sign the form.

### PART 4 — HEALTH CARE INSTRUCTIONS (LIVING WILL PROVISIONS)

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Under Minn. Stat. § 145C.04, a health care directive may include instructions regarding health care that apply even if no agent is appointed or the agent is unavailable. These instructions are binding on health care providers, subject to the provider's right of conscientious objection under Minn. Stat. § 145C.09.

**Section 4.1 — Terminal Condition.**

If I have a terminal condition from which death is expected within a relatively short time even with available medical treatment:

- [ ] I direct that life-sustaining treatment BE PROVIDED to prolong my life as long as possible.
- [ ] I direct that life-sustaining treatment NOT be provided. I want only comfort care and pain management.
- [ ] I leave this decision to my Health Care Agent.

**Section 4.2 — Persistent Vegetative State or Irreversible Coma.**

If I am in a persistent vegetative state or irreversible coma with no reasonable hope of recovery:

- [ ] I direct that life-sustaining treatment BE PROVIDED indefinitely.
- [ ] I direct that life-sustaining treatment be WITHDRAWN, with comfort care provided.
- [ ] I leave this decision to my Health Care Agent.

**Section 4.3 — Advanced Dementia or Severe Cognitive Impairment.**

If I have advanced, progressive dementia or other severe cognitive impairment and I am unable to recognize my family, communicate meaningfully, or perform basic activities of daily living:

- [ ] I direct that all available medical treatment BE PROVIDED, including life-sustaining treatment.
- [ ] I direct that life-sustaining treatment NOT be provided, but that comfort care, nutrition (by mouth if possible), hydration, and pain management be continued.
- [ ] I leave this decision to my Health Care Agent.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Dementia-specific provisions are increasingly important as the population ages. Many clients have strong feelings about the level of care they want in advanced dementia but fail to document them. The standard "terminal condition / PVS" provisions do not address the prolonged decline of dementia. This section fills that gap. Note that under Minnesota law, a patient's advance directive expressing a wish to refuse treatment in dementia is binding, though implementation can be practically challenging.

**Section 4.4 — Artificial Nutrition and Hydration.**

Regarding artificial nutrition and hydration (feeding tubes, IV fluids):

- [ ] I want artificial nutrition and hydration to be provided in ALL circumstances.
- [ ] I want artificial nutrition and hydration to be provided UNLESS I am in a terminal condition, persistent vegetative state, or irreversible coma.
- [ ] I do NOT want artificial nutrition and hydration if it would only prolong the process of dying or if I am permanently unconscious.
- [ ] I leave this decision to my Health Care Agent.

**Section 4.5 — Resuscitation (CPR/DNR).**

If my heart stops beating or I stop breathing:

- [ ] I WANT cardiopulmonary resuscitation (CPR) to be attempted.
- [ ] I do NOT want CPR if, in the judgment of my attending physician, I have a terminal condition or am in a persistent vegetative state.
- [ ] I do NOT want CPR under any circumstances.
- [ ] I leave this decision to my Health Care Agent.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** A DNR (Do Not Resuscitate) order must be issued by a physician and is separate from this Directive. However, stating the principal's wishes regarding CPR provides critical guidance to the health care agent and physicians. Under Minn. Stat. § 145C.11, compliance with a health care directive by health care providers acting in good faith provides immunity from civil or criminal liability.

**Section 4.6 — Comfort Care.**

Regardless of any other provisions of this Directive, I ALWAYS want comfort care, including:

(a) Medication for pain relief, even if it may hasten death;
(b) Oral nutrition and hydration to the extent I can accept them;
(c) Positioning, personal hygiene, and care for my dignity;
(d) Emotional and spiritual support;
(e) Hospice care when appropriate.

### PART 5 — HIPAA AUTHORIZATION

**Section 5.1 — Authorization.**

I authorize each of my Health Care Agents named above, and any health care provider, health plan, or other covered entity under the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act of 1996 (HIPAA), 42 U.S.C. § 1320d et seq., and its implementing regulations at 45 C.F.R. Parts 160 and 164, to release, disclose, and provide access to my individually identifiable health information and medical records to my Health Care Agent(s).

**Section 5.2 — Scope.**

This authorization applies to all health information, including but not limited to:

(a) All medical records, including records relating to mental health treatment, substance abuse treatment, and HIV/AIDS treatment;
(b) Billing and insurance records;
(c) Communications with health care providers;
(d) Diagnostic test results, imaging, and laboratory reports.

**Section 5.3 — Duration.**

This HIPAA authorization shall remain in effect until I revoke it in writing or until my death (or, after my death, for such period as my Personal Representative or Health Care Agent may need to administer my estate or carry out the provisions of this Directive).

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** HIPAA authorization is essential. Without it, health care providers may refuse to share medical information with the agent, even when the agent needs the information to make decisions. The authorization should be broad and should expressly cover mental health, substance abuse, and HIV/AIDS records, which are subject to heightened privacy protections under both federal and state law. Some providers prefer a stand-alone HIPAA authorization form — consider providing one in addition to this embedded authorization.

### PART 7 — ORGAN AND TISSUE DONATION

**Section 7.1 — Donation Directive.**

*(Select one)*

- [ ] **OPTION A.** I WANT to donate my organs and tissues upon my death for transplantation, therapy, medical research, and education. I authorize my Health Care Agent to consent to such donation.

- [ ] **OPTION B.** I want to donate ONLY the following organs/tissues: [specify]. I do not consent to donation of any other organs or tissues.

- [ ] **OPTION C.** I do NOT want to donate my organs or tissues.

- [ ] **OPTION D.** I leave the decision regarding organ and tissue donation to my Health Care Agent and/or my family.

**Section 7.2 — Life Support for Organ Donation.**

If I have elected organ donation, I understand and agree that life-sustaining treatment may be maintained for a reasonable period after my death has been declared for the sole purpose of preserving my organs for transplantation.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Under the Minnesota Revised Uniform Anatomical Gift Act (Minn. Stat. §§ 525A.01–525A.25), an individual may make an anatomical gift through a variety of documents, including a health care directive. The gift takes effect upon the individual's death. Under Minn. Stat. § 525A.07, a person's anatomical gift expressed in a signed writing controls over the wishes of family members, and the agent may not revoke the principal's express anatomical gift. Discuss this clearly with clients — some want their family to have final say.

### PART 8 — MENTAL HEALTH TREATMENT

**Section 8.1 — Mental Health Treatment Preferences.**

> **IMPORTANT:** Under Minn. Stat. § 253B.03, subd. 6d, a competent adult may make a declaration regarding intrusive mental health treatment, including electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) and neuroleptic medication. This section addresses the principal's preferences regarding mental health treatment.

(a) **Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT).**

- [ ] I consent to the administration of ECT if my Health Care Agent and my attending psychiatrist agree it is appropriate.
- [ ] I do NOT consent to ECT under any circumstances.
- [ ] I leave this decision to my Health Care Agent.

(b) **Psychotropic Medication.**

- [ ] I consent to the administration of psychotropic medication as recommended by my attending physician.
- [ ] I consent to psychotropic medication, but I do NOT want the following medications: [specify medications or classes of medications].
- [ ] I do NOT consent to psychotropic medication, except as authorized by court order.
- [ ] I leave this decision to my Health Care Agent.

(c) **Admission to Mental Health Treatment Facility.**

- [ ] I authorize my Health Care Agent to consent to my admission to an inpatient mental health treatment facility for up to [NUMBER] days if my attending physician determines that such treatment is necessary.
- [ ] I do NOT authorize voluntary admission to a mental health facility. If inpatient treatment is sought, it must be through the civil commitment process under Minn. Stat. Chapter 253B.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Mental health provisions in health care directives are underutilized but critically important. Many clients with a history of mental illness have strong preferences about specific medications, ECT, and hospitalization. A person who is in a mental health crisis may lack the capacity to make treatment decisions. Without a directive, treatment decisions may be delayed or may not reflect the patient's values. Minnesota's civil commitment statutes (Minn. Stat. Chapter 253B) interact with these provisions — ensure the client understands that a directive cannot override a court-ordered commitment.

### PART 10 — GENERAL PROVISIONS

**Section 10.1 — Revocation.**

I may revoke this Health Care Directive at any time by any of the following methods, as provided by Minn. Stat. § 145C.08:

(a) Executing a new Health Care Directive (the most recent directive supersedes all prior directives);
(b) Destroying or defacing this Directive with the intent to revoke it;
(c) A written and signed revocation;
(d) An oral statement made in the presence of a witness and a health care provider that I wish to revoke this Directive.

**Section 10.2 — Conflicting Directives.**

This Health Care Directive supersedes and replaces all prior Health Care Directives, living wills, health care powers of attorney, and similar documents executed by me.

**Section 10.3 — Severability.**

If any provision of this Directive is held invalid, the remaining provisions shall remain in full force and effect.

**Section 10.4 — Governing Law.**

This Directive shall be governed by the laws of the State of Minnesota, specifically the Minnesota Health Care Directive Act, Minn. Stat. Chapter 145C.

**Section 10.5 — Copies.**

Photocopies, facsimile copies, and electronic copies of this Directive shall have the same force and effect as the original (Minn. Stat. § 145C.07).

**Section 10.6 — Immunity.**

Any health care provider who, in good faith, follows the instructions of this Directive or the decisions of my Health Care Agent shall be immune from civil and criminal liability as provided by Minn. Stat. § 145C.11.

### PART 11 — EXECUTION

**EXECUTION REQUIREMENTS (Minn. Stat. § 145C.03):**

A Health Care Directive must be:
1. In writing;
2. Signed by the principal (or by another at the principal's direction, in the principal's presence);
3. Witnessed by one witness OR acknowledged before a notary public.

The witness must NOT be the appointed Health Care Agent.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Minnesota requires only ONE witness or notarization. Best practice is to include BOTH a witness signature AND notarization for maximum portability and acceptance by health care providers. If the client spends time in Florida, note that Florida requires two witnesses for a comparable health care surrogate designation (Fla. Stat. § 765.202).

## APPENDIX: DISTRIBUTION AND COMMUNICATION PLAN

### Recommended Distribution

Provide copies of this Health Care Directive to:

- [ ] Primary Health Care Agent
- [ ] Alternate Health Care Agent(s)
- [ ] Primary care physician
- [ ] Specialist physicians regularly treating the Principal
- [ ] Hospital where the Principal is most likely to be treated
- [ ] Spouse/Partner
- [ ] Close family members
- [ ] Attorney's file
- [ ] Principal's safe deposit box (keep a copy outside the box, as the box may not be accessible in an emergency)
- [ ] State registry (if available — Minnesota does not currently have a centralized directive registry, but some health systems maintain them)

### Communication Recommendations

1. Discuss your wishes thoroughly with your Health Care Agent before executing the Directive.
2. Discuss your wishes with your physician and ensure they are comfortable complying.
3. Review this Directive at least every 3-5 years or after any significant health event.
4. If you spend time in another state (especially Florida), consider executing a directive compliant with that state's laws as well. Florida's Health Care Surrogate Act (Fla. Stat. Chapter 765) has different execution requirements, including two witnesses.
