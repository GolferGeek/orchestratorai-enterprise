# Comprehensive Guide to Beneficiary Designations

**Document Type:** Practice Guide  
**Jurisdiction:** Minnesota and Florida  
**Last Updated:** April 2026  
**Applicable Law:** IRC §§ 401-408A (retirement accounts); Minn. Stat. § 524.2-804 (revocation by divorce); SECURE Act of 2019 and SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022  

---

## INTRODUCTION

Beneficiary designations are the most overlooked and most consequential element of estate planning. Assets that pass by beneficiary designation — life insurance proceeds, retirement accounts, payable-on-death (POD) bank accounts, transfer-on-death (TOD) brokerage accounts — bypass the will and trust entirely. They pass directly to the named beneficiary by operation of contract or statute, regardless of what the will or trust says.

For many American families, beneficiary designation assets represent the majority of their wealth. A $500,000 life insurance policy and a $400,000 401(k) may dwarf the equity in the home and the bank account balances. If the beneficiary designations on those assets are outdated, missing, or inconsistent with the estate plan, the entire plan fails — not because the attorney drafted a bad will, but because no one reviewed the beneficiary designations.

This guide provides a comprehensive framework for reviewing, coordinating, and maintaining beneficiary designations as an integrated component of the estate plan.

## SECTION 1: ASSETS THAT PASS BY BENEFICIARY DESIGNATION

### 1.1 Life Insurance

**How it works:** The policy owner names a beneficiary on the beneficiary designation form filed with the insurance company. Upon the insured's death, the death benefit is paid directly to the named beneficiary. The probate estate and the will have no control over the proceeds (unless the estate is named as beneficiary, which is generally inadvisable).

**Key considerations:**
- Primary and contingent beneficiaries should always be named.
- If the primary beneficiary predeceases the insured and no contingent is named, the proceeds are paid to the insured's estate, subjecting them to probate, creditor claims, and potentially estate tax.
- Naming a trust as beneficiary can provide creditor protection and controlled distribution for young or financially irresponsible beneficiaries.
- Naming a minor as beneficiary creates problems — the insurer cannot pay directly to a minor, and a guardianship or conservatorship may be required.
- Under Minn. Stat. § 524.2-804, divorce automatically revokes a beneficiary designation to the former spouse for insurance policies governed by Minnesota law. However, ERISA-governed policies (employer-provided group life) are preempted by federal law, and the Slayer Statute analysis under *Egelhoff v. Egelhoff*, 532 U.S. 141 (2001), means divorce does NOT automatically revoke the designation. Always update beneficiary designations after divorce — do not rely on the automatic revocation statute.

### 1.2 Retirement Accounts

**Types:** Traditional IRA, Roth IRA, 401(k), 403(b), 457(b), SEP IRA, SIMPLE IRA, pension plans, profit-sharing plans.

**How it works:** The account owner names a beneficiary with the plan administrator or custodian. Upon death, the account passes to the named beneficiary outside probate. The beneficiary's options for withdrawing funds (and the associated tax consequences) depend on the beneficiary's status under the SECURE Act.

**SECURE Act distribution rules (for deaths after January 1, 2020):**

| Beneficiary Type | Distribution Requirement |
|-----------------|------------------------|
| **Surviving spouse** | Rollover to own IRA; treat as own; use Uniform Lifetime Table; or elect 10-year rule |
| **Eligible designated beneficiary (EDB):** Minor child of the account owner, disabled individual, chronically ill individual, individual not more than 10 years younger than the decedent | Life expectancy distributions (stretch IRA); minor child must switch to 10-year rule upon reaching majority (age 21) |
| **Designated beneficiary (non-eligible):** All other individuals | 10-year rule — entire account must be distributed by end of 10th year after death; annual RMDs may be required during the 10-year period (per IRS proposed regulations, finalized 2024) |
| **Not a designated beneficiary:** Estate, charity, non-qualifying trust | 5-year rule (if owner died before required beginning date) or life expectancy of decedent (if owner died after RBD) |

**Key considerations:**
- NEVER transfer ownership of a retirement account to a trust during the owner's lifetime — this triggers immediate full taxation of the account.
- Naming a trust as beneficiary can provide asset protection and controlled distribution, but the trust must qualify as a "see-through trust" (also called a "conduit trust" or "accumulation trust") to use the 10-year rule rather than the 5-year rule.
- For surviving spouses, naming the spouse directly (rather than through a trust) provides the most favorable tax treatment — rollover, use of the Uniform Lifetime Table, and deferral of RMDs until the spouse's own required beginning date.
- Under the SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022, the required beginning date was increased to age 73 (effective 2023) and will increase to age 75 in 2033.

### 1.3 Payable-on-Death (POD) Bank Accounts

**How it works:** Under the Uniform Payable on Death Accounts Act and Minnesota's Multiple-Party Accounts Act (Minn. Stat. §§ 524.6-201 to 524.6-214), a bank account owner may designate one or more POD beneficiaries. Upon the owner's death, the account passes directly to the POD beneficiary without probate. The designation has no effect during the owner's lifetime — the owner retains full control.

**Key considerations:**
- Simple and effective for smaller accounts where the goal is to avoid probate.
- The POD designation overrides the will. If the will says "all bank accounts to my son" but the POD beneficiary is the owner's sister, the sister receives the account.
- No creditor protection for the beneficiary after receipt.
- Consider whether POD designations should be to the revocable trust (to ensure coordination) rather than to individuals.

### 1.4 Transfer-on-Death (TOD) Brokerage Accounts

**How it works:** Under the Uniform Transfer on Death Securities Registration Act (Minn. Stat. §§ 524.6-301 to 524.6-311), the owner of a brokerage account may register the account in TOD form, naming one or more beneficiaries. The beneficiary receives the account upon the owner's death, outside probate.

**Key considerations:**
- Similar to POD accounts, the designation overrides the will and trust.
- Stocks, bonds, mutual funds, and other securities can be registered in TOD form.
- Cost basis receives a step-up at death under IRC § 1014, which can be a significant tax benefit.
- If there are multiple beneficiaries with fractional shares, the broker must divide the account, which may trigger unwanted liquidation of positions.

### 1.5 Transfer-on-Death Deeds for Real Property

**How it works:** Minnesota adopted the Uniform Real Property Transfer on Death Act (Minn. Stat. §§ 507.071). A TOD deed allows the owner to transfer real property to a designated beneficiary at death, without probate, while retaining full ownership and control during life. The deed must be recorded before the owner's death to be effective.

**Key considerations:**
- This is a relatively new tool in Minnesota and an excellent probate avoidance mechanism for real property that is not held in a trust.
- The TOD deed is revocable at any time during the owner's life by recording a revocation.
- The property receives a full step-up in basis at death.
- The TOD deed does NOT override a properly funded trust — if the property is already titled in the trust name, the trust governs.
- For Florida real property: Florida adopted a similar statute effective June 2024 (Fla. Stat. § 732.4017), but special care is needed for homestead property due to constitutional devise restrictions.

### 1.6 Other Beneficiary Designation Assets

- **Annuities:** Named beneficiary receives the death benefit or continuation payments.
- **Health Savings Accounts (HSAs):** If the beneficiary is the surviving spouse, the HSA becomes the spouse's HSA. If the beneficiary is anyone else, the account ceases to be an HSA and the fair market value is taxable to the beneficiary.
- **529 Education Savings Accounts:** Account owner names a successor owner and can change the beneficiary. On death, the successor owner takes over.
- **U.S. Savings Bonds:** POD designee named on the bond or in the TreasuryDirect account.

## SECTION 2: COORDINATION WITH WILL AND TRUST

### 2.1 The Coordination Problem

The will controls only probate assets — assets titled in the decedent's individual name without a beneficiary designation, POD, TOD, or joint tenancy designation. For many clients, this is a small fraction of their total estate.

The trust controls only trust assets — assets re-titled in the name of the trust or naming the trust as beneficiary.

Beneficiary designations control everything else — and for most families, this is the bulk of the estate.

**If these three layers are not coordinated, the estate plan fails.**

Example: A client with a will that divides the estate equally among three children, and a revocable trust designed to hold assets for the children until age 30. The client's 401(k) names one child as sole beneficiary. The life insurance policy still names the ex-spouse (pre-divorce). The bank accounts are POD to the client's mother. Result: the will and trust are nearly irrelevant. The assets pass by beneficiary designation in a completely different pattern than the client intended.

### 2.2 Coordination Strategies

**Strategy 1: Name the Trust as Beneficiary**

For assets where you want the trust's distribution provisions to control:
- Name the revocable living trust as the primary beneficiary of life insurance policies.
- Name the trust as the contingent beneficiary of retirement accounts (primary: surviving spouse for rollover; contingent: trust).
- This ensures all assets flow through the trust and are distributed under the trust's terms.

**Caution:** Naming the trust as beneficiary of a retirement account has tax implications. Ensure the trust qualifies as a see-through trust. If it does not, the 5-year rule applies instead of the 10-year rule.

**Strategy 2: Mirror the Trust's Distribution Plan**

For assets where the tax or administrative benefits of direct beneficiary designation outweigh the coordination benefit:
- Name the same beneficiaries on the designation form that the trust would name.
- Use the same shares (e.g., equal among children).
- Problem: if the trust terms change (e.g., a child is disinherited), the beneficiary designation must also be changed.

**Strategy 3: Hybrid Approach**

- Retirement accounts: Spouse as primary; trust as contingent.
- Life insurance: Trust as primary (if asset protection or controlled distribution is important); children directly as primary (if simplicity is preferred).
- Bank/brokerage accounts: Trust as POD/TOD beneficiary, or individual beneficiaries if the amounts are small.

### 2.3 Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Consequence |
|---------|------------|
| Naming the estate as beneficiary of a retirement account | Loses stretch/10-year rule; subjects account to probate, creditor claims, and estate tax |
| Failing to update after divorce | Former spouse may receive assets (especially ERISA-governed employer plans) |
| Naming a minor child as direct beneficiary | Custodianship or guardianship required; no trust protections |
| Naming a special needs beneficiary directly | May disqualify the beneficiary from SSI, Medicaid, and other means-tested benefits |
| Different beneficiaries on different accounts with no coordination | Unintended unequal distribution |
| Stale contingent beneficiary (or no contingent) | If primary predeceases, assets go to estate by default |
| Naming a trust that does not qualify as a see-through trust | 5-year rule instead of 10-year rule for retirement accounts |

## SECTION 3: TAX IMPLICATIONS

### 3.1 Income Tax

**Life insurance:** Death benefits are generally income tax-free to the beneficiary under IRC § 101(a). This is one of the most powerful tax benefits in the Code. Naming a beneficiary directly (rather than the estate) preserves this benefit and avoids probate.

**Retirement accounts:** Distributions from traditional retirement accounts (IRA, 401(k), 403(b)) are taxed as ordinary income to the beneficiary when distributed. Roth IRA distributions are generally tax-free (if the 5-year holding period is satisfied). The SECURE Act's 10-year rule compresses the distribution period, potentially pushing beneficiaries into higher tax brackets.

**Tax planning opportunity:** If a client has both a large retirement account and a large life insurance policy, consider leaving the retirement account to charity (which pays no income tax) and the life insurance to family members (who receive it income-tax-free). This is sometimes called the "wealth replacement" strategy.

### 3.2 Estate Tax

All beneficiary designation assets are included in the decedent's gross estate for federal estate tax purposes under IRC § 2042 (life insurance) and IRC § 2039 (annuities and retirement accounts). The marital deduction (IRC § 2056) applies to assets passing to the surviving spouse.

For 2025, the federal estate tax exemption is $13.99 million per individual ($27.98 million for a married couple with portability). If current law sunsets as scheduled on January 1, 2026, the exemption will revert to approximately $7 million (indexed for inflation). Clients with estates near or above the exemption threshold should plan accordingly.

### 3.3 Minnesota Estate Tax

Minnesota imposes a state estate tax with an exemption of $3 million (Minn. Stat. § 291.03). This is significantly lower than the federal exemption. Beneficiary designation assets are included in the Minnesota taxable estate. For clients with estates between $3 million and the federal exemption amount, Minnesota estate tax planning is critical even though no federal estate tax is owed.

The Minnesota estate tax rate ranges from 13% to 16% on the taxable estate above the exemption. A married couple can effectively double the exemption through portability-like planning (disclaimer trust, QTIP trust, or outright to spouse).

### 3.4 Florida Estate Tax

Florida does not impose a separate state estate tax or inheritance tax (Fla. Stat. § 198.02, as of current law). This is a significant advantage for Florida domiciliaries with large estates.

## SECTION 4: ANNUAL REVIEW PROCEDURES

### 4.1 When to Review

Beneficiary designations should be reviewed:

1. **Annually** — as part of the client's annual estate plan review.
2. **After any life event:** marriage, divorce, birth of a child, adoption, death of a beneficiary, death of a spouse, significant change in financial circumstances, change of domicile, change of employment (new employer retirement plan).
3. **After any estate plan change:** new will, new trust, trust amendment, trust restatement.
4. **After any change in tax law** that affects retirement account distribution rules, estate tax exemptions, or beneficiary designation treatment.

### 4.2 Review Checklist

For each beneficiary designation asset, verify:

- [ ] **Primary beneficiary** is current and consistent with the estate plan
- [ ] **Contingent beneficiary** is named and current
- [ ] **Beneficiary shares** (percentages or per stirpes) are correct
- [ ] **Minor beneficiaries** are addressed through a trust or UTMA custodianship, not directly
- [ ] **Special needs beneficiaries** are addressed through a special needs trust, not directly
- [ ] **Former spouse** is NOT listed (or is intentionally listed, if required by divorce decree)
- [ ] **Beneficiary contact information** on file with the institution is current
- [ ] **Trust qualifies as see-through trust** if named as beneficiary of a retirement account
- [ ] **No beneficiary designation names the "estate"** as beneficiary

### 4.3 Documentation

Maintain a master beneficiary designation schedule for each client. The schedule should include:

| Asset | Institution | Account # (Last 4) | Primary Beneficiary | Contingent Beneficiary | Date Last Reviewed |
|-------|-----------|-------------------|--------------------|-----------------------|-------------------|
| Life Insurance | [Company] | XXXX | [Name] | [Name] | [Date] |
| 401(k) | [Employer Plan] | XXXX | [Name] | [Name] | [Date] |
| IRA | [Custodian] | XXXX | [Name] | [Name] | [Date] |
| Roth IRA | [Custodian] | XXXX | [Name] | [Name] | [Date] |
| Bank – Checking | [Bank] | XXXX | [Name] POD | n/a | [Date] |
| Bank – Savings | [Bank] | XXXX | [Name] POD | n/a | [Date] |
| Brokerage | [Firm] | XXXX | [Name] TOD | [Name] | [Date] |

Provide a copy of this schedule to the client and retain a copy in the client file. Update annually.

### 4.4 Obtaining Current Designations

Many clients do not know who their current beneficiaries are, particularly for employer retirement plans set up years ago. The following steps may be needed:

1. Contact each financial institution and request a copy of the current beneficiary designation on file.
2. For employer plans, contact the HR department or plan administrator.
3. For older life insurance policies, contact the insurance company directly — the agent who sold the policy may no longer be in business.
4. For assets where the designation is missing or unknown, treat this as an urgent matter and file a new designation immediately.

## SECTION 5: SPECIAL SITUATIONS

### 5.1 Divorce and Beneficiary Designations

Under Minn. Stat. § 524.2-804, divorce or annulment revokes all revocable dispositions to the former spouse, including beneficiary designations on life insurance, retirement accounts, POD accounts, and TOD accounts governed by Minnesota law. However:

- **ERISA preemption:** For employer-sponsored retirement plans (401(k), pension, employer life insurance), federal ERISA law preempts state law. Under *Egelhoff v. Egelhoff*, 532 U.S. 141 (2001), the plan must pay the named beneficiary, even if that person is the former spouse. State automatic revocation statutes do not apply. ALWAYS change beneficiary designations on ERISA plans after divorce.
- **Divorce decree requirements:** Many divorce decrees include provisions requiring one spouse to maintain life insurance for the benefit of the other spouse or the children. Verify that the beneficiary designation complies with any such requirement.
- **QDROs:** A Qualified Domestic Relations Order (QDRO) is required to divide employer retirement plan benefits in a divorce. The QDRO is a separate document from the beneficiary designation and must be approved by the plan administrator.

### 5.2 Blended Families

Blended families present the most complex beneficiary designation challenges. Common goals and conflicts include:

- The client wants to provide for the current spouse but also wants to ensure children from a prior marriage ultimately inherit.
- The current spouse may have their own children to provide for.
- Trust-based planning (QTIP trust, life estate, or Crummey trust) may be necessary to balance competing interests.

**Recommended approach:** Name a trust (not individuals) as the beneficiary of all significant assets. The trust can provide for the surviving spouse during their lifetime and then distribute to the client's children from the prior marriage. This prevents the surviving spouse from redirecting the assets to their own children.

### 5.3 Charitable Beneficiary Designations

Naming a charity as the beneficiary of a retirement account is one of the most tax-efficient charitable giving strategies:

- The charity pays no income tax on the retirement account distribution.
- The estate receives an estate tax charitable deduction for the full value of the account.
- Compare this to leaving the retirement account to family (who pay income tax on distributions) and leaving other assets to charity (which do not generate income tax savings).

For clients who want to make substantial charitable bequests, consider naming the charity as beneficiary of the retirement account and leaving tax-free life insurance or stepped-up-basis assets to the family.

### 5.4 Special Needs Beneficiaries

A beneficiary who receives SSI, Medicaid, or other means-tested government benefits should NEVER be named directly as the beneficiary of life insurance, retirement accounts, or other assets. A direct inheritance may disqualify the beneficiary from these benefits.

**Solution:** Name a special needs trust (also called a supplemental needs trust) as the beneficiary. The trust must be drafted to comply with the requirements of 42 U.S.C. § 1396p(d)(4) and the applicable state Medicaid rules. The trustee can use trust funds to supplement (not replace) the beneficiary's government benefits.

## SECTION 6: PRACTICE RECOMMENDATIONS FOR ATTORNEYS

1. **Make beneficiary designation review a standard part of every estate plan engagement.** Do not assume the client's designations are correct. Obtain current copies of all designations as part of the initial data-gathering process.

2. **Create a beneficiary designation schedule** for every client and include it in the estate planning binder alongside the will and trust.

3. **Draft beneficiary designation forms for the client.** Do not leave it to the client to fill out new designation forms. Prepare the forms, have the client review and sign them, and follow up to confirm the institution has received and processed them.

4. **Follow up.** Financial institutions sometimes lose or fail to process beneficiary designation changes. Contact the institution 30 days after submission to confirm the new designation is on file.

5. **Calendar annual reviews.** Send the client an annual reminder to review their beneficiary designations and provide an updated schedule.

6. **Document your advice.** If the client declines to update a problematic beneficiary designation despite your recommendation, document that advice and the client's decision in a letter to the client file.

7. **Coordinate with the client's financial advisor and insurance agent.** Beneficiary designations are a team effort. Ensure all advisors are aware of the estate plan and the intended beneficiary structure.
