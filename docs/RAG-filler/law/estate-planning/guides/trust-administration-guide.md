# Trust Administration Guide: After the Grantor's Death or Incapacity

**Document Type:** Practice Guide  
**Jurisdiction:** Minnesota (primary), Florida (secondary)  
**Last Updated:** April 2026  
**Applicable Law:** Minn. Stat. Chapter 501C (Minnesota Trust Code); IRC §§ 641-685 (taxation of estates and trusts); Fla. Stat. Chapter 736 (Florida Trust Code)  

---

## INTRODUCTION

Trust administration is the process by which a successor trustee takes control of a revocable living trust after the grantor's death or incapacity and carries out the grantor's wishes as expressed in the trust agreement. Unlike probate administration, trust administration is a private process — there is no court supervision unless a dispute arises or an interested party petitions the court.

This guide provides a comprehensive, step-by-step framework for trust administration in Minnesota, with Florida-specific notes where relevant. It is designed for both attorneys advising successor trustees and for successor trustees who are administering a trust for the first time.

---

## SECTION 1: TRIGGERING EVENTS

### 1.1 Grantor's Death

Upon the grantor's death, the revocable trust becomes irrevocable. The successor trustee must take immediate action to secure trust assets, notify beneficiaries, and begin the administration process. There is no "filing" with the court — the trustee simply steps into the role.

### 1.2 Grantor's Incapacity

If the trust includes incapacity provisions (as most revocable trusts do), the successor trustee assumes management of the trust upon the grantor's incapacity, as determined under the trust's incapacity mechanism (typically one or two physician certifications). During incapacity administration:

- The trust remains revocable (in theory), but the grantor lacks capacity to exercise that power.
- The trustee manages trust assets for the grantor's benefit.
- The trustee should maintain the grantor's standard of living and pay the grantor's bills and obligations from trust assets.
- If the grantor regains capacity, the trustee must restore management to the grantor.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Incapacity administration can last for years, particularly with progressive dementia. The trustee must balance preservation of trust assets against the cost of the grantor's care. Long-term care (nursing home, memory care, home health aides) can cost $8,000-$15,000+ per month in Minnesota. The trustee should consult with an elder law attorney about Medicaid planning options if the trust assets may be insufficient to cover the grantor's care needs for the duration of the incapacity.

## SECTION 2: IMMEDIATE STEPS AFTER GRANTOR'S DEATH

### 2.1 Obtain Certified Death Certificates

Order at least 15-20 certified copies of the death certificate from the funeral home or county vital records office. Financial institutions, insurance companies, and government agencies will each require an original certified copy. In Minnesota, death certificates are available from the county where the death occurred or from the Minnesota Department of Health.

### 2.2 Secure Trust Assets

The trustee's first duty is to take control of and protect all trust assets. This includes:

- Securing the grantor's home (change locks if necessary, ensure insurance is current, winterize if the home will be vacant).
- Locating and securing important documents: trust agreement, will, insurance policies, deeds, financial account statements, tax returns, vehicle titles, business agreements.
- Checking the mail and arranging mail forwarding if needed.
- Protecting valuable personal property (jewelry, art, collections) from loss or theft.
- Continuing to pay recurring bills (mortgage, utilities, insurance, property taxes) to avoid lapse or default.

### 2.3 Identify All Trust and Non-Trust Assets

Create a comprehensive inventory of:

| Category | Examples | Notes |
|----------|----------|-------|
| Trust-titled assets | Real property deeded to trust, bank/brokerage accounts titled in trust name | These are administered by the trustee under the trust terms |
| Pour-over assets | Assets in grantor's individual name that will pour over to the trust through probate | Require probate proceeding (even if simplified) |
| Beneficiary designation assets | Life insurance, retirement accounts, POD/TOD accounts | Pass outside both the trust and probate |
| Joint tenancy assets | Real property or accounts held in joint tenancy with right of survivorship | Pass to surviving joint tenant(s) |
| Community/marital property | Property subject to the surviving spouse's rights | Depends on state law and prenuptial agreements |

### 2.4 Obtain an Employer Identification Number (EIN)

Upon the grantor's death, the trust can no longer use the grantor's Social Security Number. Apply for a new EIN through the IRS (online at irs.gov, Form SS-4). This must be done before the trustee can open trust bank accounts, file tax returns, or transact business on behalf of the trust.

### 2.5 Open a Trust Administration Bank Account

Open a checking account in the name of the trust using the new EIN. All trust receipts (income, insurance proceeds, account transfers) should be deposited to this account, and all trust expenses should be paid from it. This creates a clear paper trail for the accounting.

## SECTION 3: BENEFICIARY NOTIFICATION

### 3.1 Minnesota Notice Requirements

Under Minn. Stat. § 501C.0813, the trustee of a revocable trust must, within **60 days** of the grantor's death, notify:

(a) Each **qualified beneficiary** — meaning each beneficiary who is currently entitled to receive distributions or who would be entitled to receive distributions if the interests of all current distributees terminated;

(b) Each beneficiary who has made a written request for notice.

The notice must include:
- The identity of the grantor and the date of death;
- The trustee's name and contact information;
- The beneficiary's right to request a copy of the trust instrument;
- The beneficiary's right to an annual accounting.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** This notice requirement is one of the most significant aspects of the Minnesota Trust Code. Failure to provide timely notice can expose the trustee to personal liability and can toll the statute of limitations on beneficiary claims against the trustee. Send the notice by certified mail, return receipt requested, and retain the receipt as proof of compliance. If a beneficiary's address is unknown, make reasonable efforts to locate them and document those efforts.

### 3.2 Florida Notice Requirements

Under Fla. Stat. § 736.05055, the trustee of a revocable trust may serve a "Notice of Trust" on qualified beneficiaries and must file the notice with the court having jurisdiction over the grantor's estate. This provides a mechanism for creditor claims against the trust (discussed in Section 5). The notice must include:
- The name and date of death of the settlor;
- The trust identification;
- The trustee's name and address;
- The beneficiary's right to a copy of the trust.

### 3.3 Practical Communication

Beyond the statutory notice, the trustee should communicate proactively with beneficiaries:

- Within the first week: notify all beneficiaries of the death and identify yourself as the trustee.
- Within 30 days: provide a preliminary timeline for administration.
- Quarterly: provide status updates on asset valuation, creditor claims, tax filings, and anticipated distribution timeline.
- Before distribution: provide a final accounting and notice of intended distribution.

Transparent communication reduces the likelihood of disputes and builds trust (no pun intended) between the trustee and the beneficiaries.

## SECTION 4: ASSET MARSHALING AND VALUATION

### 4.1 Marshaling Trust Assets

The trustee must take possession or control of all trust assets. This involves:

**Real property:**
- Obtain a current title search to confirm the trust holds title.
- Obtain a date-of-death appraisal from a qualified real estate appraiser.
- Maintain property insurance, pay property taxes, and keep the property in reasonable condition.
- If the property will be sold, consult with a real estate broker and attorney regarding the sale process. In Minnesota, a trustee generally has the power to sell trust real property without court approval (Minn. Stat. § 501C.0816).

**Financial accounts:**
- Contact each financial institution with a certified death certificate, the trust agreement (or Certificate of Trust under Minn. Stat. § 501C.1013), and the trustee's identification.
- Re-title accounts in the trustee's name (e.g., "[Trustee Name], Trustee of the [Grantor Name] Trust, dated [date], EIN: [XX-XXXXXXX]").
- Obtain date-of-death account statements showing balances and positions.

**Life insurance:**
- File death benefit claims with each insurance company.
- If the trust is the named beneficiary, the proceeds will be paid to the trust administration account.
- If individuals are named beneficiaries, the trustee is not involved — the beneficiary files the claim directly.

**Retirement accounts:**
- Contact plan administrators or custodians to determine the named beneficiary.
- If the trust is the beneficiary, the trustee must work with the custodian to establish an inherited retirement account (inherited IRA) and begin required minimum distributions under the SECURE Act rules.
- Obtain date-of-death account values for estate tax purposes.

**Business interests:**
- Review operating agreements, partnership agreements, or corporate bylaws for succession provisions.
- Determine the value of the business interest (may require a business valuation expert).
- Decide whether to continue operating the business, sell the interest, or wind down the business.

**Personal property:**
- Create a detailed inventory of tangible personal property, including photographs or video documentation.
- Determine if the grantor left a personal property memorandum directing specific bequests.
- Obtain appraisals for items of significant value (art, antiques, jewelry, collections, vehicles).

### 4.2 Date-of-Death Valuation

All trust assets must be valued as of the date of the grantor's death. This is essential for:

- Establishing the beneficiaries' cost basis (step-up in basis under IRC § 1014);
- Federal and state estate tax returns (Form 706, Minnesota M706);
- The trustee's accounting to beneficiaries;
- Determining each beneficiary's share if distribution is by percentage.

For publicly traded securities, use the mean between the high and low trading prices on the date of death (or the average of the means on the trading dates before and after death, if the death occurred on a non-trading day).

For real property, closely held businesses, and unique personal property, engage qualified appraisers.

> **PRACTICE NOTE — ALTERNATE VALUATION DATE:** If a federal estate tax return is required and the estate has declined in value since the date of death, the personal representative (or trustee, if coordinating with the estate) may elect the alternate valuation date — six months after death — under IRC § 2032. This election must be made on a timely filed Form 706 and applies to all assets, not selectively.

## SECTION 5: CREDITOR CLAIMS

### 5.1 Minnesota Creditor Claims Against Trust Assets

Under Minn. Stat. § 501C.0505, trust property of a revocable trust is subject to the claims of the grantor's creditors during the grantor's lifetime and after the grantor's death, to the extent the grantor's probate estate is insufficient to satisfy those claims.

However, Minnesota does not have a specific trust creditor claim procedure equivalent to probate. In practice:

- If there is a concurrent probate proceeding (e.g., for pour-over assets), creditors typically file claims in probate.
- If there is no probate, the trustee should identify known creditors and pay legitimate debts from trust assets.
- The trustee should exercise reasonable diligence in identifying creditors, including reviewing the grantor's mail, financial records, and tax returns for recurring obligations.
- The trustee may wish to open a probate proceeding solely to take advantage of the creditor claim bar date (four months after publication of notice in Minnesota under Minn. Stat. § 524.3-801).

### 5.2 Florida Creditor Claims Against Trust Assets

Florida has a more developed trust creditor claim procedure. Under Fla. Stat. § 736.05053:

- The trustee may publish a Notice to Creditors in a qualified newspaper.
- Creditors have three months from the date of first publication to file claims.
- The trustee must also serve known or reasonably ascertainable creditors with actual notice. Known creditors have 30 days from receipt of actual notice (or three months from publication, whichever is later) to file claims.
- This procedure mirrors the probate creditor claim process and provides finality for the trustee.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** For clients who die as Florida domiciliaries with a fully funded trust (no probate), the Florida trust creditor claim procedure is essential. Without it, creditors have a two-year statute of limitations to bring claims. The publication and notice procedure reduces this to three months, providing much earlier closure for the trustee and beneficiaries.

### 5.3 Priority of Claims

When trust assets are insufficient to satisfy all claims, the trustee should follow the priority established by the applicable state's probate code. In Minnesota, the priority under Minn. Stat. § 524.3-805 is:

1. Costs and expenses of administration
2. Reasonable funeral expenses
3. Debts and taxes with priority under federal law
4. Reasonable and necessary medical and hospital expenses of the last illness
5. Debts and taxes with priority under state law
6. All other claims

## SECTION 6: TAX FILINGS

### 6.1 Final Individual Income Tax Return (Form 1040)

The personal representative (or surviving spouse) must file the grantor's final Form 1040, covering income from January 1 through the date of death. This is due on the normal filing date (April 15 of the following year). If the grantor was married, the surviving spouse may file a joint return for the year of death.

The trustee should coordinate with the personal representative (if different) and the tax preparer to ensure all income, deductions, and credits are properly reported.

### 6.2 Fiduciary Income Tax Return (Form 1041)

After the grantor's death, the trust is a separate taxable entity and must file Form 1041 for each tax year in which it has $600 or more of gross income, any taxable income, or a beneficiary who is a nonresident alien.

**Key features of trust income taxation:**

- Trust income is taxed at highly compressed rates. For 2025, trust income above approximately $15,450 is taxed at the top federal rate of 37%. This makes it tax-advantageous to distribute income to beneficiaries whenever possible.
- The trust receives a distribution deduction (IRC § 661) for amounts distributed or required to be distributed to beneficiaries. The beneficiaries then report the distributed income on their individual returns.
- Distributable Net Income (DNI) determines the character and amount of income that passes through to beneficiaries.
- The trust may elect a fiscal year (unlike estates, trusts must use a calendar year under IRC § 644).

**Minnesota fiduciary income tax:** Minnesota taxes the income of trusts with a Minnesota resident grantor, Minnesota resident beneficiaries, or Minnesota-situs property. File Minnesota Form M2, "Income Tax Return for Estates and Trusts."

### 6.3 Federal Estate Tax Return (Form 706)

Form 706 must be filed if the gross estate (including trust assets, beneficiary designation assets, joint tenancy assets, and lifetime gifts) exceeds the applicable exclusion amount ($13.99 million for 2025). The return is due nine months after the date of death, with an automatic six-month extension available.

Even if no estate tax is owed, a Form 706 may be filed to:
- Elect portability of the deceased spouse's unused exclusion (DSUE) under IRC § 2010(c)(4);
- Establish the basis of assets for capital gains purposes;
- Allocate the generation-skipping transfer (GST) tax exemption.

> **CRITICAL — PORTABILITY:** For married couples, the surviving spouse can use the deceased spouse's unused estate tax exemption, but ONLY if a timely Form 706 is filed to elect portability. There is no dollar threshold for this filing — it must be filed even if the estate is well below the exemption. The IRS has provided a simplified late portability election procedure (Revenue Procedure 2022-32), but timely filing is always preferred.

### 6.4 Minnesota Estate Tax Return (Form M706)

Minnesota requires a state estate tax return if the Minnesota taxable estate exceeds $3 million (Minn. Stat. § 291.03). The Minnesota exemption is NOT portable between spouses (unlike the federal exemption). The return is due nine months after death, with extensions available.

### 6.5 Generation-Skipping Transfer Tax

If any trust beneficiaries are "skip persons" (grandchildren or more remote descendants, or unrelated individuals more than 37.5 years younger than the grantor), the generation-skipping transfer (GST) tax may apply. The GST tax rate is a flat 40% (equal to the top estate tax rate) and applies in addition to the estate tax. Each individual has a GST exemption (equal to the estate tax exemption — $13.99 million for 2025) that can be allocated to transfers to skip persons.

## SECTION 7: DISTRIBUTION PROCEDURES

### 7.1 Timing of Distributions

The trustee should not make distributions until:

1. All debts, expenses, and taxes are paid or adequately reserved for;
2. The creditor claim period has expired (or there is no reasonable basis to expect additional claims);
3. All tax returns have been filed (or adequate reserves are held for anticipated tax liabilities);
4. A final accounting has been prepared and provided to beneficiaries;
5. Beneficiaries have had a reasonable opportunity to review the accounting and raise objections.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Premature distribution is one of the most common trustee errors. If the trustee distributes all trust assets and subsequently a creditor claim, tax liability, or beneficiary dispute arises, the trustee may be personally liable. Hold adequate reserves and do not rush to distribute. A reasonable timeline for most trust administrations is 6-12 months after death, though more complex estates may take 18-24 months.

### 7.2 Partial Distributions

In many cases, the trustee can make partial distributions of specific bequests (e.g., tangible personal property, specific dollar amounts) relatively early, while retaining the residuary estate for later distribution after all liabilities are resolved.

### 7.3 Distributions in Cash vs. In Kind

The trustee typically has discretion to distribute assets in cash, in kind (i.e., transferring the actual asset), or a combination. Considerations include:

- **Marketable securities:** Can be distributed in kind by transferring shares to the beneficiary's brokerage account. The beneficiary receives a stepped-up basis as of the date of death.
- **Real property:** Can be distributed in kind by executing a trustee's deed to the beneficiary. Consider whether the property should be sold and proceeds distributed instead, particularly if there are multiple beneficiaries.
- **Cash:** The simplest option, particularly when assets must be divided among multiple beneficiaries in specific percentages.
- **Tax considerations:** Distributing appreciated assets in kind does not trigger capital gains tax at the trust level (the beneficiary takes the trust's basis, which is the stepped-up date-of-death value).

### 7.4 Receipts and Releases

Before making final distributions, the trustee should obtain a signed receipt and release from each beneficiary, acknowledging:

- Receipt of the distribution;
- Review of the trustee's accounting;
- Release of the trustee from liability for actions taken during administration (except for fraud, willful misconduct, or gross negligence).

A beneficiary is not required to sign a release, but the trustee should request one. If a beneficiary refuses, the trustee may petition the court for approval of the accounting and discharge under Minn. Stat. § 501C.0817.

## SECTION 8: ACCOUNTING REQUIREMENTS

### 8.1 Trustee's Duty to Account

Under Minn. Stat. § 501C.0813, subd. 3, the trustee of an irrevocable trust must provide an annual accounting to each qualified beneficiary who requests one. The accounting must include:

- A statement of trust receipts and disbursements for the period;
- A listing of trust assets with current values;
- The trustee's compensation for the period;
- A statement of any changes in the trust's assets (sales, purchases, distributions);
- Any other information necessary to protect the beneficiary's interests.

### 8.2 Format

There is no required statutory format for the trust accounting in Minnesota, but best practice is to follow a format similar to the Uniform Principal and Income Act categories:

1. **Receipts:** Income (interest, dividends, rent, business income), principal additions (insurance proceeds, account transfers, pour-over from probate)
2. **Disbursements:** Administration expenses (attorney fees, trustee compensation, accounting fees, appraisal fees), debts paid, taxes paid, specific distributions, income distributions, principal distributions
3. **Assets on hand:** Detailed listing of all trust assets with current fair market value
4. **Gains and losses:** Capital gains and losses from sales of trust assets

### 8.3 Principal and Income Allocation

Under the Uniform Principal and Income Act (adopted in Minnesota at Minn. Stat. § 501C.0901 et seq.) and the new Uniform Fiduciary Income and Principal Act (UFIPA), the trustee must allocate receipts and disbursements between income and principal. Key allocations:

| Receipt/Disbursement | Allocation |
|---------------------|-----------|
| Interest, dividends, rent | Income |
| Capital gains | Principal |
| Insurance proceeds | Principal |
| Trustee compensation | 50% income / 50% principal (default) |
| Attorney and accounting fees | Typically principal (may be allocated to income if related to income administration) |
| Property taxes and insurance | Income (for income-producing property) or principal |
| Depreciation | Principal |

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** The distinction between income and principal matters when the trust has separate income and remainder beneficiaries (e.g., "income to surviving spouse for life, remainder to children"). For trusts that distribute outright to a single class of beneficiaries, the distinction is less important but still relevant for tax purposes.

## SECTION 9: TRUSTEE COMPENSATION

### 9.1 Minnesota Standards

Under the Minnesota Trust Code, a trustee is entitled to reasonable compensation (Minn. Stat. § 501C.0708). "Reasonable" is determined by reference to:

- The complexity of the trust and its assets;
- The time and skill required;
- The trustee's experience and qualifications;
- Compensation paid to trustees of comparable trusts in the community;
- The trust terms (if the trust specifies compensation).

### 9.2 Typical Fee Structures

| Trustee Type | Typical Fee |
|-------------|------------|
| Individual (family member) | Often serves without compensation or at a modest flat fee ($2,000-$10,000 per year) |
| Individual (professional, non-corporate) | $75-$200 per hour, or 0.5%-1.0% of trust assets annually |
| Corporate trustee | 0.5%-1.5% of trust assets annually, often with minimum fees of $3,000-$10,000/year |

### 9.3 Tax Treatment

Trustee compensation is taxable income to the trustee and deductible by the trust as an administration expense on Form 1041 (subject to the 2% floor for miscellaneous itemized deductions, which was suspended through 2025 by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act but applies to trusts differently).

## SECTION 10: COMMON PITFALLS IN TRUST ADMINISTRATION

1. **Failure to provide statutory notice to beneficiaries.** This is a mandatory requirement under Minn. Stat. § 501C.0813. Failure to comply can toll statutes of limitation and expose the trustee to liability.

2. **Commingling trust funds with personal funds.** The trustee MUST keep trust assets separate from personal assets. Open a dedicated trust bank account and never deposit trust funds into a personal account.

3. **Premature distribution.** Distributing all assets before confirming that all debts, taxes, and claims are satisfied. The trustee may be personally liable for amounts that should have been withheld.

4. **Failure to file Form 1041.** After the grantor's death, the trust is a separate tax entity. Failure to file the fiduciary income tax return can result in penalties and interest from the IRS and Minnesota Department of Revenue.

5. **Failure to elect portability.** For married couples, failing to file Form 706 to elect portability of the deceased spouse's unused exemption is an irreversible planning loss (except through the late election procedure, which has a limited window).

6. **Self-dealing.** The trustee purchasing trust assets for personal use, lending trust funds to themselves, or using trust assets for personal benefit. This is a breach of fiduciary duty under Minn. Stat. § 501C.0802.

7. **Failure to diversify investments.** Under the Prudent Investor Rule (Minn. Stat. § 501C.0901), the trustee must diversify trust investments unless special circumstances make concentration reasonable. Holding a concentrated position in a single stock (even if it was the grantor's favorite stock) is a breach of duty unless the trust specifically authorizes retention.

8. **Ignoring the trust terms.** The trust agreement is the governing document. The trustee must read it carefully and follow its terms. Beneficiaries' requests that contradict the trust terms must be declined.

9. **Failure to communicate with beneficiaries.** Even when not legally required, regular communication reduces disputes. Silence breeds suspicion.

10. **Acting without professional advice.** Trust administration involves tax, legal, and financial complexities. A trustee who attempts to administer a significant trust without professional guidance is likely to make costly errors.

## SECTION 11: TRUST ADMINISTRATION TIMELINE

| Timeframe | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| **Week 1** | Obtain death certificates; secure trust assets; identify successor trustee responsibilities; notify immediate family |
| **Week 2-4** | Obtain EIN; open trust administration bank account; begin marshaling assets; engage attorney, accountant, and appraiser |
| **Within 60 days** | Send statutory notice to beneficiaries (Minn. Stat. § 501C.0813) |
| **Month 1-3** | Complete asset inventory; obtain date-of-death valuations; identify and pay debts; file life insurance claims; begin beneficiary designation transfers |
| **Month 3-6** | Resolve creditor claims; file grantor's final income tax return (1040); prepare trust accounting |
| **Month 6-9** | File estate tax returns (Form 706, M706) if required; file fiduciary income tax return (Form 1041); make partial distributions if appropriate |
| **Month 9-12** | Receive tax clearance; prepare final accounting; obtain beneficiary receipts and releases; make final distributions |
| **Month 12+** | Close trust administration; file final Form 1041; retain records for at least 7 years |
