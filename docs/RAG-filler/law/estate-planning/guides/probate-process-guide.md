# Minnesota Probate Process Guide

**Document Type:** Practice Guide  
**Jurisdiction:** Minnesota  
**Last Updated:** April 2026  
**Applicable Law:** Minn. Stat. Chapter 524 (Uniform Probate Code — Minnesota version); Minn. R. Gen. Prac. 401-418 (Probate Rules)  

---

## INTRODUCTION

Probate is the legal process by which a deceased person's estate is administered, debts are paid, and remaining assets are distributed to the rightful heirs or beneficiaries. In Minnesota, probate is governed by the Uniform Probate Code (UPC) as adopted in Minnesota Statutes Chapter 524.

Minnesota's UPC-based system is generally considered one of the more efficient and streamlined probate systems in the United States. The UPC provides multiple paths through probate — from fully supervised formal proceedings to largely unsupervised informal proceedings — allowing the complexity of the process to match the complexity (and contentiousness) of the estate.

This guide covers the complete probate process in Minnesota, including the types of probate proceedings, personal representative appointment, inventory and appraisal, creditor claims, distribution, and closing the estate.

---

## SECTION 1: TYPES OF PROBATE PROCEEDINGS IN MINNESOTA

### 1.1 Informal Probate (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-301 et seq.)

Informal probate is the simplest and most common form of probate in Minnesota. It is appropriate when:

- There is no dispute about the validity of the will;
- There is no dispute about who should serve as personal representative;
- The estate is not unusually complex;
- All interested persons agree on the proposed personal representative.

**Key features:**
- Application is filed with the court, but there is no hearing unless a dispute arises.
- The registrar (a court officer) reviews the application and, if all requirements are met, issues a Statement of Informal Probate and Letters of General Administration.
- The personal representative administers the estate without ongoing court supervision (unsupervised administration).
- The process is faster, less expensive, and more private than formal probate.

**Limitations:**
- If any interested person objects, the proceeding may be converted to formal probate.
- Certain actions (such as selling real property to the personal representative or distributing a disputed estate) may require court approval even in informal proceedings.

### 1.2 Formal Probate (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-401 et seq.)

Formal probate involves a court hearing before a judge and is required when:

- The validity of the will is contested;
- There is a dispute about who should serve as personal representative;
- A testacy determination is needed (i.e., whether the decedent died with or without a valid will);
- The court's involvement is needed to resolve a specific issue (e.g., interpretation of the will, removal of a personal representative).

**Key features:**
- A petition is filed, and notice is given to all interested persons.
- A hearing is held, and the judge issues an order.
- Formal probate may be supervised or unsupervised. Supervised probate requires court approval for most significant actions (sales, distributions, compensation). Unsupervised formal probate is similar to informal probate but with a judicial determination at the outset.

### 1.3 Supervised Administration (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-501 et seq.)

Supervised administration is the most restrictive form of probate. Any interested person may petition for supervised administration. Once ordered, the personal representative must obtain court approval before:

- Making distributions;
- Selling real property;
- Paying compensation to the personal representative or attorney;
- Taking other significant actions.

Supervised administration is typically requested when there is distrust among the beneficiaries, concern about the personal representative's competence or impartiality, or a complex estate with high litigation risk.

### 1.4 Summary Proceedings — Small Estate Alternatives

Minnesota provides two simplified alternatives for small estates:

**a) Collection of Personal Property by Affidavit (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-1201)**

If the total value of all personal property in the estate (excluding real property, joint tenancy property, and beneficiary designation assets) does not exceed **$75,000** and at least 30 days have passed since the decedent's death:

- A successor (heir or devisee) may collect the decedent's personal property by presenting an affidavit to the holder of the property.
- No court proceeding is required.
- The affidavit must state that the total value of personal property does not exceed $75,000, that 30 days have passed, and that no probate proceeding has been commenced.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** This is an extremely useful tool for small estates. It can be used to collect bank accounts, brokerage accounts, security deposits, final paychecks, and other personal property without opening a probate case. However, it cannot be used for real property. If the decedent owned real property in their individual name (not in a trust, not in joint tenancy), a probate proceeding or affidavit of heirship will be needed.

**b) Summary Administration (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-1203)**

If the entire estate (including real property) can be administered under an expedited process — for example, if the estate qualifies for special handling due to the surviving spouse's right to certain allowances — summary administration may be available.

## SECTION 2: INITIATING PROBATE

### 2.1 Determining Whether Probate Is Necessary

Probate is necessary only for assets that were titled in the decedent's individual name at death without a beneficiary designation, POD, TOD, or joint tenancy designation. These are called "probate assets."

**Assets that DO require probate:**
- Real property titled in the decedent's name alone (or as tenants in common)
- Bank accounts in the decedent's name alone without POD
- Vehicles titled in the decedent's name alone
- Personal property of significant value
- Business interests in the decedent's name
- Claims and causes of action belonging to the decedent

**Assets that do NOT require probate:**
- Trust assets (titled in the trust's name)
- Joint tenancy assets with right of survivorship
- Life insurance with a named beneficiary
- Retirement accounts with a named beneficiary
- POD bank accounts
- TOD brokerage accounts
- TOD deeds

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** For estates with a fully funded revocable living trust, probate may not be necessary at all (or may involve only a minimal pour-over probate for any assets that were not transferred to the trust during life). Even a minimal probate can be useful to take advantage of the creditor claim bar date.

### 2.2 Venue

Probate is filed in the county of the decedent's domicile at the time of death (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-201). If the decedent was domiciled outside Minnesota but owned real property in Minnesota, ancillary probate may be filed in the county where the property is located (Minn. Stat. § 524.4-204).

### 2.3 Filing the Application or Petition

**For informal probate:** File an Application for Informal Probate of Will and Informal Appointment of Personal Representative (or Application for Informal Appointment of Personal Representative in an Intestate Estate) with the district court.

**For formal probate:** File a Petition for Formal Probate of Will and Formal Appointment of Personal Representative with the district court.

Required documents typically include:
- The original will (if any);
- A certified death certificate;
- The application or petition;
- Proposed Letters of General Administration;
- Filing fee (varies by county; typically $300-$400 as of 2026);
- Acceptance of appointment by the proposed personal representative;
- A bond or bond waiver (if the will waives bond, no bond is required unless an interested person requests one).

### 2.4 Notice to Interested Persons

**Informal probate:** No notice is required before filing. After appointment, the personal representative must give written notice of the appointment to all heirs and devisees within 30 days (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-705).

**Formal probate:** Notice must be given to all interested persons at least 14 days before the hearing. Notice may be given by mail or personal service (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-403).

## SECTION 3: PERSONAL REPRESENTATIVE

### 3.1 Who May Serve

Under Minn. Stat. § 524.3-203, priority for appointment as personal representative is:

| Priority | Person |
|----------|--------|
| 1 | Person named in the will |
| 2 | Surviving spouse who is a devisee (named in the will) |
| 3 | Other devisees of the decedent |
| 4 | Surviving spouse (not named in the will) |
| 5 | Other heirs of the decedent |
| 6 | Creditors (45 days after death) |
| 7 | Any suitable person (if no higher-priority person is willing and able) |

A personal representative must be at least 18 years old and must not have been convicted of a felony. Non-residents may serve but may be required to appoint an agent for service of process in Minnesota.

### 3.2 Letters of General Administration

Upon appointment, the court issues Letters of General Administration (or Letters Testamentary). These letters are the personal representative's proof of authority to act on behalf of the estate. Financial institutions, title companies, and other third parties will require presentation of the Letters before dealing with the personal representative.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Obtain several certified copies of the Letters. Each institution will require its own copy, and the copies must be dated within 30-60 days of presentation (most institutions will not accept stale Letters).

### 3.3 Powers of the Personal Representative

Under Minn. Stat. § 524.3-715, the personal representative has broad powers, including the power to:

- Collect and preserve estate property;
- Pay debts, claims, and expenses of administration;
- Sell, lease, mortgage, or exchange estate property (real and personal) at public or private sale;
- Borrow money and pledge estate assets;
- Continue operation of the decedent's business;
- Vote stocks and exercise rights as a security holder;
- Employ attorneys, accountants, and other agents;
- Distribute estate property in cash or in kind;
- Make tax elections.

In unsupervised administration, these powers may be exercised without court approval. In supervised administration, court approval is required for most significant actions.

### 3.4 Duties of the Personal Representative

The personal representative is a fiduciary and owes duties to the estate and its beneficiaries, including:

- **Duty of loyalty:** Act in the best interests of the estate and beneficiaries, not in the personal representative's own interest.
- **Duty of care:** Exercise the care and skill of a reasonably prudent person in managing estate assets.
- **Duty to inventory:** Prepare an inventory of estate assets within three months of appointment.
- **Duty to account:** Provide information and accountings to interested persons upon request.
- **Duty to administer expeditiously:** Settle and distribute the estate as expeditiously as is consistent with the best interests of the estate and interested persons (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-703).

### 3.5 Compensation

The personal representative is entitled to reasonable compensation for services rendered (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-719). Minnesota does not use a statutory fee schedule — compensation is based on reasonableness, considering:

- The time and effort required;
- The complexity of the estate;
- The results obtained;
- Customary fees in the community.

Typical compensation ranges from 1% to 3% of the gross estate for individual personal representatives, though this varies significantly based on complexity.

## SECTION 4: INVENTORY AND APPRAISAL

### 4.1 Inventory Requirement

Under Minn. Stat. § 524.3-706, the personal representative must prepare an inventory of all probate assets within **three months** after appointment. The inventory must include:

- A description of each asset;
- The fair market value as of the date of death;
- The type and amount of any encumbrance (mortgage, lien).

The inventory must be sent to all interested persons who request it. In informal unsupervised administration, the inventory is not filed with the court unless ordered.

### 4.2 Appraisal

For most assets, the personal representative can determine fair market value based on account statements, online valuations, and comparable sales. For assets that are difficult to value, the personal representative should engage a professional appraiser:

| Asset Type | Appraiser Type |
|-----------|---------------|
| Real property | Licensed real estate appraiser |
| Closely held business interests | Business valuation expert (ASA, ABV, or CVA certified) |
| Art, antiques, and collectibles | Qualified personal property appraiser |
| Jewelry | Certified gemologist/appraiser |
| Mineral rights, oil and gas interests | Petroleum engineer or mineral rights appraiser |

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Appraisal fees are a legitimate estate administration expense and are deductible on the estate tax return (if one is filed) or on the estate's income tax return (Form 1041). For estates that must file Form 706, IRS-compliant appraisals are essential. For real property, the appraiser should be state-certified and follow Uniform Standards of Professional Appraisal Practice (USPAP).

## SECTION 5: CREDITOR CLAIMS

### 5.1 Notice to Creditors

The personal representative must publish a Notice to Creditors in a legal newspaper in the county where the probate case is filed (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-801). The notice must be published once and must include:

- The decedent's name and date of death;
- The personal representative's name and address;
- A statement that creditors must present their claims within four months of the date of publication or the claims will be barred.

In addition, the personal representative must give actual notice (by mail) to all known or reasonably ascertainable creditors (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-801(b)).

### 5.2 Claims Period

Creditors have **four months** from the date of first publication of the Notice to Creditors to present their claims (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-803). Claims not presented within this period are barred, with limited exceptions:

- Creditors who did not receive actual notice and were not reasonably ascertainable have **one year** from the date of death to present claims (the "absolute bar" under Minn. Stat. § 524.3-803(a)).
- Government claims (taxes) may be subject to different limitation periods.

### 5.3 Allowance and Disallowance of Claims

The personal representative reviews each claim and either allows (pays) or disallows (rejects) it. Under Minn. Stat. § 524.3-806:

- If the personal representative does not act on a claim within 60 days, it is deemed allowed.
- A disallowed claim must be contested by the creditor within 60 days of the disallowance, or it is barred.

### 5.4 Priority of Claims

Claims are paid in order of priority under Minn. Stat. § 524.3-805:

1. Costs and expenses of administration (attorney fees, personal representative compensation, court costs);
2. Reasonable funeral expenses (including burial and cremation);
3. Debts and taxes with priority under federal law (e.g., federal income taxes);
4. Reasonable and necessary medical and hospital expenses of the last illness, including compensation of persons who cared for the decedent;
5. Debts and taxes with priority under state law;
6. All other claims.

Within each class, if estate assets are insufficient to pay all claims in full, claims are paid pro rata.

### 5.5 Insolvent Estates

If the estate's debts exceed its assets, the estate is insolvent. The personal representative must pay claims in order of priority and distribute remaining assets pro rata within each class. Beneficiaries receive nothing until all higher-priority claims are satisfied. The personal representative should consult an attorney experienced in insolvent estate administration, as there are specific statutory requirements and potential personal liability issues.

## SECTION 6: FAMILY ALLOWANCES AND EXEMPT PROPERTY

Minnesota law provides certain protections for the surviving spouse and minor children, regardless of the will's terms:

### 6.1 Homestead Allowance (Minn. Stat. § 524.2-402)

The surviving spouse is entitled to a homestead allowance of **$22,500** (adjusted periodically). If there is no surviving spouse, each minor child and each dependent child is entitled to an equal share of the homestead allowance. The homestead allowance is exempt from creditor claims and is in addition to any share passing under the will or by intestacy.

### 6.2 Exempt Property (Minn. Stat. § 524.2-403)

The surviving spouse is entitled to exempt property (household furniture, automobiles, furnishings, appliances, and personal effects) up to a value of **$15,000** beyond the homestead allowance, from the estate. If there is no surviving spouse, the decedent's minor children and dependent children share this allowance.

### 6.3 Family Maintenance Allowance (Minn. Stat. § 524.2-404)

The surviving spouse and minor children whom the decedent was obligated to support are entitled to a reasonable allowance from the estate during the period of administration, but not exceeding **18 months** after the decedent's death. The allowance is intended to maintain the family's standard of living during probate. It has priority over all claims against the estate except costs of administration.

### 6.4 Elective Share (Minn. Stat. § 524.2-202)

Minnesota's elective share allows the surviving spouse to claim a percentage of the "augmented estate" regardless of the will's terms. The elective share percentage depends on the length of the marriage, ranging from approximately 3% (for marriages of less than 1 year) to 50% (for marriages of 15 years or more). The augmented estate includes the probate estate, the decedent's non-probate transfers, the spouse's assets, and the spouse's non-probate transfers.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** The elective share is a powerful tool for a disinherited or underprovided-for surviving spouse. It reaches not only probate assets but also trust assets, beneficiary designation assets, and joint tenancy assets. Clients who wish to disinherit or limit a spouse's inheritance should understand that the elective share sets a floor on the spouse's entitlement. A valid prenuptial or postnuptial agreement can waive the elective share under Minn. Stat. § 524.2-213.

## SECTION 7: DISTRIBUTION

### 7.1 Testate Distribution (With a Will)

If the decedent left a valid will, assets are distributed according to the will's terms, subject to the surviving spouse's rights (homestead allowance, exempt property, family maintenance, elective share) and after payment of debts, expenses, and taxes.

### 7.2 Intestate Distribution (Without a Will)

If the decedent died without a valid will (intestate), assets are distributed according to Minnesota's intestacy statute (Minn. Stat. § 524.2-102 et seq.):

| Survivors | Distribution |
|-----------|-------------|
| Spouse, no descendants or parents | Entire estate to spouse |
| Spouse and descendants (all of whom are also descendants of the spouse) | First $300,000 plus 50% of remainder to spouse; rest to descendants per stirpes |
| Spouse and descendants (one or more of whom are NOT descendants of the spouse) | First $225,000 plus 50% of remainder to spouse; rest to descendants per stirpes |
| Spouse and parent(s) but no descendants | First $300,000 plus 75% of remainder to spouse; rest to parent(s) |
| Descendants, no spouse | Entire estate to descendants per stirpes |
| Parent(s), no spouse or descendants | Entire estate to parent(s) |
| Siblings, no spouse, descendants, or parents | Entire estate to siblings (and descendants of deceased siblings) |
| More remote relatives | To grandparents and their descendants, in a statutory order |
| No heirs | Escheat to the State of Minnesota |

### 7.3 Abatement

If the estate is insufficient to satisfy all bequests, bequests abate (are reduced) in the following order under Minn. Stat. § 524.3-902:

1. Property not disposed of by the will (intestate property);
2. Residuary devises;
3. General devises;
4. Specific devises.

Within each class, devises abate pro rata. The will may override this default order.

### 7.4 Practical Distribution Steps

1. Prepare a proposed distribution plan showing each beneficiary's share.
2. Provide the plan and a final accounting to all beneficiaries.
3. Allow a reasonable period (30 days) for beneficiaries to review and object.
4. Execute deeds, assignments, and transfer documents for in-kind distributions.
5. Obtain receipts and releases from each beneficiary.
6. Distribute assets.
7. Retain a reserve for any remaining expenses (final tax return preparation, attorney fees for closing).

## SECTION 8: CLOSING THE ESTATE

### 8.1 Informal Closing (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-1003)

In an unsupervised informal probate, the personal representative may close the estate by filing a **Closing Statement** with the court. The Closing Statement must include:

- A statement that the personal representative has administered the estate in compliance with Minnesota law;
- A statement that all claims have been paid or barred;
- A statement that all required tax returns have been filed;
- A description of the distributions made.

The Closing Statement must be filed no earlier than **six months** after the date of the personal representative's appointment. After filing, the personal representative is discharged from liability except for claims filed within **one year** of the Closing Statement.

### 8.2 Formal Closing (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-1001)

In supervised or formal proceedings, the personal representative files a **Petition for Complete Settlement** with the court. The petition includes a final accounting, a list of distributions made and proposed, and a request for discharge. Notice is given to all interested persons, and a hearing is held. If the court approves, an order is entered settling the estate and discharging the personal representative.

### 8.3 After Closing

After the estate is closed:

- Retain all estate records for at least **seven years** (for tax audit purposes).
- File the final fiduciary income tax return (Form 1041 and Minnesota M2) for the estate's final tax year.
- Cancel the estate's EIN (or allow it to become inactive).
- Notify the court if any previously unknown assets are discovered (a supplemental administration may be needed under Minn. Stat. § 524.3-1008).

## SECTION 9: SPECIAL TOPICS

### 9.1 Ancillary Probate for Out-of-State Property

If a Minnesota decedent owned real property in another state, ancillary probate must be opened in that state to transfer title. For Minnesota decedents who own Florida real property:

- An ancillary administration proceeding must be opened in the Florida county where the property is located (Fla. Stat. § 734.102).
- The domiciliary personal representative (from Minnesota) may be appointed as the ancillary personal representative in Florida if they meet Florida's requirements (Fla. Stat. § 733.304 — non-resident personal representatives must be a spouse, sibling, parent, child, or other close relative of the decedent, or a resident of Florida).
- Florida homestead property is subject to constitutional restrictions on devise (Art. X, § 4, Fla. Const.) and homestead creditor protections, adding complexity.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Ancillary probate is expensive and time-consuming. It is one of the primary reasons estate planners recommend holding out-of-state real property in a revocable trust or LLC. A TOD deed (where available) is another option. For snowbird clients with Florida property, address this issue proactively during the estate planning engagement.

### 9.2 Will Contests

A will contest must be filed within the earlier of **12 months** after the informal probate or the time for commencing a formal testacy proceeding (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-108). Grounds for contesting a will include:

- **Lack of testamentary capacity:** The testator did not understand the nature of the act of making a will, the extent of their property, or the natural objects of their bounty.
- **Undue influence:** A person in a confidential relationship with the testator exerted influence that overcame the testator's free will.
- **Fraud:** The testator was deceived into signing the will.
- **Improper execution:** The will was not signed and witnessed in compliance with Minn. Stat. § 524.2-502.
- **Revocation:** The will was revoked by a subsequent will, physical destruction, or operation of law.

### 9.3 Disclaimers

Under Minn. Stat. § 524.2-1106 (Uniform Disclaimer of Property Interests Act), any beneficiary may disclaim (refuse) an inheritance within **nine months** of the decedent's death. A disclaimed interest passes as if the disclaimant predeceased the decedent. Disclaimers are a valuable post-mortem planning tool:

- A surviving spouse may disclaim a portion of an outright inheritance, causing it to fall into a bypass trust.
- A beneficiary in a high tax bracket may disclaim in favor of a beneficiary in a lower bracket.
- A beneficiary who does not need the inheritance may disclaim in favor of their children.

The disclaimer must be in writing, must be signed and notarized, and must be delivered to the personal representative within the nine-month period.

### 9.4 Digital Assets

Under the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (Minn. Stat. §§ 521A.01–521A.17), the personal representative has default access to a catalogue of the decedent's electronic communications and the content of other digital assets (files, photos, social media accounts, cryptocurrency wallets, email) but does NOT have default access to the content of electronic communications (email, text messages, social media messages) unless:

- The decedent's will, trust, or power of attorney expressly grants access;
- The decedent used the online tool provided by the service provider (e.g., Google's Inactive Account Manager, Facebook's Legacy Contact);
- A court orders disclosure.

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** Cryptocurrency presents a unique challenge. Unlike traditional financial assets, cryptocurrency is controlled by private keys. If the decedent's private keys are lost, the cryptocurrency may be permanently inaccessible. Advise clients who hold cryptocurrency to include private key recovery information in their estate planning documents (stored securely, of course).

## SECTION 10: PROBATE TIMELINE

| Timeframe | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| **Immediately** | Secure property, locate will, obtain death certificates |
| **Week 1-2** | Engage probate attorney; prepare and file application/petition |
| **Week 2-4** | Receive Letters; open estate bank account; obtain EIN |
| **Within 30 days** | Give written notice to heirs and devisees (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-705) |
| **Within 30 days** | Publish Notice to Creditors |
| **Within 3 months** | Prepare and distribute inventory |
| **4 months after publication** | Creditor claims period expires |
| **Month 4-6** | Resolve claims; prepare accountings; file tax returns |
| **Month 6-9** | File estate tax returns (if required); make distributions |
| **6 months after appointment** | Earliest date to file Closing Statement |
| **Month 9-12** | File Closing Statement; obtain discharge; retain records |

## SECTION 11: COSTS OF PROBATE IN MINNESOTA

| Expense | Typical Range |
|---------|--------------|
| Court filing fee | $300-$400 |
| Attorney fees | $3,000-$15,000+ (depends on estate complexity) |
| Personal representative compensation | 1%-3% of gross estate |
| Publication of Notice to Creditors | $75-$200 |
| Appraisal fees | $300-$5,000+ (depends on asset type) |
| Certified copies of Letters | $10-$25 each |
| Bond premium (if required) | 0.5%-1.0% of estate value annually |
| Real estate transfer fees | Varies by county |
| Accounting/tax preparation | $500-$5,000+ |

> **PRACTICE NOTE:** The total cost of probate in Minnesota for a typical estate ranges from $5,000 to $25,000, depending on complexity. This is significantly less than in states like California, where statutory attorney fees can be 2-4% of the gross estate. Nevertheless, probate avoidance through trust-based planning, TOD deeds, and beneficiary designations can save the family significant time, expense, and privacy.

## SECTION 12: COMMON PITFALLS IN PROBATE ADMINISTRATION

1. **Filing in the wrong county.** Probate must be filed in the county of the decedent's domicile, not the county where the decedent died or where the assets are located.

2. **Failing to publish Notice to Creditors promptly.** The four-month creditor claims period does not begin until publication. Delayed publication extends the administration timeline.

3. **Distributing before the claims period expires.** The personal representative is personally liable for claims that cannot be paid because assets were prematurely distributed.

4. **Failing to identify and notify known creditors.** Publication is not sufficient for known creditors — actual notice by mail is required (Minn. Stat. § 524.3-801(b)). Known creditors who do not receive actual notice may have up to one year to present claims.

5. **Personal representative self-dealing.** Purchasing estate assets, borrowing from the estate, or otherwise engaging in transactions that create a conflict of interest. This is a breach of fiduciary duty and can result in personal liability, surcharge, and removal.

6. **Failing to file tax returns.** The personal representative is personally liable for taxes that are not paid due to distributions made before taxes were satisfied. File all required returns (final 1040, Form 1041, Form 706, Minnesota M706) timely.

7. **Ignoring the surviving spouse's rights.** The homestead allowance, exempt property, family maintenance allowance, and elective share are powerful protections that cannot be overridden by the will (except by a valid prenuptial or postnuptial agreement). Failing to inform the surviving spouse of these rights is a disservice to the spouse and may expose the personal representative and the attorney to liability.

8. **Treating informal probate as "no probate."** Even in informal, unsupervised probate, the personal representative has all of the duties and responsibilities of a fiduciary. The lack of court supervision does not mean the lack of accountability.
