## PURPOSE

This checklist provides a systematic, step-by-step process for reviewing any contract before execution. It is designed to be used by OrchestratorAI's Managing Member, contract administrators, and external counsel when evaluating agreements that OrchestratorAI will enter into -- whether as the drafting party or when reviewing contracts presented by counterparties.

Every contract must complete this review process before execution. The depth of review scales with the contract's value and risk level, as described in the Risk Classification section below.

---

## RISK CLASSIFICATION

Before beginning the review, classify the contract:

### Tier 1: Standard Risk
- Annual value under $25,000
- Standard template with minimal customization
- Existing counterparty with positive history
- Standard terms, no unusual provisions
- **Review requirement:** Self-review + Managing Member sign-off

### Tier 2: Elevated Risk
- Annual value $25,000 - $100,000
- Customized terms or counterparty-drafted agreement
- New counterparty or new engagement type
- Involves personal data processing or regulated activities
- **Review requirement:** Full checklist + Managing Member + External counsel (recommended)

### Tier 3: High Risk
- Annual value over $100,000
- Counterparty-drafted with non-standard terms
- International counterparty or cross-border obligations
- Involves high-value IP, significant liability exposure, or regulatory complexity
- **Review requirement:** Full checklist + Managing Member + External counsel (required)

## SECTION 1: PRELIMINARY REVIEW

### 1.1. Document Completeness

- [ ] **All pages present.** Verify page count and pagination. No pages missing or out of order.
- [ ] **All exhibits and schedules attached.** Every exhibit, schedule, annex, and attachment referenced in the body is physically attached and complete.
- [ ] **All blanks filled in.** No empty brackets, placeholder text ("[TBD]", "[INSERT]"), or unfilled fields.
- [ ] **Correct version.** Confirm this is the most recent version that incorporates all agreed-upon negotiation changes.
- [ ] **No unauthorized changes.** If this is a returning redline, verify no changes were made outside of tracked changes. Run a document comparison against the prior version.
- [ ] **Formatting consistent.** Section numbering sequential, cross-references correct, defined terms consistent.

**Notes:** _______________________________________________

### 1.2. Party Identification

- [ ] **OrchestratorAI correctly identified.** Full legal name: "Orchestrator AI LLC" -- verify spelling, capitalization, and entity type.
- [ ] **Jurisdiction of formation stated.** Florida limited liability company.
- [ ] **Principal place of business stated.** Minneapolis/St. Paul, Minnesota.
- [ ] **Counterparty correctly identified.** Full legal name matches their state registration. Verify via Secretary of State records.
- [ ] **Counterparty entity type confirmed.** Corporation, LLC, partnership, sole proprietorship, individual, etc.
- [ ] **Counterparty jurisdiction confirmed.** State of formation and principal place of business.
- [ ] **Signatory authority verified.** Confirm the person signing has authority to bind the counterparty (officer, authorized member, etc.).

## SECTION 2: BUSINESS TERMS

### 2.1. Scope of Services/License

- [ ] **Scope clearly defined.** Services, deliverables, or license rights are described with sufficient specificity to avoid ambiguity.
- [ ] **Out-of-scope items identified.** What is NOT included is explicitly stated.
- [ ] **Acceptance criteria specified.** For deliverable-based engagements, acceptance criteria and process are defined.
- [ ] **Performance standards stated.** Service levels, quality standards, or conformance requirements are set.
- [ ] **Scope matches business discussions.** The written scope aligns with what was discussed and agreed upon verbally.

**Risk flags:**
- Vague scope language that could be interpreted broadly ("all services reasonably requested")
- Missing acceptance process (creates disputes about completion)
- Performance standards that are unmeasurable or unrealistic

**Notes:** _______________________________________________

### 2.2. Financial Terms

- [ ] **Pricing clearly stated.** All fees, rates, and charges are unambiguous. No hidden fees.
- [ ] **Pricing consistent with approved rates.** Hourly rates align with standard rate card ($80-100/hr for training, $40-75/hr for contractors, etc.).
- [ ] **Payment terms specified.** Invoice timing, payment due dates, and payment methods are defined.
- [ ] **Late payment consequences.** Interest rate on late payments is stated and reasonable (standard: 1.5%/month).
- [ ] **Expense reimbursement rules.** If expenses are reimbursable, pre-approval requirements, limits, and documentation requirements are specified.
- [ ] **Taxes addressed.** Tax responsibility is allocated (standard: each party responsible for own income taxes; client responsible for sales/use tax).
- [ ] **Fee escalation/adjustment provisions.** If the agreement spans multiple years, rate increase provisions are reasonable (standard cap: 5-7% annually).
- [ ] **Not-to-exceed amounts.** For T&M engagements, NTE caps are specified.
- [ ] **Currency specified.** USD (standard for all OrchestratorAI agreements).

**Risk flags:**
- "Most favored nation" pricing clauses (obligate you to match lower prices given to others)
- Unbounded T&M without NTE caps
- Payment terms longer than Net 45
- Audit rights on pricing (may be burdensome)

**Notes:** _______________________________________________

### 2.3. Timeline and Milestones

- [ ] **Start date specified.** Clear effective date or commencement trigger.
- [ ] **Milestones with dates.** Key milestones have target or required dates.
- [ ] **Dependencies identified.** Client responsibilities that are prerequisites to Provider milestones are stated.
- [ ] **Delay consequences addressed.** What happens when milestones slip (client-caused vs. provider-caused).
- [ ] **Force majeure covers realistic risks.** Pandemics, supply chain disruptions, semiconductor shortages, etc.

## SECTION 3: INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY

### 3.1. IP Ownership

- [ ] **OrchestratorAI platform IP protected.** The agreement does not transfer or encumber OrchestratorAI's platform IP.
- [ ] **Work product ownership clear.** For deliverable-based engagements, who owns what is unambiguous.
- [ ] **"Work made for hire" clause present.** For contractor agreements, WFH + assignment belt-and-suspenders approach used.
- [ ] **Assignment language comprehensive.** Covers all IP types: patents, copyrights, trade secrets, trademarks.
- [ ] **Moral rights waiver included.** For contractor agreements.
- [ ] **Prior inventions disclosed.** Contractor agreements include Prior Inventions exhibit.
- [ ] **License-back provisions.** If OrchestratorAI assigns IP, verify if a license-back is needed for continued use of the methodology.
- [ ] **Open source provisions.** Restrictions on incorporating GPL/AGPL code. Approval process for OSS.
- [ ] **No third-party IP infringement warranties reasonable.** Standard non-infringement representations included.
- [ ] **Client data ownership preserved.** In license agreements, client clearly owns their data.

**Risk flags:**
- Counterparty claiming ownership of custom agents or configurations built on OrchestratorAI platform
- Broad IP assignment clauses in counterparty-drafted agreements that could sweep in platform IP
- Missing open-source compliance provisions (risk of copyleft contamination)
- Joint ownership provisions (creates complex co-ownership issues)

**Notes:** _______________________________________________

### 3.2. IP Protection Mechanisms

- [ ] **Source code not delivered.** License agreements provide object code only (unless source code escrow is agreed).
- [ ] **Reverse engineering prohibited.** Standard restriction on decompilation, disassembly, reverse engineering.
- [ ] **Platform use restrictions adequate.** No service bureau use, no competitive development, no benchmarking without consent.
- [ ] **Feedback clause included.** If client provides feedback, Company can use it freely.

## SECTION 4: CONFIDENTIALITY

- [ ] **Definition of Confidential Information appropriate.** Broad enough to cover platform code, algorithms, business information.
- [ ] **Exclusions standard.** Public information, prior knowledge, independent development, third-party disclosure.
- [ ] **Permitted disclosures limited.** Only to those with need-to-know, bound by similar obligations.
- [ ] **Duration adequate.** Minimum 3-5 years for general CI; indefinite for trade secrets.
- [ ] **Return/destruction obligations clear.** Post-termination return/destruction with certification.
- [ ] **Mutual vs. one-way.** Appropriate for the relationship (mutual for partnerships; one-way or mutual for consulting, depending on CI flow).
- [ ] **Compelled disclosure procedure.** Notice and cooperation requirements for legally compelled disclosure.

**Risk flags:**
- Short confidentiality duration (less than 2 years)
- Broad exclusion for "residual knowledge" (allows counterparty to use memorized CI)
- Missing return/destruction provisions
- No requirement to notify of compelled disclosure

**Notes:** _______________________________________________

## SECTION 5: DATA PROTECTION AND PRIVACY

- [ ] **Personal data processing identified.** Agreement acknowledges whether personal data will be processed.
- [ ] **DPA attached or referenced.** If personal data is involved, a Data Processing Agreement is in place.
- [ ] **Data locality provisions adequate.** For local deployments, data stays on client hardware.
- [ ] **Breach notification obligations reasonable.** 48-72 hour notification timeline (standard).
- [ ] **Sub-processor approval process included.** Notice and objection rights for sub-processor changes.
- [ ] **Data subject rights accommodated.** Assistance obligations for access, deletion, portability requests.
- [ ] **Data retention and deletion provisions.** Post-termination data handling is specified.
- [ ] **CCPA/CPRA compliance.** "Service provider" obligations and certifications included (for California data).
- [ ] **GDPR compliance.** SCCs attached for international transfers (if applicable).
- [ ] **AI-specific provisions.** If AI processes personal data, transparency and fairness obligations addressed.

**Risk flags:**
- No DPA when personal data is clearly involved
- Unreasonably short breach notification timeline (under 24 hours)
- Broad rights to use client data for "service improvement" without clear anonymization
- Missing data deletion provisions post-termination

## SECTION 6: LIABILITY AND RISK ALLOCATION

### 6.1. Limitation of Liability

- [ ] **Consequential damages excluded.** Standard mutual exclusion of indirect, incidental, special, consequential, and punitive damages.
- [ ] **Liability cap reasonable.** Standard: 12 months' fees paid/payable. Verify the cap is sufficient for the risk profile.
- [ ] **Exceptions to cap identified.** Typical exceptions: IP infringement, confidentiality breach, willful misconduct. Ensure exceptions are mutual and reasonable.
- [ ] **"Fundamental breach" carve-outs.** Some counterparties attempt to exclude fundamental breach from the LoL. This may void the entire limitation.
- [ ] **Essential basis language included.** Both parties acknowledge the LoL is fundamental to the pricing.

**Risk flags:**
- Unlimited liability for any obligation (even indemnification should have a cap)
- Asymmetric liability caps (counterparty gets higher protection than OrchestratorAI)
- LoL so low it does not meaningfully protect against realistic loss scenarios
- Exclusion of "direct damages" (makes the LoL almost meaningless)

**Notes:** _______________________________________________

### 6.2. Indemnification

- [ ] **Indemnification obligations mutual.** Both parties indemnify for their own breaches and negligence.
- [ ] **IP indemnification by OrchestratorAI.** Standard: OrchestratorAI indemnifies for platform IP infringement when used as authorized.
- [ ] **IP indemnification remedies.** Obtain right / modify / refund -- standard three-option remedy.
- [ ] **Procedure specified.** Notice, cooperation, sole control of defense.
- [ ] **Indemnification cap.** Verify whether indemnification is subject to the general liability cap or uncapped.

**Risk flags:**
- Uncapped indemnification for OrchestratorAI with no corresponding protection
- Broad indemnification triggers ("any claim arising from or related to the agreement")
- Missing procedural requirements (failure to follow procedure should limit indemnitor's obligations)

**Notes:** _______________________________________________

### 6.3. Insurance

- [ ] **Insurance requirements reasonable.** If the agreement requires insurance, verify coverage types and limits are obtainable and appropriate.
- [ ] **Certificate of insurance required.** Standard requirement for both parties (typical: $1M general liability, $1M professional liability/E&O, $1M cyber liability).
- [ ] **Additional insured status.** If requested, verify with insurance broker that it can be provided.

## SECTION 7: TERM AND TERMINATION

- [ ] **Term clearly stated.** Initial term, renewal terms, and total potential duration are clear.
- [ ] **Auto-renewal provisions.** Verify whether the agreement auto-renews and what the non-renewal notice period is. Set calendar reminders.
- [ ] **Termination for convenience.** Both parties have reasonable termination-for-convenience rights with appropriate notice periods.
- [ ] **Termination for cause.** Material breach with cure period. Cure period is reasonable (standard: 15-30 days).
- [ ] **Insolvency/bankruptcy termination.** Right to terminate if counterparty becomes insolvent.
- [ ] **Post-termination obligations clear.** Payment, data return/export, confidential information destruction, access revocation, IP return.
- [ ] **Survival clause.** Key provisions (IP, confidentiality, liability, indemnification) explicitly survive termination.
- [ ] **No lock-in concerns.** OrchestratorAI is not locked into an unfavorable long-term commitment without exit options.

**Risk flags:**
- Long initial terms with no early termination right
- Short cure periods (less than 10 days)
- Missing post-termination data export rights
- Automatic renewal with long non-renewal notice periods (creates trap)
- Penalty fees for early termination that exceed actual damages

## SECTION 8: GOVERNING LAW AND DISPUTES

- [ ] **Governing law: Florida.** Standard for all OrchestratorAI agreements (with Minnesota provisions where required).
- [ ] **Dispute resolution process.** Negotiation -> Mediation -> Arbitration (standard sequence).
- [ ] **Venue: Hennepin County, Minnesota.** Standard for mediation and arbitration.
- [ ] **Arbitration rules specified.** AAA Commercial Arbitration Rules (standard).
- [ ] **Injunctive relief carve-out.** Right to seek emergency injunctive relief in court for IP/confidentiality breaches.
- [ ] **Attorneys' fees provision.** Prevailing party entitled to reasonable fees (standard).
- [ ] **Class action waiver.** For consumer-facing agreements (Divinr.ai).

**Risk flags:**
- Counterparty's home jurisdiction for governing law (unfamiliar courts, unfavorable law)
- Mandatory litigation in distant venue (cost and inconvenience)
- Jury trial waiver missing (may want to include for commercial agreements)
- No injunctive relief carve-out (forced to arbitrate even emergency IP matters)

## SECTION 9: GENERAL PROVISIONS (BOILERPLATE)

Do not skip the "boilerplate." These provisions have legal significance:

- [ ] **Entire agreement clause.** Supersedes all prior agreements and understandings.
- [ ] **Amendment requirement.** Written amendment signed by both parties.
- [ ] **Waiver clause.** No waiver of any right unless in writing. No implied waivers.
- [ ] **Severability.** Invalid provisions severed; remainder survives.
- [ ] **Assignment.** OrchestratorAI retains right to assign to successor; counterparty assignment restricted.
- [ ] **Notice provisions.** Acceptable methods (email, mail, courier), addresses, effectiveness triggers.
- [ ] **Force majeure.** Covers relevant risks (pandemic, natural disaster, government orders, supply chain, internet outages).
- [ ] **Counterparts.** Electronic signatures accepted.
- [ ] **Independent contractor status.** For service agreements, independent contractor status is clear (not employer-employee).
- [ ] **Non-exclusivity.** Agreement does not create exclusive relationship (unless intentional).
- [ ] **Headings.** Headings for convenience, not interpretation.
- [ ] **No third-party beneficiaries.** Unless intentional.

## SECTION 10: AI-SPECIFIC PROVISIONS

For agreements involving AI products and services, verify these additional items:

- [ ] **AI output disclaimers.** No warranty on accuracy, completeness, or reliability of AI-generated outputs.
- [ ] **No professional advice disclaimer.** AI outputs are not legal, financial, medical, or other professional advice (especially critical for Divinr.ai).
- [ ] **Model limitations acknowledged.** Counterparty understands AI models may produce incorrect or biased outputs.
- [ ] **Training data provisions.** Agreement addresses whether client data may be used to train or improve models. Standard: no training on client data without consent.
- [ ] **Responsible AI use.** Provisions addressing prohibited uses (e.g., autonomous weapons, mass surveillance, discrimination).
- [ ] **Transparency obligations.** Appropriate disclosure that AI is being used, particularly for client-facing applications.
- [ ] **Human oversight provisions.** For high-stakes use cases, human review of AI outputs is required.
