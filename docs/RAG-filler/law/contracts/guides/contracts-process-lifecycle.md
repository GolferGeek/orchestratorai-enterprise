## 1. INTRODUCTION AND PURPOSE

### 1.1. Purpose of This Guide

This guide establishes the standard contract management process for Orchestrator AI LLC ("OrchestratorAI" or the "Company"). It governs the entire lifecycle of every contractual agreement the Company enters into, from initial opportunity identification through execution, management, renewal, and archival.

As a single-member LLC operating in the AI technology space, OrchestratorAI enters into a variety of contractual relationships, including: independent contractor agreements with 1099 developers and interns; software license agreements for OrchestratorAI platform deployments; SaaS subscription agreements for Divinr.ai; consulting and training engagements; partnership agreements; hardware procurement; data processing agreements; and referral partner arrangements.

### 1.2. Why Contract Management Matters

Effective contract management is critical for several reasons:

**Revenue Protection.** With project-based platform deployments generating $20,000+/year in recurring revenue and training services billed at $80-100/hour, each contract represents significant financial commitment. Poorly managed contracts lead to revenue leakage, disputes, and lost renewals.

**Intellectual Property Security.** The OrchestratorAI platform and Divinr.ai represent the Company's core assets. Every contract must properly protect source code, algorithms, training data, and proprietary methodologies through IP assignment clauses, confidentiality provisions, and access restrictions.

**Regulatory Compliance.** The Company handles client data, deploys AI systems, and processes market predictions. Contracts must address GDPR, CCPA/CPRA, MCDPA, securities regulations (for Divinr.ai), and export controls.

**Operational Clarity.** Clear contracts prevent disputes by establishing mutual expectations regarding scope, deliverables, timelines, payment, and responsibilities.

### 1.3. Scope

This guide applies to all agreements to which OrchestratorAI is a party, including but not limited to:

- Independent Contractor Agreements
- Master Services Agreements and Statements of Work
- Software License Agreements
- SaaS Subscription Agreements
- Consulting and Training Agreements
- Partnership Agreements
- Referral Partner Agreements
- Hardware Purchase Agreements
- Data Processing Agreements
- Non-Disclosure Agreements
- Letters of Intent
- Amendments and Addenda

## 3. PHASE 1: PRE-CONTRACT (ORIGINATION AND PLANNING)

### 3.1. Trigger Events

Contracts originate from the following events:

| Trigger | Typical Contract Type |
|---------|----------------------|
| New client opportunity from sales pipeline | MSA + SOW + License Agreement |
| Inbound lead from referral partner | MSA + SOW + License Agreement |
| New developer/intern onboarding | Independent Contractor Agreement |
| Training engagement request | Consulting Services Agreement |
| Divinr.ai signup | SaaS Subscription Agreement (auto) |
| Partnership discussion | Partnership Agreement |
| New referral partner | Referral Partner Agreement |
| Client requests data processing terms | Data Processing Agreement |
| Hardware need identified for deployment | Hardware Purchase Agreement |
| Prospect requests formal intent | Letter of Intent |

### 3.2. Opportunity Assessment

Before initiating a contract, the following assessment must be completed:

**Business Viability:**
- Is the opportunity aligned with OrchestratorAI's product offerings and capabilities?
- What is the estimated contract value (initial and recurring)?
- What resources will be required (personnel, hardware, time)?
- What is the expected margin?
- Are there conflicts with existing obligations?

**Risk Assessment:**
- What is the counterparty's creditworthiness and reputation?
- Are there jurisdictional issues (international clients, specific state requirements)?
- Are there IP risks (client in competing space, open-source concerns)?
- Are there data privacy considerations (personal data, regulated industries)?
- Are there technical risks (untested integrations, custom requirements)?

**Strategic Fit:**
- Does this engagement create a valuable reference customer?
- Does it advance the Company's market position?
- Is there potential for expansion (upsell, cross-sell)?

### 3.3. Pre-Contract Checklist

Before moving to the drafting phase:

- [ ] Opportunity assessment completed
- [ ] Counterparty basic due diligence conducted (business registration, online presence, references)
- [ ] Preliminary scope and pricing discussed with counterparty
- [ ] Contract type(s) identified
- [ ] NDA executed (if needed before sharing proprietary information)
- [ ] Internal resource availability confirmed
- [ ] Any special terms or non-standard requirements identified
- [ ] Approval to proceed obtained from Managing Member

### 3.4. Quality Gate: Authorization to Draft

The Managing Member must authorize the commencement of drafting based on the completed opportunity assessment and pre-contract checklist.

## 4. PHASE 2: DRAFTING

### 4.1. Template Selection

OrchestratorAI maintains a library of standard contract templates. The appropriate template(s) should be selected based on the engagement type:

| Engagement Type | Primary Template | Supporting Templates |
|-----------------|------------------|----------------------|
| Platform deployment | Software License Agreement | MSA, SOW, DPA, NDA |
| Training engagement | Consulting Services Agreement | NDA |
| Divinr.ai subscription | SaaS Subscription Agreement | (auto-generated) |
| Developer onboarding | Independent Contractor Agreement | NDA |
| Intern program | Partnership Agreement + IC Agreement | NDA |
| Hardware procurement | Hardware Purchase Agreement | (standalone or with License) |
| Referral partner | Referral Partner Agreement | NDA |
| General services | MSA + SOW | NDA, DPA |

### 4.2. Drafting Process

**Step 1: Copy the Template.** Never edit the master template directly. Copy the template to a working draft folder and assign a contract number using the standard numbering format: OAI-[TYPE]-[YEAR]-[###].

**Step 2: Populate Standard Fields.** Fill in all bracketed placeholders with engagement-specific information:
- Party names and addresses
- Effective dates
- Agreement reference numbers
- Pricing and payment terms
- Scope descriptions
- Schedule and timeline information
- Contact information for notices

**Step 3: Customize Business Terms.** Adapt the template provisions to reflect the specific engagement:
- Adjust scope of services/license
- Set appropriate pricing tier
- Configure support levels
- Specify deliverables and milestones
- Set applicable compliance requirements (GDPR, CCPA, industry-specific)
- Tailor restrictive covenants to the engagement

**Step 4: Prepare Exhibits and Schedules.** Complete all exhibits, schedules, and attachments. Exhibits are substantive extensions of the agreement and must be as carefully drafted as the main body.

**Step 5: Cross-Reference Check.** Verify that all internal references (section numbers, exhibit references, defined terms) are correct and consistent.

### 4.3. Drafting Standards

**Defined Terms:** All key terms must be defined in Section 1 (Definitions) or upon first use. Defined terms are capitalized throughout the agreement.

**Plain Language:** While maintaining legal precision, avoid unnecessarily complex language. The agreement should be understandable to business stakeholders, not just attorneys.

**Consistency:** Use consistent terminology throughout. If a concept is called "Services" in one section, do not switch to "Work" or "Tasks" in another.

**Governing Law:** Standard governing law for all OrchestratorAI agreements is Florida, with Minnesota provisions where required for Services performed in Minnesota.

**Company Name:** Always use "Orchestrator AI LLC" (with space between "Orchestrator" and "AI"). The short form "OrchestratorAI" (no space) is acceptable as a defined term after first use.

### 4.4. Quality Gate: Draft Complete

- [ ] All template placeholders replaced with engagement-specific information
- [ ] All exhibits and schedules completed
- [ ] Cross-references verified
- [ ] Defined terms consistent
- [ ] Pricing and payment terms confirmed with Managing Member
- [ ] Draft saved in contract management system with proper naming

## 5. PHASE 3: INTERNAL REVIEW AND APPROVAL

### 5.1. Review Workflow

All contracts must undergo internal review before being sent to the counterparty. The review workflow depends on the contract value and type:

**Standard Review (contracts under $25,000 annual value):**
1. Self-review by drafter using the Contract Review Checklist
2. Managing Member review and approval

**Enhanced Review (contracts $25,000 - $100,000 annual value):**
1. Self-review by drafter
2. Managing Member business terms review
3. External legal counsel review (recommended)

**Full Review (contracts over $100,000 annual value or non-standard terms):**
1. Self-review by drafter
2. Managing Member business terms review
3. External legal counsel full review (required)
4. Insurance broker review of indemnification and liability provisions (recommended)

### 5.2. Review Checklist Summary

Each reviewer should verify the following categories (see separate Contract Review Checklist for detailed line items):

- **Parties and Authority:** Correct legal names, entity types, authority to contract
- **Scope and Services:** Clear, complete, consistent with discussions
- **Financial Terms:** Pricing, payment, taxes, expenses correctly specified
- **Intellectual Property:** IP ownership, assignment, licensing properly addressed
- **Confidentiality:** Obligations appropriate for the engagement
- **Data Privacy:** DPA attached or referenced if personal data involved
- **Liability:** Limitation of liability, indemnification, insurance provisions balanced
- **Term and Termination:** Duration, renewal, termination triggers, post-termination obligations clear
- **Governing Law and Disputes:** Standard provisions in place
- **Compliance:** Regulatory requirements addressed
- **Boilerplate:** Standard general provisions included and correct

### 5.3. Approval Authority

| Contract Type | Approval Authority |
|---------------|--------------------|
| All contracts | Managing Member |
| Contracts over $100K annual value | Managing Member + external counsel confirmation |
| Non-standard liability terms | Managing Member + external counsel confirmation |
| International agreements | Managing Member + external counsel confirmation |

### 5.4. Quality Gate: Approved for Distribution

- [ ] All reviews completed
- [ ] All comments and issues resolved
- [ ] Managing Member written approval obtained
- [ ] External counsel sign-off obtained (if required)
- [ ] Clean version prepared for counterparty

## 6. PHASE 4: NEGOTIATION

### 6.1. Distribution to Counterparty

Send the clean draft to the counterparty's designated contact via email, with a cover message that:
- Identifies the enclosed documents
- Highlights key terms for the counterparty's attention
- Requests a timeline for review
- Provides a contact for questions
- Notes any deadline or expiration (e.g., for LOIs or pricing holds)

### 6.2. Negotiation Principles

**Know Your Walk-Away Points.** Before negotiation begins, identify:
- Must-have terms (non-negotiable)
- Important terms (prefer to keep, but have alternatives)
- Nice-to-have terms (willing to concede)
- Absolute deal-breakers

**Standard Non-Negotiable Terms for OrchestratorAI:**
- IP assignment for contractor-created work product
- Confidentiality and trade secret protection
- Governing law (Florida/Minnesota)
- AI output disclaimer (for Divinr.ai)
- Data ownership (client owns their data)
- Right to terminate for cause

**Commonly Negotiated Terms:**
- Limitation of liability caps and exclusions
- Indemnification scope and caps
- Non-compete scope and duration
- Payment terms and timing
- Warranty period and remedies
- Termination notice periods
- Insurance requirements

### 6.3. Redline Management

**Version Control.** Maintain strict version control during negotiations:
- Label each version: "v1" (initial draft), "v2" (counterparty redline), "v3" (OrchestratorAI response), etc.
- Always work from the most recent version
- Track changes in Word or use a dedicated contract comparison tool
- Keep a clean copy and a redline copy of each version

**Redline Response Protocol:**
1. Receive counterparty's redline
2. Review all changes systematically (do not skim)
3. Categorize each change: Accept / Reject / Counter-propose / Needs discussion
4. Draft response redline with comments explaining rejections and counter-proposals
5. Internal review of response before sending (repeat Phase 3 review for material changes)
6. Send response with cover note summarizing key positions

### 6.4. Escalation

If negotiations stall on material terms, escalate to:
1. Business-level discussion between principals
2. External counsel-to-counsel discussion
3. Managing Member decision to accept, counter, or walk away

### 6.5. Quality Gate: Negotiation Complete

- [ ] All terms agreed upon
- [ ] Final clean version prepared incorporating all agreed changes
- [ ] Both parties have confirmed the final version is acceptable
- [ ] Any side letters or supplemental agreements finalized
- [ ] Managing Member final approval of negotiated terms

## 7. PHASE 5: EXECUTION

### 7.1. Pre-Execution Verification

Before signatures:
- [ ] Verify final version matches the last agreed-upon redline (no unauthorized changes)
- [ ] Confirm all exhibits, schedules, and attachments are included and complete
- [ ] Verify all blanks are filled in (dates, amounts, names)
- [ ] Confirm signatory authority (is the person signing authorized to bind the entity?)
- [ ] For entities: confirm entity name matches state registration
- [ ] Confirm all conditions precedent are satisfied (e.g., NDA signed, insurance certificates received)

### 7.2. Signature Process

**Electronic Signatures.** OrchestratorAI accepts electronic signatures via DocuSign, Adobe Sign, or similar platforms. Electronic signatures are legally valid under the federal ESIGN Act (15 U.S.C. Section 7001 et seq.) and the Uniform Electronic Transactions Act (UETA).

**Wet Signatures.** If the counterparty requires wet ink signatures, prepare two original copies. Both parties sign both copies, and each retains one original.

**Signature Authority.** Only the Managing Member (or designee with written delegation) may execute agreements on behalf of OrchestratorAI.

### 7.3. Post-Execution Processing

After full execution:

1. **Confirm Execution.** Verify that all required signatures have been obtained and the agreement is fully executed.

2. **Distribute Copies.** Send a fully executed copy to: (a) the counterparty; (b) internal stakeholders who need to perform under the agreement; and (c) external counsel (if they participated in the negotiation).

3. **Record in Contract Register.** Enter the agreement into the Company's contract register with the following information:
   - Contract number and type
   - Counterparty name
   - Effective date and term
   - Renewal dates and notice deadlines
   - Key financial terms
   - Key obligations and milestones
   - Assigned internal owner

4. **Set Calendar Reminders.** Set reminders for:
   - Payment milestones
   - Deliverable deadlines
   - Renewal/non-renewal notice deadlines (set reminder well in advance of the deadline)
   - Annual review dates
   - Expiration dates

5. **Secure Storage.** Store the fully executed agreement in the Company's secure document management system.

### 7.4. Quality Gate: Execution Complete

- [ ] Fully executed by all parties
- [ ] Copies distributed
- [ ] Contract register updated
- [ ] Calendar reminders set
- [ ] Securely stored
- [ ] Kickoff meeting scheduled (for project-based engagements)

## 8. PHASE 6: ACTIVE CONTRACT MANAGEMENT

### 8.1. Performance Monitoring

During the active life of the agreement:

**Obligation Tracking.** Monitor both parties' compliance with contractual obligations:
- Deliverable timelines and quality
- Payment schedules (incoming and outgoing)
- Reporting obligations
- Support response times
- Data protection compliance

**Issue Management.** When issues arise:
1. Document the issue with specifics (what, when, impact, relevant contract section)
2. Raise the issue with the counterparty promptly and in writing
3. Refer to the contract's dispute resolution provisions
4. Maintain a log of all issues and resolutions

**Change Management.** When changes to the agreement are needed:
1. Use the formal Change Order process (for SOWs) or Amendment process
2. Never rely on verbal agreements to modify contractual terms
3. Document all changes in writing, signed by both parties
4. Update the contract register to reflect changes

### 8.2. Financial Management

**Invoice Tracking.** For agreements where OrchestratorAI invoices:
- Issue invoices per the agreed schedule
- Track payment receipts and aging
- Follow up on overdue payments per the agreement's terms
- Exercise contractual remedies for persistent non-payment (late fees, suspension, termination)

**Expense Management.** For agreements with expense provisions:
- Track pre-approval requirements
- Ensure expenses are within agreed limits
- Invoice expenses with proper documentation

### 8.3. Compliance Monitoring

**Data Protection.** For agreements involving personal data:
- Verify DPA obligations are being met
- Respond to data subject requests per agreed timelines
- Report and manage any data breaches per the notification requirements
- Maintain records of processing activities

**Regulatory Changes.** Monitor for changes in applicable law that may affect contractual obligations:
- New data protection regulations
- Changes to employment/contractor classification rules
- Securities regulation changes (for Divinr.ai)
- Export control changes

### 8.4. Periodic Reviews

Conduct periodic reviews of active contracts:

| Contract Type | Review Frequency |
|---------------|------------------|
| License Agreements | Annually (90 days before renewal) |
| Contractor Agreements | Semi-annually |
| Partnership Agreements | Quarterly |
| SaaS Subscriptions | Annually |
| MSAs with active SOWs | Monthly (with project reviews) |

### 8.5. Quality Gate: Active Management

Continuous monitoring with documented quarterly assessments.

## 9. PHASE 7: RENEWAL AND RENEGOTIATION

### 9.1. Renewal Timeline

```
90 days before renewal: Review contract performance and value
60 days before renewal: Decision to renew, renegotiate, or non-renew
45 days before renewal: If renegotiating, begin discussions
30 days before renewal: Non-renewal notice deadline (typical)
Renewal date: Auto-renew if no action taken
```

### 9.2. Renewal Decision Framework

**Renew As-Is:** The contract is performing well, terms are market-appropriate, and the relationship is positive.

**Renew with Modifications:** The contract needs updated terms (pricing, scope, support levels). Use the Amendment template to document changes.

**Renegotiate:** Material changes are needed. Treat as a new negotiation while preserving the existing relationship.

**Non-Renew:** The contract is no longer serving the Company's interests. Issue the non-renewal notice per the agreement's requirements using the Termination Letter template.

### 9.3. Renewal Considerations

- Has the counterparty met its obligations?
- Is the pricing still appropriate for the value delivered?
- Have market conditions changed (competitor pricing, new technologies)?
- Are there opportunities to upsell (upgrade license tier, add services)?
- Are there outstanding issues that need resolution?
- Have regulatory requirements changed, necessitating contract updates?

### 9.4. Quality Gate: Renewal Decision Made

- [ ] Contract performance reviewed
- [ ] Financial analysis completed
- [ ] Decision documented (renew / modify / renegotiate / non-renew)
- [ ] Action taken within required notice periods
- [ ] Contract register updated

## 10. PHASE 8: TERMINATION AND ARCHIVAL

### 10.1. Termination Types

| Type | Process |
|------|---------|
| Expiration (end of term, no renewal) | Complete post-termination checklist |
| Non-renewal | Issue non-renewal notice, then post-termination checklist |
| Termination for convenience | Issue termination notice, then post-termination checklist |
| Termination for cause | Document breach, issue cure notice (if applicable), issue termination notice |

### 10.2. Post-Termination Checklist

- [ ] Termination notice properly issued (correct method, timing, content)
- [ ] Acknowledgment of receipt obtained (or proof of delivery)
- [ ] Outstanding invoices issued and collected
- [ ] Refunds processed (if applicable)
- [ ] Confidential information returned/destroyed (both directions)
- [ ] Destruction certifications exchanged
- [ ] System access revoked
- [ ] API keys and credentials deactivated
- [ ] Company property retrieved
- [ ] Data export completed (if applicable)
- [ ] Platform access terminated
- [ ] Transition assistance provided (if applicable)
- [ ] Surviving obligations documented and calendared
- [ ] Final financial reconciliation completed
- [ ] Contract register updated with termination date and reason
- [ ] Lessons learned documented

### 10.3. Archival

**Retention Requirements:**
- Executed contracts: minimum seven (7) years from termination
- Financial records: minimum seven (7) years
- Tax-related documents (1099s, W-9s): minimum seven (7) years
- Correspondence and negotiation records: minimum three (3) years from termination
- Data processing records: as required by applicable data protection law

**Archival Process:**
1. Transfer the complete contract file to the archive repository
2. Ensure the archive includes: executed agreement, all amendments, key correspondence, invoices, deliverable records, and termination documentation
3. Apply retention labels per the retention schedule
4. Remove from active contract monitoring
5. Set calendar reminder for retention period expiration and destruction


## 14. COMPLIANCE AND RISK MANAGEMENT

### 14.1. Regular Compliance Tasks

| Task | Frequency | Responsible |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| Contract register audit | Quarterly | Contract Administrator |
| Renewal deadline review | Monthly | Contract Administrator |
| Outstanding invoice review | Bi-weekly | Finance |
| Insurance coverage verification | Annually | Managing Member |
| Template update review | Semi-annually | Managing Member + Counsel |
| Regulatory change assessment | Quarterly | Managing Member + Counsel |

### 14.2. Risk Mitigation

**Concentration Risk.** Monitor revenue concentration across contracts. No single client should represent more than 40% of total revenue.

**IP Risk.** Ensure all contractor and partner agreements include proper IP assignment and confidentiality provisions. Conduct periodic audits of open-source component usage.

**Regulatory Risk.** Maintain awareness of evolving AI regulations, data protection laws, and securities regulations (for Divinr.ai). Update templates as needed.

**Financial Risk.** Maintain adequate cash reserves to cover contractual obligations. Review liability exposure across all active contracts annually.
