# Enforcement and Penalties

## Regulatory Basis
45 CFR Part 160, Subpart D (45 CFR 160.400-160.426) establishes the enforcement framework, including investigation procedures, civil monetary penalties, and hearing procedures. Criminal penalties are established under 42 U.S.C. 1320d-6. The HITECH Act [HIPAA-HITECH] substantially increased penalty amounts and expanded enforcement authority.

## Enforcement Authority

### Office for Civil Rights (OCR)
The HHS Office for Civil Rights is the primary enforcement agency for HIPAA Privacy, Security, and Breach Notification Rules.
- Investigates complaints filed by individuals.
- Conducts compliance reviews and audits.
- Provides technical assistance and guidance.
- Imposes civil monetary penalties (CMPs) or enters into resolution agreements.

### Department of Justice (DOJ)
The DOJ handles criminal enforcement of HIPAA violations referred by HHS.

### State Attorneys General
Under HITECH [HIPAA-HITECH], state attorneys general may bring civil actions on behalf of state residents for HIPAA violations. This is a significant expansion of enforcement authority beyond the federal level.

## Civil Monetary Penalties — 45 CFR 160.404

### Four-Tier Penalty Structure (as amended by HITECH)

#### Tier 1: Did Not Know (and would not have known by exercising reasonable diligence)
- **Per violation:** $100 minimum to $50,000 maximum (adjusted for inflation)
- **Annual maximum:** $25,000 for all identical violations in a calendar year (adjusted for inflation)
- Applies when the covered entity or business associate was unaware of the violation and could not have known through reasonable diligence.

#### Tier 2: Reasonable Cause (not willful neglect)
- **Per violation:** $1,000 minimum to $50,000 maximum (adjusted for inflation)
- **Annual maximum:** $100,000 for all identical violations in a calendar year (adjusted for inflation)
- Applies when the violation was due to reasonable cause and not willful neglect. "Reasonable cause" means an act or omission in which a covered entity or business associate knew or should have known of the violation, but the violation was not due to willful neglect.

#### Tier 3: Willful Neglect — Corrected within 30 days
- **Per violation:** $10,000 minimum to $50,000 maximum (adjusted for inflation)
- **Annual maximum:** $250,000 for all identical violations in a calendar year (adjusted for inflation)
- Applies when the violation was the result of willful neglect that was corrected within 30 days of when the entity knew or should have known of the violation.

#### Tier 4: Willful Neglect — Not corrected within 30 days
- **Per violation:** $50,000 minimum (adjusted for inflation) — no cap per violation
- **Annual maximum:** $1,500,000 for all identical violations in a calendar year (adjusted for inflation)
- Applies when the violation was the result of willful neglect and was NOT corrected within 30 days.
- HHS MUST impose a penalty for violations involving willful neglect (mandatory investigation and penalty under HITECH).

### Inflation-Adjusted Penalty Amounts
The Federal Civil Penalties Inflation Adjustment Act requires periodic adjustment. As of the most recent adjustments, the effective maximums are approximately:
- Tier 1: $137 — $68,928 per violation; annual cap ~$2,067,813
- Tier 2: $1,379 — $68,928 per violation; annual cap ~$2,067,813
- Tier 3: $13,785 — $68,928 per violation; annual cap ~$2,067,813
- Tier 4: $68,928 per violation (no per-violation cap); annual cap ~$2,067,813

Note: HHS published a 2019 enforcement discretion notice reducing annual caps for Tiers 1-3, but this was challenged in court. Organizations should plan for the higher HITECH-era caps as the conservative approach.

### Factors in Determining Penalty Amount — 45 CFR 160.408
HHS considers the following aggravating and mitigating factors:
1. **Nature and extent of the violation** — number of individuals affected, duration, types of PHI involved.
2. **Nature and extent of harm** — physical, financial, reputational harm to affected individuals; harm to the entity's ability to provide health care.
3. **History of prior compliance** — prior violations, prior indications of noncompliance, repeat offender status.
4. **Financial condition of the entity** — ability to pay the penalty without jeopardizing the entity's ability to provide health care.
5. **Such other matters as justice may require** — cooperative behavior during investigation, voluntary disclosure, remediation efforts.

## Criminal Penalties — 42 U.S.C. 1320d-6

### Three Tiers of Criminal Penalties

#### Tier 1: Knowingly obtaining or disclosing PHI in violation of HIPAA
- **Fine:** Up to $50,000
- **Imprisonment:** Up to 1 year
- Applies to knowing violations without more culpable intent.

#### Tier 2: Offenses committed under false pretenses
- **Fine:** Up to $100,000
- **Imprisonment:** Up to 5 years
- Applies when PHI was obtained through misrepresentation, false statements, or fraudulent pretenses.

#### Tier 3: Offenses committed with intent to sell, transfer, or use for commercial advantage, personal gain, or malicious harm
- **Fine:** Up to $250,000
- **Imprisonment:** Up to 10 years
- Applies to the most egregious violations — identity theft, sale of records, intentional harm.

### Criminal Liability
- Criminal penalties apply to **individuals**, not just organizations.
- Any person (not just covered entities) who knowingly obtains or discloses individually identifiable health information in violation of HIPAA may face criminal penalties.
- DOJ has prosecuted individuals including employees, executives, and external parties.

## Resolution Agreements and Corrective Action Plans

### Resolution Agreements
Most enforcement actions result in resolution agreements rather than formal CMP proceedings. A resolution agreement typically includes:
- **Monetary settlement** — payment to HHS (historically ranging from $50,000 to $16 million).
- **Corrective Action Plan (CAP)** — multi-year plan requiring the entity to implement specific compliance measures, subject to HHS monitoring.

### Corrective Action Plan Components
Typical CAP requirements include:
- Appointment or confirmation of Privacy and Security Officers.
- Development and implementation of revised policies and procedures.
- Workforce training programs.
- Risk analysis and risk management plan implementation.
- Reporting of compliance activities to HHS at specified intervals (typically 1-3 years).
- Retention of an independent monitor in serious cases.
- Immediate reporting of any subsequent violations during the monitoring period.

### Notable Enforcement Actions (Illustrative)
- Anthem Inc. (2018): $16 million — largest HIPAA settlement. Breach affecting 78.8 million individuals due to cyber attack. Failures in risk analysis, access controls, and security procedures.
- Premera Blue Cross (2020): $6.85 million — breach affecting 10.4 million. Inadequate risk analysis and access controls.
- Banner Health (2023): $1.25 million — breach affecting 2.81 million. Lack of enterprise-wide risk analysis.
- Small provider penalties: range from $50,000 to $500,000 for individual right of access violations, demonstrating that OCR enforces against entities of all sizes.

## State Attorney General Enforcement — HITECH Section 13410(e)

### Authority
State attorneys general may bring civil actions in federal district court on behalf of state residents who have been or are threatened or adversely affected by a HIPAA violation.

### Damages
- **Statutory damages:** Up to $25,000 per identical violation per calendar year (originally; subject to inflation adjustment in line with federal penalties).
- **Injunctive relief:** Courts may grant temporary or permanent injunctions.
- **Attorneys' fees and costs:** May be awarded.

### Coordination with HHS
- The state AG must provide written notice to the Secretary of HHS prior to filing an action.
- HHS may intervene in the action.
- If HHS has already initiated an action, the state AG may not bring a separate action during HHS proceedings.

## HIPAA Audit Program

### OCR Audit Program
OCR conducts periodic audits of covered entities and business associates to assess compliance with the Privacy, Security, and Breach Notification Rules.

### Audit Process
1. **Entity selection** — based on size, type, and risk profile. Both random and risk-based selection.
2. **Data request** — entity must provide documentation (policies, procedures, risk analysis, training records, BAAs, etc.).
3. **Desk audit or on-site audit** — review of documentation and practices.
4. **Draft findings** — entity has opportunity to respond.
5. **Final audit report** — may result in technical assistance or referral to enforcement.

### Audit Focus Areas
Common focus areas include:
- Risk analysis completion and adequacy per [HIPAA-SR-ADMIN]
- Individual right of access compliance per [HIPAA-PR-RIGHTS]
- Notice of Privacy Practices compliance per [HIPAA-PR-NOTICE]
- Breach notification compliance per [HIPAA-BN-REQ]
- Business associate management per [HIPAA-SR-ORG]
- Security awareness training per [HIPAA-SR-ADMIN]

## Implementation Checklist
- [ ] Conduct comprehensive compliance assessment across all HIPAA Rules
- [ ] Document compliance program demonstrating reasonable diligence (Tier 1 defense)
- [ ] Implement 30-day corrective action capability for identified violations (Tier 3 vs. Tier 4)
- [ ] Establish voluntary self-disclosure procedures to HHS for mitigating factors
- [ ] Maintain compliance history documentation for penalty mitigation
- [ ] Prepare for OCR audit readiness (policies, risk analysis, training records, BAA inventory)
- [ ] Monitor state AG enforcement actions relevant to the entity's jurisdictions
- [ ] Establish relationship with legal counsel experienced in HIPAA enforcement defense
- [ ] Budget for potential penalties and remediation costs based on risk profile
- [ ] Implement compliance monitoring to detect and correct violations before enforcement
- [ ] Train leadership on personal criminal liability for knowing violations
- [ ] Review and update compliance program at least annually
