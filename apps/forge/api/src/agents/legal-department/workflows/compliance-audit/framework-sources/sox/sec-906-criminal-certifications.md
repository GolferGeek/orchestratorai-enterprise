# Section 906: Criminal CEO/CFO Certification of Financial Reports

## Overview

Section 906 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (codified at 18 U.S.C. Section 1350) imposes criminal penalties on the chief executive officer and chief financial officer of a public company who certify a periodic report knowing that it does not comply with the requirements of the Securities Exchange Act or that the financial information does not fairly present the company's financial condition and results of operations. This section operates alongside the civil certifications required by Section 302 [SOX-SEC-302] but carries significantly more severe consequences.

## Certification Requirements

### Written Statement Requirement
Each periodic report containing financial statements filed by an issuer with the SEC pursuant to Section 13(a) or 15(d) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 must be accompanied by a **written statement** by the chief executive officer and the chief financial officer (or equivalent thereof) of the issuer.

### Content of the Certification
The certifying officer must certify that:

#### Statement 1: Full Compliance
The periodic report containing the financial statements **fully complies** with the requirements of Section 13(a) or 15(d) of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934.

This encompasses compliance with all applicable SEC rules governing the form and content of periodic reports, including:
- Regulation S-X (form and content of financial statements)
- Regulation S-K (non-financial statement disclosures)
- Specific form requirements (Form 10-K, Form 10-Q)
- All SEC staff accounting bulletins and interpretive guidance

#### Statement 2: Fair Presentation
The information contained in the periodic report **fairly presents, in all material respects**, the financial condition and results of operations of the issuer.

The "fairly presents" standard under Section 906 has been interpreted as potentially broader than compliance with GAAP:
- It may encompass the overall fairness of the financial presentation beyond technical GAAP compliance
- An issuer could technically comply with every GAAP rule and still fail the "fairly presents" test if the overall presentation is misleading
- This creates a substantive standard that looks at the totality of the financial disclosure

### Reports Subject to Certification
- Annual reports on Form 10-K
- Quarterly reports on Form 10-Q
- Amendments to such reports (10-K/A, 10-Q/A)
- Transition reports filed on Form 10-K or 10-Q

### Filing Requirement
The Section 906 certification is filed as **Exhibit 32** to the periodic report. Under current SEC practice, Exhibit 32 certifications are "furnished" rather than "filed" with the SEC, which affects potential liability under Section 18 of the Exchange Act but does not affect criminal liability under 18 U.S.C. Section 1350.

## Criminal Penalties

### Knowing Violation (18 U.S.C. Section 1350(c)(1))
Any person who certifies any statement as set forth in Section 906 **knowing** that the periodic report accompanying the statement does not comport with all the requirements set forth in this section shall be fined not more than **$1,000,000** or imprisoned not more than **10 years**, or both.

#### Elements of a "Knowing" Violation
- The certifying officer was aware that the report did not fully comply with Exchange Act requirements, OR
- The certifying officer was aware that the financial information did not fairly present the financial condition and results of operations
- Knowledge may be inferred from circumstantial evidence, including the officer's access to information, involvement in the reporting process, and awareness of issues raised by subordinates, auditors, or the audit committee

### Willful Violation (18 U.S.C. Section 1350(c)(2))
Any person who **willfully** certifies any statement as set forth in Section 906 knowing that the periodic report accompanying the statement does not comport with all the requirements set forth in this section shall be fined not more than **$5,000,000** or imprisoned not more than **20 years**, or both.

#### Elements of a "Willful" Violation
- All elements of a knowing violation, PLUS
- The certifying officer acted with the specific intent to deceive or with deliberate and conscious disregard for the truth
- A willful violation involves a volitional act done with bad purpose — not merely carelessness or negligence
- The distinction between "knowing" and "willful" is critical: knowing requires awareness of the non-compliance; willful requires awareness plus intentional misconduct

## Comparison: Section 302 vs Section 906

| Feature | Section 302 [SOX-SEC-302] | Section 906 |
|---------|---------------------------|-------------|
| Codification | SEC Rule 13a-14(a)/15d-14(a) | 18 U.S.C. Section 1350 |
| Nature | Civil/administrative | Criminal |
| Exhibit | Exhibit 31 | Exhibit 32 |
| Filing status | "Filed" with SEC | "Furnished" to SEC |
| Standard | "Based on certifying officer's knowledge" | "Fully complies" and "fairly presents" |
| Scope of certification | Disclosure controls, internal controls, specific representations | Full compliance with Exchange Act, fair presentation |
| Penalty — knowing | SEC civil enforcement, disgorgement, civil penalties | Up to $1,000,000 fine, up to 10 years imprisonment |
| Penalty — willful | SEC civil enforcement, officer/director bars | Up to $5,000,000 fine, up to 20 years imprisonment |
| Private right of action | Generally yes (under Rule 10b-5 and Section 18) | No — criminal enforcement only by DOJ |
| Clawback | Yes, under Section 304 | Not directly applicable |

## Who Must Certify

### Principal Executive Officer
- The CEO or person performing the functions of the CEO
- If the issuer has co-CEOs, both must certify
- An interim or acting CEO must certify for reports filed during their tenure

### Principal Financial Officer
- The CFO or person performing the functions of the CFO
- If the issuer has a CFO and a separate principal accounting officer, the CFO certifies under Section 906
- An interim or acting CFO must certify for reports filed during their tenure

### No Delegation
- The certification cannot be delegated to a subordinate officer
- The certification cannot be signed by power of attorney
- Both officers must personally sign — there is no exception

## Relationship to Internal Controls Assessment [SOX-SEC-404]

The Section 906 certification that the report "fully complies" with Exchange Act requirements implicitly encompasses compliance with Section 404's internal control reporting requirements. If management's internal control report contains a material misstatement (e.g., concluding ICFR is effective when a material weakness exists), the Section 906 certification may be false.

This creates a cascading liability chain:
1. Material weakness exists but is not identified or reported (Section 404 violation)
2. CEO/CFO certify that disclosure controls are effective (Section 302 violation)
3. CEO/CFO certify under criminal penalty that the report fully complies (Section 906 violation)
4. If the material weakness results in a material misstatement, the "fairly presents" certification is also false

## DOJ Prosecution Considerations

### Referral Process
- The SEC may refer potential Section 906 violations to the Department of Justice for criminal prosecution
- The DOJ's Fraud Section and U.S. Attorney's Offices evaluate referrals based on the strength of evidence, the egregiousness of the conduct, and the deterrent effect of prosecution
- The SEC and DOJ frequently coordinate parallel civil and criminal proceedings

### Prosecution History
- Section 906 prosecutions are relatively rare due to the high burden of proving knowledge or willfulness beyond a reasonable doubt
- The most significant prosecutions have involved cases where the CEO/CFO had direct, documented knowledge of material misstatements
- Many cases involving financial statement fraud are prosecuted under the broader securities fraud statute (Section 10(b) and Rule 10b-5) rather than Section 906 specifically

### Constitutional Considerations
- The "fairly presents" standard has been challenged as potentially vague, but courts have generally upheld it as providing adequate notice of the prohibited conduct
- The distinction between "knowing" and "willful" has been scrutinized for constitutional sufficiency
- Due process challenges have generally been unsuccessful given the clarity of the certification language

## Practical Implications for Certifying Officers

### Due Diligence Before Certification
Before signing Section 906 certifications, the CEO and CFO should:
- Review the periodic report in its entirety
- Review sub-certifications from business unit and functional leaders
- Discuss significant accounting judgments and estimates with the audit committee and external auditor
- Review any open or unresolved accounting or disclosure issues
- Review the results of the most recent disclosure controls and procedures evaluation [SOX-SEC-302]
- Review any material weaknesses or significant deficiencies in internal controls [SOX-SEC-404]
- Consult with legal counsel regarding any potential compliance issues

### Documentation
- Maintain a record of the review and due diligence process undertaken before each certification
- Document any issues considered and the basis for their resolution
- Retain copies of sub-certifications and management representation letters
- Preserve correspondence with the audit committee and external auditor regarding financial reporting matters

## Compliance Checklist

- [ ] CEO and CFO personally review each periodic report before signing Section 906 certifications
- [ ] Section 906 certification language conforms exactly to 18 U.S.C. Section 1350 requirements
- [ ] Certifications are included as Exhibit 32 to each periodic report (10-K, 10-Q)
- [ ] Both CEO and CFO sign — no delegation or power of attorney
- [ ] Due diligence process is documented for each certification cycle
- [ ] Sub-certification process provides CEO/CFO with reasonable basis for certification
- [ ] All material accounting judgments and disclosure decisions are reviewed with audit committee
- [ ] Open accounting issues are resolved before certification
- [ ] Legal counsel reviews for potential compliance issues before each filing
- [ ] Officers understand the distinction between knowing ($1M/10 years) and willful ($5M/20 years) penalties
- [ ] D&O insurance coverage is reviewed for adequacy against potential Section 906 claims
- [ ] Coordinate Section 906 (Exhibit 32) and Section 302 (Exhibit 31) certifications to ensure consistency
