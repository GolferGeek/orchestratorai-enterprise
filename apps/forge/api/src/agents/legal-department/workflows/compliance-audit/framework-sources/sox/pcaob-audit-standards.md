# PCAOB Audit Standards and Oversight

## Overview

The Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB) was established by Section 101 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act to oversee the audits of public companies in order to protect the interests of investors and further the public interest in the preparation of informative, accurate, and independent audit reports. The PCAOB registers public accounting firms, establishes auditing and related professional practice standards, conducts inspections of registered firms, and investigates and disciplines firms and individuals for violations.

## PCAOB Establishment and Authority (SOX Section 101)

### Structure
- **Board composition**: Five members appointed by the SEC after consultation with the Chairman of the Federal Reserve Board and the Secretary of the Treasury
- **Independence**: Two members must be or have been certified public accountants; three members must not be and must never have been CPAs
- **Term**: Five-year terms, staggered; members serve full-time
- **Funding**: Funded through annual accounting support fees assessed on issuers (based on relative market capitalization) and registration and annual fees from registered firms

### Authority
- **Registration**: All public accounting firms that prepare or issue audit reports for issuers must register with the PCAOB (Section 102)
- **Standards**: The PCAOB establishes or adopts auditing, quality control, ethics, independence, and other standards (Section 103)
- **Inspections**: The PCAOB conducts inspections of registered firms (Section 104) — annually for firms auditing more than 100 issuers, at least triennially for smaller firms
- **Investigations and enforcement**: The PCAOB may investigate potential violations and impose sanctions (Section 105)

## AS 2201: Audit of Internal Control Over Financial Reporting

### Integrated Audit Approach
AS 2201 (formerly AS No. 5) requires auditors to perform an **integrated audit** — auditing both the financial statements and the effectiveness of internal control over financial reporting (ICFR) in a coordinated manner. The objectives are:

1. **Express an opinion** on the effectiveness of the company's ICFR
2. **Plan and perform the audit** to obtain reasonable assurance about whether material weaknesses exist as of the assessment date
3. **Integrate** the audit of ICFR with the audit of the financial statements to achieve efficiencies

### Identifying Significant Accounts and Disclosures
The auditor must identify significant accounts and disclosures by evaluating:
- **Size and composition** of the account
- **Susceptibility to misstatement** due to error or fraud
- **Volume of activity, complexity, and homogeneity** of transactions
- **Nature of the account or disclosure** (e.g., estimates involve more judgment)
- **Accounting and reporting complexities** associated with the account
- **Exposure to losses** in the account
- **Existence of related-party transactions** in the account
- **Changes from the prior period** in account characteristics or related processes

### Selecting Controls to Test
The auditor must:
- Understand the flow of transactions, including how transactions are initiated, authorized, processed, recorded, and reported
- Identify points at which misstatements could occur (what could go wrong — "WCGW")
- Identify controls that management has implemented to address each WCGW
- Select controls for testing based on which controls sufficiently address the assessed risk of misstatement for each relevant assertion

### Testing Design Effectiveness
The auditor evaluates whether the control is suitably designed to prevent or detect material misstatements:
- Is the control properly designed for the risk it addresses?
- Would the control, if operating as designed, effectively prevent or detect misstatements?
- Is the person performing the control competent and authorized?
- Testing methods: inquiry, observation, walkthroughs, inspection of documentation

### Testing Operating Effectiveness
The auditor determines whether the control operated as designed throughout the period:
- **Inquiry** alone is not sufficient — must be combined with other procedures
- **Observation** of the control being performed
- **Inspection** of relevant documentation evidencing performance of the control
- **Reperformance** of the control by the auditor
- Timing: controls must be tested over a sufficient period (not just point-in-time)
- For controls operating throughout the year, the auditor should test over a representative portion of the period

### Evaluating Identified Deficiencies
The auditor must evaluate each identified control deficiency:

#### Severity Assessment
- **Deficiency**: A control does not allow prevention or detection of misstatements on a timely basis
- **Significant Deficiency**: Important enough to merit attention by those charged with governance [SOX-SEC-301]
- **Material Weakness**: Reasonable possibility that a material misstatement will not be prevented or detected on a timely basis — requires an **adverse opinion** on ICFR

#### Factors in Severity Evaluation
- Financial statement amounts or totals of transactions exposed to the deficiency
- Volume of activity in the account exposed to the deficiency during the current period or expected in future periods
- Whether there is a reasonable possibility that one deficiency, or a combination of deficiencies, could result in a misstatement of an account balance or disclosure

### Forming an Opinion on ICFR
- **Unqualified opinion**: ICFR is effective in all material respects based on the COSO framework
- **Adverse opinion**: One or more material weaknesses exist — ICFR is NOT effective
- **Disclaimer of opinion**: The auditor is unable to express an opinion due to a scope limitation
- The auditor may NOT issue a qualified opinion on ICFR ("except for" opinions are not permitted)

## AS 1301: Communications with Audit Committees

### Required Communications Before the Audit
The auditor must communicate to the audit committee [SOX-SEC-301]:
- The auditor's responsibilities under PCAOB standards
- An overview of the planned scope and timing of the audit
- Significant risks identified during planning
- The auditor's approach to internal control in an integrated audit

### Required Communications During and After the Audit
The auditor must communicate:
- **Significant accounting policies and practices**: critical accounting estimates, significant unusual transactions, the auditor's evaluation of the quality of the entity's accounting principles
- **Critical audit matters (CAMs)**: matters communicated or required to be communicated to the audit committee that relate to accounts or disclosures material to the financial statements and involved especially challenging, subjective, or complex auditor judgment
- **Significant difficulties encountered**: delays in management providing information, unnecessarily brief timetables, unavailability of expected information or personnel
- **Disagreements with management**: regarding accounting principles, audit scope or procedures, or disclosures
- **Material weaknesses and significant deficiencies** identified during the audit [SOX-SEC-404]
- **Fraud involving senior management** or fraud that causes a material misstatement
- **Illegal acts**: unless clearly inconsequential
- **Related-party transactions**: significant transactions identified during the audit
- **Going concern**: substantial doubt about the entity's ability to continue as a going concern
- **Uncorrected misstatements**: all misstatements identified during the audit that were not corrected by management
- **Auditor independence**: all relationships between the firm and the issuer that may reasonably be thought to bear on independence [SOX-SEC-201]

### Timing and Form
- Communications should be made in a timely manner — before issuance of the audit report for most matters
- Written communication is required for material weaknesses, significant deficiencies, and independence matters
- The auditor should communicate with the audit committee directly, not through management

## AS 2401: Consideration of Fraud in a Financial Statement Audit

### Auditor Responsibility
The auditor has a responsibility to plan and perform the audit to obtain **reasonable assurance** about whether the financial statements are free of material misstatement, whether caused by **error or fraud**.

### Two Types of Fraud
1. **Fraudulent financial reporting**: intentional misstatements or omissions of amounts or disclosures in the financial statements (e.g., earnings manipulation, fictitious revenues, improper asset valuations)
2. **Misappropriation of assets**: theft of the entity's assets (e.g., embezzlement, theft of inventory, fraudulent disbursements, skimming)

### Fraud Risk Assessment Procedures
The auditor must:
- **Make inquiries** of management, the audit committee, internal audit, and others regarding the risks of fraud and the programs and controls to mitigate those risks
- **Perform analytical procedures** to identify unusual or unexpected relationships that may indicate fraud risk
- **Consider fraud risk factors**: incentives/pressures (e.g., financial targets, bonus structures), opportunities (e.g., weak controls, management override), and attitudes/rationalizations (e.g., management's attitude toward aggressive accounting)
- **Discuss among the engagement team**: brainstorming session about how and where fraud could occur

### Required Audit Procedures
- **Revenue recognition**: There is a presumption that improper revenue recognition is a fraud risk in every audit (the auditor must either design specific procedures or document why the presumption does not apply)
- **Management override of controls**: This is always a fraud risk — the auditor must test:
  - Journal entries and other adjustments (particularly period-end entries)
  - Accounting estimates for bias
  - Business rationale for significant unusual transactions
- **Unpredictability**: The auditor should incorporate an element of unpredictability in the selection, timing, extent, and nature of audit procedures

### Responding to Identified or Suspected Fraud
When fraud is identified or suspected:
- Obtain additional evidence to determine whether fraud has occurred
- Consider the implications for other aspects of the audit
- Evaluate whether identified fraud is indicative of a material weakness [SOX-SEC-404]
- **Communicate to management** one level above those involved in the fraud
- **Communicate directly to the audit committee** any fraud involving senior management or any fraud (whether material or not) involving others that causes a material misstatement [SOX-SEC-301]
- Consider obligations to report to the SEC under Section 10A(b) of the Exchange Act (reporting illegal acts)
- **Document** the fraud risk assessment procedures, identified risks, audit responses, and conclusions

## PCAOB Inspection Program

### Inspection Scope
- Firms auditing more than 100 issuers: inspected **annually**
- All other registered firms: inspected at least every **3 years**
- Inspections include review of selected audit engagements and evaluation of the firm's quality control system

### Inspection Findings
- **Part I findings**: deficiencies identified in specific audit engagements (publicly disclosed)
- **Part II findings**: deficiencies in the firm's quality control system (withheld from public for 12 months to allow remediation; published if not satisfactorily addressed)

### PCAOB Enforcement (Section 105)
Sanctions for violations of SOX, PCAOB rules, professional standards, or securities laws:
- **Censure**: public reprimand
- **Temporary suspension** of registration (up to a specified period)
- **Permanent revocation** of registration
- **Temporary or permanent bar** of an individual from association with a registered firm
- **Civil money penalties**: up to **$100,000** per violation for individuals; up to **$2,000,000** per violation for firms (or for intentional/knowing/reckless conduct: up to **$750,000** for individuals, up to **$15,000,000** for firms)
- **Additional professional education or training**
- **Disgorgement** of fees or other amounts

## Compliance Checklist

- [ ] External auditor is registered with the PCAOB
- [ ] Integrated audit approach covers both financial statements and ICFR
- [ ] Significant accounts and disclosures have been identified and documented
- [ ] Key controls are selected and tested for both design and operating effectiveness
- [ ] Deficiencies are evaluated and classified (deficiency, significant deficiency, material weakness)
- [ ] Audit committee receives all required communications under AS 1301
- [ ] CAMs are identified and communicated to the audit committee
- [ ] Fraud risk assessment is performed per AS 2401, including management override testing
- [ ] Revenue recognition fraud presumption is addressed or documented as not applicable
- [ ] Journal entry testing is performed for management override
- [ ] Engagement team brainstorming session on fraud risk is documented
- [ ] All material weaknesses and significant deficiencies are communicated in writing to the audit committee
- [ ] Auditor independence is confirmed and communicated to the audit committee [SOX-SEC-201]
- [ ] Audit workpapers comply with AS 1215 documentation requirements (45-day assembly, 7-year retention) [SOX-SEC-802]
