# GDPR Articles 44-49 — Transfers of Personal Data to Third Countries or International Organizations

## Article 44 — General Principle for Transfers

Any transfer of personal data which is undergoing processing or is intended for processing after transfer to a third country or to an international organization shall take place only if the conditions in Chapter V are complied with by the controller and processor, including for onward transfers from the third country or international organization to another third country or international organization.

All provisions of Chapter V shall be applied in order to ensure that the level of protection of natural persons guaranteed by the GDPR is not undermined.

## Article 45 — Transfers on the Basis of an Adequacy Decision

### Article 45(1) — Adequacy Mechanism

A transfer of personal data to a third country or an international organization may take place where the European Commission has decided that the third country, a territory, or one or more specified sectors within that third country, or the international organization in question ensures an adequate level of protection.

Countries with adequacy decisions (as of 2024) include:
- Andorra, Argentina, Canada (commercial organizations under PIPEDA), Faroe Islands, Guernsey, Israel, Isle of Man, Japan, Jersey, New Zealand, Republic of Korea, Switzerland, United Kingdom, Uruguay
- United States (under the EU-US Data Privacy Framework for certified organizations)

### Article 45(2) — Assessment Criteria

The Commission shall assess:
- The rule of law, human rights, and relevant legislation
- The existence and effective functioning of independent supervisory authorities
- International commitments entered into by the third country

### Article 45(3)-(9) — Monitoring and Revocation

The Commission shall monitor developments in third countries and may repeal, amend, or suspend adequacy decisions. Adequacy decisions are reviewed at least every four years.

## Article 46 — Transfers Subject to Appropriate Safeguards

### Article 46(1)-(2) — Safeguard Mechanisms

In the absence of an adequacy decision, a controller or processor may transfer personal data to a third country or an international organization only if appropriate safeguards are provided, and on condition that enforceable data subject rights and effective legal remedies for data subjects are available.

Appropriate safeguards may be provided by:

**(a)** A legally binding and enforceable instrument between public authorities or bodies

**(b) Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs)**
Approved under Article 47. BCRs are data protection policies adhered to by a group of undertakings for transfers within the group. Approval process involves:
- Submission to the lead supervisory authority
- Consistency mechanism under Article 63
- Must be legally binding and enforceable by every member

**(c) Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)**
Adopted by the Commission under Article 46(2)(c). The current SCCs (adopted June 2021) cover four modules:
- Module 1: Controller to controller (C2C)
- Module 2: Controller to processor (C2P)
- Module 3: Processor to processor (P2P)
- Module 4: Processor to controller (P2C)

**(d) Standard Contractual Clauses adopted by a supervisory authority** and approved by the Commission

**(e) Approved codes of conduct** under Article 40 with binding and enforceable commitments by the controller or processor in the third country

**(f) Approved certification mechanisms** under Article 42 with binding and enforceable commitments by the controller or processor in the third country

### Transfer Impact Assessment (TIA)

Following the Schrems II judgment (CJEU C-311/18), controllers using SCCs or BCRs must conduct a transfer impact assessment to verify that the legal framework of the recipient country does not impinge on the effectiveness of the safeguards. The TIA must assess:

1. The circumstances of the transfer (nature of data, purpose, sector, recipient country)
2. The laws and practices of the recipient country relevant to the transfer
3. Whether supplementary measures are necessary to ensure essentially equivalent protection

Supplementary measures may include:
- Technical: Encryption where the key is retained by the exporter, pseudonymization, split processing
- Contractual: Obligations on the importer to challenge government access requests, transparency commitments
- Organizational: Internal policies restricting access, audit procedures

## Article 47 — Binding Corporate Rules

BCRs must specify at least:
- The structure and contact details of the group
- The data transfers covered (categories of data, type of processing, purposes, affected data subjects)
- Their legally binding nature, internally and externally
- Application of the general data protection principles, especially purpose limitation, data minimization, limited storage periods, data quality, data protection by design and by default, legal basis for processing, processing of special categories, measures to ensure data security, and requirements for onward transfers
- The rights of data subjects and means to exercise them
- Acceptance by the controller or processor of liability for breaches of the BCRs by any member
- How the information on the BCRs is provided to the data subjects
- The tasks of the DPO
- The complaint procedures
- The mechanisms within the group for ensuring verification of compliance with the BCRs
- The mechanisms for reporting and recording changes to the rules and reporting those changes to the supervisory authority
- The cooperation mechanism with the supervisory authority
- The mechanisms for reporting to the competent supervisory authority any legal requirements to which a member of the group of undertakings is subject in a third country which are likely to have a substantial adverse effect on the guarantees provided by the BCRs
- Appropriate data protection training for personnel having permanent or regular access to personal data

## Article 49 — Derogations for Specific Situations

In the absence of an adequacy decision or appropriate safeguards, a transfer may take place only if one of the following derogations applies:

**(a)** Explicit consent — after being informed of the possible risks
**(b)** Necessary for contract performance between data subject and controller
**(c)** Necessary for pre-contractual measures at data subject's request
**(d)** Necessary for contract performance in the interest of the data subject between controller and another person
**(e)** Necessary for important reasons of public interest
**(f)** Necessary for establishment, exercise, or defense of legal claims
**(g)** Necessary to protect vital interests when data subject is incapable of giving consent

Derogations must be interpreted restrictively and generally cannot justify systematic, large-scale, or repeated transfers.

### Article 49(1) Second Subparagraph — Compelling Legitimate Interests

Where none of the derogations apply, a transfer may take place only if:
- The transfer is not repetitive
- It concerns only a limited number of data subjects
- It is necessary for compelling legitimate interests of the controller
- The controller has assessed all circumstances and provided suitable safeguards
- The controller has informed the supervisory authority and the data subject

## Enforcement

Violations of Chapter V (Articles 44-49) are subject to the higher tier of administrative fines under Article 83(5)(c): up to EUR 20,000,000 or 4% of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher.
