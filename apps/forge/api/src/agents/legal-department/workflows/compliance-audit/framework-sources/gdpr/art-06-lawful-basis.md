# GDPR Article 6 — Lawfulness of Processing

## Article 6(1) — Six Lawful Bases

Processing of personal data is lawful only if and to the extent that at least one of the following applies:

### (a) Consent
The data subject has given consent to the processing of their personal data for one or more specific purposes. Consent must meet the conditions specified in Article 7 and, for children, Article 8. Consent must be freely given, specific, informed, and an unambiguous indication of wishes through a clear affirmative action.

Requirements for valid consent:
- Must be freely given — not a precondition of a service unless necessary for that service
- Must be specific — separate consent for distinct processing purposes
- Must be informed — data subject must know the controller's identity and processing purposes
- Must be unambiguous — requires a clear affirmative act (pre-ticked boxes do not constitute consent)
- Must be demonstrable — controller must be able to prove consent was obtained
- Must be withdrawable — data subjects can withdraw at any time, withdrawal must be as easy as giving consent

### (b) Contract Performance
Processing is necessary for the performance of a contract to which the data subject is party, or to take steps at the data subject's request prior to entering into a contract. This basis covers only processing that is objectively necessary for the contract, not merely useful or mentioned in contract terms.

### (c) Legal Obligation
Processing is necessary for compliance with a legal obligation to which the controller is subject. The legal obligation must be established by Union or Member State law. This basis does not cover voluntary industry standards or contractual obligations — only obligations imposed by law.

### (d) Vital Interests
Processing is necessary to protect the vital interests of the data subject or of another natural person. This basis is generally limited to life-or-death situations and cannot be used when another lawful basis is available. It covers emergency medical situations, natural disasters, and humanitarian crises.

### (e) Public Interest or Official Authority
Processing is necessary for the performance of a task carried out in the public interest or in the exercise of official authority vested in the controller. The basis for this processing must be established in Union or Member State law.

### (f) Legitimate Interests
Processing is necessary for the purposes of the legitimate interests pursued by the controller or by a third party, except where such interests are overridden by the interests or fundamental rights and freedoms of the data subject, in particular where the data subject is a child. This basis does not apply to processing carried out by public authorities in the performance of their tasks.

Legitimate interest assessment (balancing test) must document:
1. Purpose test — identify the legitimate interest being pursued
2. Necessity test — assess whether processing is necessary for that interest
3. Balancing test — weigh the controller's interests against the data subject's rights, considering:
   - Nature of the data (sensitive data weighs against the controller)
   - Reasonable expectations of the data subject
   - Status of the data subject (children require special consideration)
   - Impact on the data subject
   - Safeguards the controller can implement to mitigate impact

## Article 6(2)-(3) — Member State Law

Member States may maintain or introduce more specific provisions to adapt the application of the rules regarding lawful bases (c) and (e). The legal basis for processing under (c) and (e) must be laid down by Union law or Member State law, which must meet an objective of public interest and be proportionate to the legitimate aim pursued.

## Article 6(4) — Compatible Further Processing

Where processing for a purpose other than that for which the personal data was originally collected is not based on consent or Union/Member State law, the controller must assess compatibility considering:
- Any link between original and new purposes
- Context of collection and data subject relationship
- Nature of the data
- Possible consequences for data subjects
- Existence of appropriate safeguards

## Documentation Requirements

Controllers must document:
- The lawful basis relied upon for each processing activity
- The reasoning for selecting that basis (especially for legitimate interests)
- How the basis was communicated to data subjects (via privacy notice under Articles 13-14)
- Any changes to the lawful basis and the justification

## Enforcement

Violations of Article 6 are subject to the higher tier of administrative fines under Article 83(5)(a): up to EUR 20,000,000 or 4% of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher.
