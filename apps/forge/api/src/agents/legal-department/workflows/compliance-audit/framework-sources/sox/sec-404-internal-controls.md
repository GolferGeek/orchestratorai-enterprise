# Section 404: Management Assessment of Internal Controls

## Overview

Section 404 is the most operationally significant provision of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act. It establishes requirements for management's assessment of internal control over financial reporting (ICFR) and, for larger filers, mandates an independent auditor attestation on that assessment. Section 404 has fundamentally changed how public companies design, document, test, and report on their internal control environments.

## Section 404(a): Management's Internal Control Report

### Requirements
Each annual report filed under Section 13(a) or 15(d) of the Securities Exchange Act shall contain an **internal control report** which shall:

1. **State the responsibility** of management for establishing and maintaining an adequate internal control structure and procedures for financial reporting
2. **Contain an assessment**, as of the end of the most recent fiscal year of the issuer, of the **effectiveness** of the internal control structure and procedures of the issuer for financial reporting

### Applicability
- All public companies (accelerated filers, large accelerated filers, non-accelerated filers, smaller reporting companies, and emerging growth companies) must comply with Section 404(a)
- There is no exemption from management's own assessment requirement

## Section 404(b): External Auditor Attestation

### Requirements
The registered public accounting firm that prepares or issues the audit report for the issuer shall **attest to, and report on**, the assessment made by the management of the issuer in accordance with Section 404(a). The attestation must be made in accordance with standards for attestation engagements issued or adopted by the PCAOB.

### Exemptions from Section 404(b)
- **Non-accelerated filers** with public float below **$75 million** are permanently exempt from auditor attestation (Dodd-Frank Act Section 989G, amending SOX Section 404(c))
- **Emerging Growth Companies (EGCs)** are exempt during the EGC period (up to 5 years from IPO, subject to revenue and float thresholds)
- Section 404(a) management assessment still applies to these exempt companies

## COSO Framework (2013)

### Standard Framework
The SEC has indicated that management should use a **suitable, recognized control framework** to conduct its assessment. The most widely used framework is the **Committee of Sponsoring Organizations of the Treadway Commission (COSO) Internal Control — Integrated Framework (2013)**.

### Five Components of Internal Control
1. **Control Environment** — the set of standards, processes, and structures that provide the basis for carrying out internal control across the organization (tone at the top, governance, organizational structure, accountability)
2. **Risk Assessment** — the process for identifying and assessing risks to the achievement of objectives, including risks of material misstatement in financial reporting
3. **Control Activities** — the actions established through policies and procedures that help ensure management directives to mitigate risks are carried out (approvals, authorizations, verifications, reconciliations, segregation of duties)
4. **Information and Communication** — the information necessary for the entity to carry out internal control responsibilities, and the communication of that information internally and externally
5. **Monitoring Activities** — ongoing evaluations, separate evaluations, or some combination to ascertain whether each of the five components of internal control is present and functioning

### 17 Principles
Each COSO component contains specific principles (17 total) that must be present and functioning for internal control to be effective. All 17 principles must be present and functioning, and operating together in an integrated manner, for the system to be effective.

## Key Definitions

### Material Weakness
A deficiency, or a combination of deficiencies, in internal control over financial reporting such that there is a **reasonable possibility** that a material misstatement of the company's annual or interim financial statements will not be prevented or detected on a timely basis. A material weakness requires management to conclude that ICFR is **not effective**.

### Significant Deficiency
A deficiency, or a combination of deficiencies, in internal control over financial reporting that is less severe than a material weakness yet important enough to merit **attention by those charged with governance** (the audit committee). A significant deficiency does not prevent management from concluding ICFR is effective, but it must be communicated to the audit committee.

### Deficiency
A deficiency exists when the design or operation of a control does not allow management or employees, in the normal course of performing their assigned functions, to prevent or detect misstatements on a timely basis. A deficiency in design exists when a control necessary to meet the control objective is missing or is not properly designed. A deficiency in operation exists when a properly designed control does not operate as designed or the person performing the control lacks the authority or competence to perform effectively.

## Top-Down Risk Assessment Approach

### Step 1: Identify Financial Reporting Risks
- Identify significant accounts and disclosures in the financial statements
- Determine relevant assertions (existence/occurrence, completeness, valuation/allocation, rights and obligations, presentation and disclosure)
- Assess inherent risk factors (volume, complexity, subjectivity, susceptibility to fraud)

### Step 2: Identify Entity-Level Controls
- Controls related to the control environment (tone at the top, code of ethics)
- Management's risk assessment process
- Centralized processing and controls, including shared service environments
- Controls to monitor results of operations and other controls
- Controls over the period-end financial reporting process
- Policies that address significant business control and risk management practices

### Step 3: Identify Significant Processes and Controls
- Map significant accounts to business processes
- Identify process-level controls (preventive and detective)
- Identify key controls that address the risk of material misstatement for each relevant assertion

### Step 4: Identify IT General Controls (ITGCs)
- **Program change management** — controls over changes to applications and systems
- **Access to programs and data** — logical and physical access controls
- **Program development** — controls over new system development and implementation
- **Computer operations** — controls over job scheduling, backup and recovery, and incident management

## Documentation Requirements

### Flowcharts and Narratives
- Process flowcharts documenting the flow of transactions from initiation through financial statement reporting
- Written narratives describing the process, risks, and controls at each step
- Identification of control owners responsible for performing each control

### Risk-Control Matrices
- Mapping of identified risks to specific controls that mitigate those risks
- Identification of relevant financial statement assertions addressed by each control
- Classification of controls as preventive or detective, manual or automated
- Assessment of control design effectiveness

### Evidence of Testing
- Documentation of test procedures performed (inquiry, observation, inspection, reperformance)
- Sample sizes and selection methodology
- Test results, including any exceptions identified
- Evaluation of exceptions and their impact on control effectiveness
- Conclusions regarding design effectiveness and operating effectiveness

## Testing Requirements

### Design Effectiveness
- Evaluate whether the control, if operating as designed, would effectively prevent or detect material misstatements in the relevant assertion
- Consider whether the control is properly designed for the risk it addresses
- Verify that control procedures are documented and that responsible parties understand their roles

### Operating Effectiveness
- Test whether the control operated as designed throughout the period under assessment
- Testing methods: inquiry alone is insufficient — must combine with observation, inspection of documentation, or reperformance
- **Sample-based testing**: sample sizes should be sufficient to provide a reasonable basis for conclusions about operating effectiveness over the entire period
- **Timing of tests**: controls should be tested over a sufficient period to determine operating effectiveness (not just point-in-time)

## Remediation of Deficiencies

### Remediation Timeline
- Material weaknesses must be remediated before management can conclude ICFR is effective
- Remediation must be in place and operating for a sufficient period to allow testing of operating effectiveness
- The SEC does not prescribe a specific minimum remediation period, but auditors typically require the remediated control to operate for a sufficient portion of the fiscal year

### Disclosure Requirements
- Material weaknesses must be disclosed in the annual report (10-K) and in any quarterly report (10-Q) if the weakness existed as of the quarterly period end
- Management's remediation plans should be disclosed
- The external auditor's report will include an adverse opinion on ICFR if a material weakness exists

## Penalties for Non-Compliance

- SEC enforcement actions for failure to maintain adequate internal controls (Section 13(b)(2)(B))
- Potential restatement of financial statements if material weaknesses result in material misstatements
- CEO/CFO certification liability under Sections 302 [SOX-SEC-302] and 906 [SOX-SEC-906]
- Shareholder litigation (securities fraud class actions) based on internal control failures
- PCAOB disciplinary action against auditors who fail to properly attest to management's assessment [SOX-SEC-PCAOB]

## Compliance Checklist

- [ ] Adopt a suitable recognized control framework (COSO 2013 recommended)
- [ ] Perform top-down risk assessment to identify significant accounts and processes
- [ ] Identify and document entity-level controls across all five COSO components
- [ ] Identify and document process-level controls for significant processes
- [ ] Identify and document IT general controls supporting financial reporting
- [ ] Prepare and maintain risk-control matrices for all significant processes
- [ ] Test design effectiveness of all key controls
- [ ] Test operating effectiveness of all key controls with appropriate sample sizes
- [ ] Evaluate and classify all identified deficiencies (deficiency, significant deficiency, material weakness)
- [ ] Remediate material weaknesses and retest before year-end
- [ ] Communicate significant deficiencies and material weaknesses to audit committee [SOX-SEC-301]
- [ ] Prepare management's internal control report for inclusion in Form 10-K
- [ ] Coordinate with external auditor for Section 404(b) attestation (if applicable)
- [ ] CEO and CFO certify effectiveness of disclosure controls [SOX-SEC-302]
