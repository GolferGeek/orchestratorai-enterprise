# Breach Notification Requirements

## Regulatory Basis
45 CFR 164.400 through 164.414 (Subpart D) establish the breach notification requirements for covered entities and business associates. These provisions were added by the HITECH Act [HIPAA-HITECH] and require notification to affected individuals, HHS, and in certain cases the media following a breach of unsecured PHI.

## Definition of Breach — 45 CFR 164.402

### What Constitutes a Breach
A breach is the acquisition, access, use, or disclosure of PHI in a manner not permitted under the Privacy Rule [HIPAA-PR-USE] that compromises the security or privacy of the PHI.

### Compromise Standard
A use or disclosure of PHI is presumed to be a breach unless the covered entity or business associate demonstrates that there is a low probability that the PHI has been compromised based on a risk assessment of at least the following four factors:

#### Four-Factor Risk Assessment
1. **Nature and extent of PHI involved** — types of identifiers and likelihood of re-identification. Disclosures involving financial information (SSN, credit card), clinical information, or other sensitive data weigh toward notification.
2. **Unauthorized person who used the PHI or to whom the disclosure was made** — whether the recipient has obligations to protect PHI (e.g., another covered entity or business associate) or is an unauthorized third party.
3. **Whether the PHI was actually acquired or viewed** — documentary evidence such as forensic analysis, audit logs, or attestation from the recipient that PHI was not retained or further disclosed.
4. **Extent to which the risk to the PHI has been mitigated** — actions taken such as obtaining the recipient's satisfactory assurances that the PHI will not be further used or disclosed, or confirmation of destruction.

The covered entity must document the risk assessment regardless of outcome. If the risk assessment does not demonstrate low probability of compromise, breach notification is required.

### Exclusions from Breach Definition — 45 CFR 164.402(1)
Three categories of uses or disclosures are NOT breaches:

1. **Unintentional access by workforce member** — acting under authority of the covered entity or business associate, made in good faith and within the scope of authority, and the information is not further used or disclosed in an impermissible manner.
2. **Inadvertent disclosure between authorized persons** — within the same covered entity, business associate, or organized health care arrangement, and the information is not further used or disclosed in an impermissible manner.
3. **Good faith belief of inability to retain** — the covered entity or business associate has a good faith belief that the unauthorized person to whom the disclosure was made would not reasonably have been able to retain the information.

### Unsecured PHI
Breach notification requirements apply only to **unsecured PHI** — PHI that has not been rendered unusable, unreadable, or indecipherable to unauthorized persons through the use of encryption or destruction per HHS guidance. See [HIPAA-BN-UNSECURED] for the safe harbor standards.

## Individual Notification — 45 CFR 164.404

### Timeline
- Notification must be provided **without unreasonable delay** and in no case later than **60 calendar days from the date of discovery** of the breach.
- **Date of discovery:** The first day on which the breach is known to the covered entity, or by exercising reasonable diligence would have been known. Knowledge of any workforce member (other than the person committing the breach) is imputed to the covered entity.
- A business associate must notify the covered entity of a breach without unreasonable delay and no later than 60 days from discovery. The covered entity's 60-day clock then starts from when the covered entity is notified.

### Method of Notification
- **Written notice** by first-class mail to the last known address of the individual (or next of kin if the individual is deceased).
- If the individual has agreed to electronic notice, notification may be provided electronically.
- Notification must be in plain language.

### Required Content — 45 CFR 164.404(c)
The notification must include:
1. A brief description of what happened, including the date of the breach and the date of discovery (if known).
2. A description of the types of unsecured PHI involved (such as name, SSN, date of birth, diagnosis, disability code, or other specific types — but not the actual PHI).
3. Any steps individuals should take to protect themselves from potential harm resulting from the breach.
4. A brief description of what the covered entity is doing to investigate the breach, mitigate harm, and protect against further breaches.
5. Contact procedures, including a toll-free telephone number, email address, postal address, or website for individuals to ask questions or obtain additional information.

### Substitute Notice — 45 CFR 164.404(d)
If the covered entity has insufficient or out-of-date contact information for affected individuals:

- **Fewer than 10 individuals:** Alternative individual notice (telephone, email, or other means).
- **10 or more individuals:** Conspicuous posting on the covered entity's home page for at least 90 days OR conspicuous notice in major print or broadcast media in the geographic areas where affected individuals likely reside. Must include a toll-free telephone number that remains active for at least 90 days.

### Urgent Notification
If the covered entity determines that notification requires urgency because of possible imminent misuse of unsecured PHI, the covered entity may provide notification by telephone or other expedited means in addition to written notice.

## HHS Notification — 45 CFR 164.408

### Breaches Involving 500 or More Individuals
- Notification to the Secretary of HHS must be provided **contemporaneously with individual notification** — no later than 60 days from discovery.
- HHS publishes these breaches on its public breach portal (the "Wall of Shame") at https://ocrportal.hhs.gov/ocr/breach/breach_report.jsf.
- HHS may initiate an investigation based on the report.

### Breaches Involving Fewer Than 500 Individuals
- Notification to the Secretary of HHS may be submitted **annually** — no later than 60 days after the end of the calendar year in which the breach was discovered.
- Submitted through the HHS breach reporting portal.

### Required Content for HHS Notification
Same content as individual notification, plus:
- Number of individuals affected (or best estimate if exact count is not known).
- Actions taken in response to the breach.

## Media Notification — 45 CFR 164.406

### Threshold
If a breach affects **500 or more individuals** in a single state or jurisdiction.

### Timeline
Without unreasonable delay and no later than **60 days from discovery** — concurrent with individual notification.

### Method
Notification to prominent media outlets serving the state or jurisdiction (press release, public statement, or direct media contact).

## Law Enforcement Delay — 45 CFR 164.412
If a law enforcement official determines that notification, notice, or posting would impede a criminal investigation or cause damage to national security:
- **Written request from law enforcement:** Delay notification for the time period specified in the written statement.
- **Oral request from law enforcement:** Delay notification for no more than 30 days from the date of the oral statement. The covered entity must document the identity of the official and the date of the request.

## Business Associate Obligations — 45 CFR 164.410
A business associate must notify the covered entity following discovery of a breach of unsecured PHI:
- **Without unreasonable delay** and no later than **60 days from discovery** (or sooner if required by the BAA per [HIPAA-SR-ORG]).
- Must include identification of each individual whose unsecured PHI has been or is reasonably believed to have been breached.
- Must provide any other available information that the covered entity is required to include in its notification to individuals.
- The covered entity retains ultimate responsibility for individual, HHS, and media notification.

## Documentation and Burden of Proof — 45 CFR 164.414(b)
- The covered entity (or business associate) bears the burden of demonstrating that all required notifications were made or that a use or disclosure did not constitute a breach.
- Documentation must be maintained for **6 years** per [HIPAA-PR-ADMIN].
- All risk assessments, notification decisions, notification content, and delivery records must be retained.
