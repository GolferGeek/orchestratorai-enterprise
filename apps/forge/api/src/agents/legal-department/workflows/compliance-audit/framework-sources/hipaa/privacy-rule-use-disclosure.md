# Privacy Rule — Permitted and Required Uses and Disclosures of PHI

## Regulatory Basis
45 CFR 164.502 through 164.514 establish the foundational rules governing when and how covered entities may use or disclose protected health information (PHI).

## Core Principle: General Rule (45 CFR 164.502(a))
A covered entity may NOT use or disclose PHI except as permitted or required by the Privacy Rule. All uses and disclosures not specifically permitted require valid individual authorization per 45 CFR 164.508.

## Required Disclosures (45 CFR 164.502(a)(2))
Only two situations require mandatory disclosure:
1. **To the individual** — when the individual (or personal representative) requests access to their PHI under 45 CFR 164.524 or an accounting of disclosures under 45 CFR 164.528.
2. **To HHS** — for compliance investigations, reviews, or enforcement actions under 45 CFR 160.306.

## Permitted Uses and Disclosures Without Authorization

### Treatment, Payment, and Healthcare Operations (TPO) — 45 CFR 164.506
- **Treatment:** Provision, coordination, or management of health care among providers. No consent required under federal law (state law may impose stricter requirements).
- **Payment:** Activities related to obtaining reimbursement, billing, claims management, utilization review, and determining eligibility or coverage.
- **Healthcare Operations:** Quality assessment, competency assurance, conducting or arranging for medical review, legal and auditing functions, business planning, customer service, and resolution of internal grievances.
- A covered entity may disclose PHI to another covered entity for the recipient's treatment activities. For payment or operations, the recipient must have or have had a relationship with the individual.

### Public Interest and Benefit Activities — 45 CFR 164.512
Twelve categories of permitted disclosures without authorization:
1. **Required by law** (164.512(a)) — court orders, statutes, regulations
2. **Public health activities** (164.512(b)) — reporting to public health authorities, FDA, employer workplace surveillance
3. **Victims of abuse, neglect, or domestic violence** (164.512(c)) — to government authorities authorized to receive such reports
4. **Health oversight activities** (164.512(d)) — audits, investigations, inspections by health oversight agencies
5. **Judicial and administrative proceedings** (164.512(e)) — pursuant to court order or subpoena with satisfactory assurances
6. **Law enforcement purposes** (164.512(f)) — six specific circumstances including court orders, identifying suspects, and reporting crimes on premises
7. **Decedents** (164.512(g)) — to coroners, medical examiners, funeral directors
8. **Cadaveric organ, eye, or tissue donation** (164.512(h))
9. **Research** (164.512(i)) — with IRB/Privacy Board waiver, preparatory to research, or decedent research
10. **Serious threat to health or safety** (164.512(j)) — good faith belief that disclosure is necessary to prevent or lessen a serious and imminent threat
11. **Essential government functions** (164.512(k)) — military, veterans, national security, intelligence, protective services, correctional institutions
12. **Workers' compensation** (164.512(l)) — as authorized by and to the extent necessary to comply with workers' compensation laws

## Minimum Necessary Standard — 45 CFR 164.502(b), 164.514(d)
Covered entities must make reasonable efforts to limit PHI to the minimum necessary to accomplish the intended purpose of the use, disclosure, or request.

### Exceptions to Minimum Necessary:
- Disclosures to or requests by a health care provider for treatment purposes
- Disclosures to the individual who is the subject of the information
- Uses or disclosures made pursuant to a valid authorization under 164.508
- Disclosures to HHS for enforcement purposes
- Uses or disclosures required by law
- Uses or disclosures required for HIPAA compliance

### Implementation Requirements:
- **For uses:** Identify persons or classes of persons who need access to PHI and the categories of PHI needed for each role.
- **For routine disclosures:** Develop standard protocols limiting PHI disclosed to what is reasonably necessary.
- **For non-routine disclosures:** Develop criteria for review on a case-by-case basis and designate responsible reviewers.

## De-identification Standards — 45 CFR 164.514(a)-(c)
De-identified information is NOT PHI and is not subject to HIPAA. Two methods:

### Safe Harbor Method (164.514(b)(2))
Remove ALL 18 identifiers:
1. Names
2. Geographic subdivisions smaller than a state (first 3 digits of ZIP may be retained if the geographic unit contains >20,000 persons)
3. All dates (except year) related to an individual, and ages over 89
4. Telephone numbers
5. Fax numbers
6. Email addresses
7. Social Security numbers
8. Medical record numbers
9. Health plan beneficiary numbers
10. Account numbers
11. Certificate/license numbers
12. Vehicle identifiers and serial numbers
13. Device identifiers and serial numbers
14. Web URLs
15. IP addresses
16. Biometric identifiers (fingerprints, voiceprints)
17. Full-face photographs and comparable images
18. Any other unique identifying number, characteristic, or code

The covered entity must have no actual knowledge that the remaining information could be used alone or in combination to identify an individual.

### Expert Determination Method (164.514(b)(1))
A person with appropriate knowledge of and experience with generally accepted statistical and scientific principles and methods for rendering information not individually identifiable must determine that the risk is very small that the information could be used, alone or in combination, to identify an individual. The expert must document the methods and results.

## Business Associate Requirements — 45 CFR 164.502(e), 164.504(e)
A covered entity may disclose PHI to a business associate and allow the business associate to create, receive, maintain, or transmit PHI on its behalf only if the covered entity obtains satisfactory assurance that the business associate will appropriately safeguard the information. This assurance must be documented in a Business Associate Agreement (BAA). See [HIPAA-SR-ORG] for BAA contract requirements.

## Authorization Requirements — 45 CFR 164.508
Uses and disclosures not otherwise permitted require valid written authorization containing:
- Description of the information to be used or disclosed
- Name of the person authorized to make the disclosure
- Name of the person to whom the disclosure may be made
- Description of the purpose (or statement that it is at the individual's request)
- Expiration date or event
- Individual's signature and date
- If signed by a personal representative, description of authority

### Uses Requiring Authorization Regardless of Purpose:
- Psychotherapy notes (with limited exceptions)
- Marketing communications (unless face-to-face or involving promotional gifts of nominal value)
- Sale of PHI [HIPAA-HITECH]
- Most uses of genetic information for underwriting purposes

- [ ] Implement minimum necessary policies with role-based access definitions
- [ ] Establish protocols for routine vs. non-routine disclosures
- [ ] Create authorization forms meeting all 45 CFR 164.508 requirements
- [ ] Execute BAAs with all business associates per [HIPAA-SR-ORG]
