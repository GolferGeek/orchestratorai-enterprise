# Section 303: Improper Influence on Conduct of Audits

## Overview

Section 303 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act prohibits any officer or director of an issuer, or any other person acting under the direction thereof, from taking any action to fraudulently influence, coerce, manipulate, or mislead any independent public or certified accountant engaged in the performance of an audit or review of the financial statements of that issuer for the purpose of rendering such financial statements materially misleading.

## Statutory Text and Scope

### Prohibition (Section 303(a))
No officer or director of an issuer, or any other person acting under the direction thereof, shall take any action to:
- **Fraudulently influence**
- **Coerce**
- **Manipulate**
- **Mislead**

any independent public or certified accountant engaged in the performance of an audit or review of the financial statements of that issuer for the purpose of rendering such financial statements materially misleading.

### SEC Rule 13b2-2 Implementation
The SEC implemented Section 303 through amendments to Rule 13b2-2 under the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, which provides additional detail on prohibited conduct.

## Persons Subject to the Prohibition

### Direct Actors
- **Officers** of the issuer — including CEO, CFO, COO, General Counsel, Controller, Treasurer, and any other person performing policy-making functions
- **Directors** of the issuer — all members of the board of directors, including independent directors

### Indirect Actors
- **Any person acting under the direction** of an officer or director — this extends the prohibition to subordinate employees, outside consultants, legal counsel, and any agent who acts at the behest of an officer or director
- The "acting under the direction" language is broadly construed to capture any person who is directed, instructed, or requested by an officer or director to exert improper influence on the auditor

### Persons NOT Directly Covered
- The statute does not directly apply to persons who act on their own initiative without direction from an officer or director (though such persons may be subject to other anti-fraud provisions)
- The auditor itself is not subject to Section 303 (auditor misconduct is addressed by PCAOB disciplinary proceedings and other SEC enforcement tools)

## Elements of a Violation

### 1. Action Taken
The person must take some affirmative **action** — passive non-disclosure alone is generally insufficient to establish a Section 303 violation (though it may violate other provisions such as Section 13(b)(5) regarding books and records)

### 2. Fraudulent Intent
The action must be taken **fraudulently** — meaning with knowledge of the falsity or with reckless disregard for the truth. The SEC need not prove that the financial statements were actually rendered materially misleading, but must show that the action was taken for that purpose.

### 3. Directed at the Auditor
The action must be directed at an **independent public or certified accountant** engaged in performing an audit or review. This includes:
- The lead audit partner and engagement team
- Concurring review partner
- Any member of the audit firm performing audit or review procedures
- Specialists engaged by the audit firm (e.g., valuation specialists, IT audit specialists)

### 4. Purpose of Rendering Financial Statements Materially Misleading
The SEC must establish that the purpose of the improper influence was to render the issuer's financial statements **materially misleading**. This includes:
- Causing the auditor to issue an unqualified opinion when a qualification is warranted
- Causing the auditor to overlook or not report material misstatements
- Causing the auditor to accept inappropriate accounting treatments
- Causing the auditor to fail to communicate material weaknesses or significant deficiencies to the audit committee

## Examples of Prohibited Conduct

### Direct Pressure on Audit Judgments
- Threatening to terminate the auditor if the auditor does not accept management's preferred accounting treatment
- Conditioning audit fees on favorable audit outcomes
- Directing auditors not to perform procedures in specific areas where misstatements exist
- Pressuring the auditor to agree with management's estimates or valuations that lack reasonable support

### Withholding or Falsifying Information
- Directing employees to provide false or incomplete responses to auditor inquiries
- Instructing employees to deny the auditor access to documents, records, or personnel
- Creating fictitious documents or records for the auditor to review
- Directing the destruction or alteration of documents prior to auditor review (also violates [SOX-SEC-802])

### Indirect Pressure
- Using the auditor's desire to retain the engagement as leverage to influence audit conclusions
- Engaging in ex parte communications with audit team members outside the presence of the lead partner, designed to influence their judgment
- Offering personal benefits (employment, consulting arrangements) to individual audit team members to influence their objectivity

### Manipulation of Scope or Timing
- Directing the auditor to limit the scope of testing in areas where management knows misstatements exist
- Artificially accelerating or delaying transactions to move them outside the audit period
- Reclassifying items to avoid audit scrutiny in specific accounts

## Relationship to Other SOX Provisions

### Section 201 — Auditor Independence [SOX-SEC-201]
Section 303 complements Section 201 by addressing the demand side of independence threats. While Section 201 restricts what services the auditor may provide, Section 303 restricts how management may interact with the auditor during the engagement.

### Section 302 — CEO/CFO Certifications [SOX-SEC-302]
The CEO and CFO certify that they have disclosed all significant deficiencies and fraud to the auditors. Improper influence under Section 303 would directly undermine these certifications and could expose the certifying officers to civil and criminal liability.

### Section 802 — Document Destruction [SOX-SEC-802]
Destroying documents to prevent the auditor from discovering misstatements constitutes both improper influence under Section 303 and obstruction under Section 802.

### Section 906 — Criminal Certifications [SOX-SEC-906]
If improper influence results in materially misleading financial statements, the CEO/CFO's criminal certifications under Section 906 become the basis for potential criminal prosecution.

## Penalties

### SEC Civil Enforcement
- Cease-and-desist orders under Sections 21C of the Exchange Act
- Civil money penalties under Section 21(d): up to $181,071 per violation for individuals (as adjusted)
- Injunctions prohibiting future violations
- Officer and director bars — prohibition from serving as officer or director of a public company
- Disgorgement of ill-gotten gains

### Criminal Referral
- Willful violations may be referred to the Department of Justice for criminal prosecution
- Potential charges under 18 U.S.C. Section 1520 (destruction of audit records) and Section 1519 (obstruction)
- Potential charges under the general securities fraud provisions of Section 10(b) and Rule 10b-5

## SEC Enforcement Precedent

The SEC has brought enforcement actions under Section 303 and its implementing rules in cases involving:
- Executives who pressured auditors to accept aggressive revenue recognition policies
- Officers who directed subordinates to withhold loan default information from auditors
- Directors who threatened to replace the audit firm if it did not accept management's goodwill impairment analysis
- Controllers who fabricated journal entries and concealed them from the audit team

## Compliance Checklist

- [ ] Establish a written policy prohibiting improper influence on auditors
- [ ] Include Section 303 compliance in officer and director training programs
- [ ] Ensure the audit committee has direct, unimpeded access to the external auditor
- [ ] Maintain a clear reporting channel for auditors to report pressure or improper influence attempts
- [ ] Prohibit audit fee negotiations from being tied to audit outcomes
- [ ] Restrict management communications with auditors to authorized personnel
- [ ] Document all significant communications between management and the external auditor
- [ ] Include Section 303 awareness in the issuer's code of ethics
- [ ] Audit committee should privately meet with external auditors without management present at least quarterly
