# GDPR Articles 40-43 — Codes of Conduct and Certification Mechanisms

## Article 40 — Codes of Conduct

### Article 40(1) — Encouragement

Member States, the supervisory authorities, the European Data Protection Board, and the Commission shall encourage the drawing up of codes of conduct intended to contribute to the proper application of the GDPR, taking account of the specific features of the various processing sectors and the specific needs of micro, small, and medium-sized enterprises.

### Article 40(2) — Scope of Codes

Associations and other bodies representing categories of controllers or processors may prepare codes of conduct, amend, or extend such codes for the purpose of specifying the application of the GDPR, including with regard to:

**(a)** Fair and transparent processing
**(b)** The legitimate interests pursued by controllers in specific contexts
**(c)** The collection of personal data
**(d)** The pseudonymization of personal data
**(e)** The information provided to the public and to data subjects
**(f)** The exercise of the rights of data subjects
**(g)** The information provided to, and the protection of, children, and the manner in which consent of the holders of parental responsibility is to be obtained
**(h)** Technical and organizational measures, including data protection by design and by default and security measures
**(i)** Breach notification
**(j)** Transfer of personal data to third countries
**(k)** Out-of-court proceedings and other dispute resolution procedures

### Article 40(4)-(5) — Approval Process

A code of conduct must:
- Provide sufficient appropriate safeguards, including enforceable data subject rights
- Be submitted to the competent supervisory authority for approval
- If relating to processing in several Member States, be submitted to the EDPB for an opinion before supervisory authority approval

### Article 40(6) — Registration

Approved codes of conduct are registered and published by the supervisory authority and the EDPB.

## Article 41 — Monitoring of Approved Codes of Conduct

### Article 41(1) — Accredited Monitoring Bodies

Without prejudice to the tasks and powers of the competent supervisory authority, the monitoring of compliance with a code of conduct may be carried out by a body which has an appropriate level of expertise and is accredited for that purpose by the competent supervisory authority.

### Article 41(2) — Accreditation Requirements

A body may be accredited to monitor compliance with a code of conduct if it:
**(a)** Has demonstrated independence and expertise in relation to the subject-matter of the code
**(b)** Has established procedures to assess the eligibility of controllers and processors to apply the code, monitor compliance, and periodically review its operation
**(c)** Has established procedures and structures to handle complaints about infringements or the manner in which the code is implemented
**(d)** Demonstrates that its tasks and duties do not result in a conflict of interests

### Article 41(4) — Enforcement by Monitoring Bodies

A monitoring body shall take appropriate action in cases of code infringement, including suspension or exclusion of the controller or processor concerned from the code. It shall inform the competent supervisory authority of such actions and the reasons for them.

## Article 42 — Certification

### Article 42(1) — Certification Mechanisms

Member States, the supervisory authorities, the EDPB, and the Commission shall encourage the establishment of data protection certification mechanisms, data protection seals, and data protection marks for the purpose of demonstrating compliance with the GDPR of processing operations by controllers and processors.

### Article 42(3) — Voluntary Nature

Certification shall be voluntary and available via a process that is transparent.

### Article 42(4) — Scope

Certification does not reduce the responsibility of the controller or processor for compliance with the GDPR. Certification is granted by certification bodies under Article 43 or by the competent supervisory authority based on criteria approved by the competent supervisory authority or by the EDPB.

### Article 42(5) — Obligations of Certified Controllers/Processors

A controller or processor which submits its processing to the certification mechanism shall provide the certification body or the supervisory authority with all information and access to its processing activities which are necessary to conduct the certification procedure.

### Article 42(7) — Duration and Renewal

Certification shall be issued for a maximum period of three years and may be renewed under the same conditions, provided that the relevant requirements continue to be met. Certification shall be withdrawn by the certifying body or supervisory authority where the requirements are no longer met.

### Using Certification to Demonstrate Compliance

Certification may be used as an element to demonstrate compliance with:
- Article 24(3): Controller obligations
- Article 25(3): Data protection by design and by default
- Article 28(5): Processor guarantees
- Article 32(3): Security of processing
- Article 46(2)(f): Appropriate safeguards for international transfers

## Article 43 — Certification Bodies

### Article 43(1) — Accreditation Requirements

Certification bodies must:
- Demonstrate independence and expertise
- Have submitted themselves to accreditation by the competent supervisory authority or the national accreditation body
- Committed themselves to approved certification criteria under Article 42(5)
- Established procedures for issuing, reviewing, and withdrawing certification

### Article 43(2) — Accreditation Process

Certification bodies are accredited based on:
**(a)** Independence and expertise in data protection
**(b)** Commitment to approved certification criteria
**(c)** Established procedures for issuing, reviewing, and withdrawing certification
**(d)** Established procedures and structures for handling complaints
**(e)** Demonstration that tasks do not result in conflicts of interests

## Enforcement

Violations of Articles 42-43 (certification bodies) are subject to the lower tier of administrative fines under Article 83(4)(b): up to EUR 10,000,000 or 2% of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher.
