# Section 806: Whistleblower Protections for Employees of Publicly Traded Companies

## Overview

Section 806 of the Sarbanes-Oxley Act (codified at 18 U.S.C. Section 1514A) provides civil protections against retaliation for employees of publicly traded companies who report suspected violations of federal securities laws, SEC rules and regulations, or federal anti-fraud statutes. This provision is designed to encourage internal reporting of corporate misconduct by ensuring that employees who come forward are protected from adverse employment actions.

## Protected Activities

### Categories of Protected Conduct
No company with a class of securities registered under Section 12 of the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, or that is required to file reports under Section 15(d), or any officer, employee, contractor, subcontractor, or agent of such company, may discharge, demote, suspend, threaten, harass, or in any other manner discriminate against an employee in the terms and conditions of employment because of any lawful act done by the employee to:

#### 1. Provide Information to Federal Agencies
Provide information, cause information to be provided, or otherwise assist in an investigation regarding any conduct which the employee **reasonably believes** constitutes a violation of:
- Section 1341 (mail fraud)
- Section 1343 (wire fraud)
- Section 1344 (bank fraud)
- Section 1348 (securities fraud)
- Any rule or regulation of the Securities and Exchange Commission
- Any provision of federal law relating to fraud against shareholders

The information must be provided to:
- A federal regulatory or law enforcement agency
- Any member of Congress or any committee of Congress

#### 2. Provide Information to Supervisors
Provide information or assist in an investigation regarding the same categories of suspected violations when the information is provided to a person with supervisory authority over the employee (or such other person working for the employer who has the authority to investigate, discover, or terminate misconduct).

#### 3. File, Testify, or Participate in Proceedings
File, cause to be filed, testify, participate in, or otherwise assist in a proceeding filed or about to be filed (with any knowledge of the employer) relating to an alleged violation of the enumerated statutes.

### Reasonable Belief Standard
The employee need not prove that an actual violation occurred. The employee must demonstrate:
- A **subjective belief** that the conduct constituted a violation of one of the enumerated provisions
- That the belief was **objectively reasonable** — a reasonable person in the same factual circumstances and with the same training and experience would believe the conduct constituted a violation
- The belief need not be correct — the protection attaches to reasonable, good-faith reports even if the underlying conduct is ultimately determined not to be a violation

## Prohibited Retaliatory Actions

### Forms of Retaliation
The following adverse employment actions are prohibited when taken because of protected activity:
- **Discharge** — termination of employment
- **Demotion** — reduction in title, responsibilities, or grade
- **Suspension** — with or without pay
- **Threats** — verbal or written threats of adverse action
- **Harassment** — workplace harassment designed to intimidate or discourage protected activity
- **Discrimination** — any other manner of discrimination in the terms and conditions of employment

### Broad Interpretation
Courts have interpreted "any other manner of discrimination" broadly to include:
- Negative performance evaluations motivated by protected activity
- Transfer to less desirable positions or locations
- Reduction in work hours or denial of overtime
- Exclusion from meetings, projects, or professional development opportunities
- Constructive discharge (creating intolerable working conditions that force resignation)
- Blacklisting or providing negative references to prospective employers

### Covered Employers and Individuals
- **Companies**: Any company with securities registered under Section 12 or required to file reports under Section 15(d) of the Exchange Act, including subsidiaries and affiliates whose financial information is included in the registrant's consolidated financial statements
- **Individual liability**: Officers, employees, contractors, subcontractors, and agents of covered companies may be individually liable for retaliatory actions (confirmed by *Lawson v. FMR LLC*, 571 U.S. 429 (2014))
- **Contractors and subcontractors**: Employees of contractors and subcontractors of public companies are covered (per *Lawson*)

## Filing Procedures and Timelines

### Administrative Complaint — OSHA
1. **Filing deadline**: The complaint must be filed with the Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) within **180 days** of the date of the alleged retaliatory action
2. **Contents**: The complaint should identify the protected activity, the adverse action, and the causal connection between the two
3. **OSHA investigation**: OSHA will investigate the complaint and may interview witnesses, review documents, and request a position statement from the employer
4. **OSHA determination**: OSHA will issue preliminary findings within 60 days of the complaint
5. **Preliminary reinstatement**: If OSHA finds reasonable cause to believe a violation occurred, it may order **preliminary reinstatement** of the employee pending final adjudication

### Administrative Hearing — ALJ
6. Either party may request a hearing before an **Administrative Law Judge (ALJ)** of the Department of Labor within 30 days of OSHA's determination
7. The ALJ conducts a de novo hearing with full evidentiary procedures
8. The ALJ issues a decision and order, which may be appealed to the Administrative Review Board (ARB)

### Federal Court — Kick-Out Provision
9. If the Department of Labor has not issued a **final decision within 180 days** of the filing of the complaint (and the delay is not due to the complainant's bad faith), the complainant may **bring an action at law or equity** in the appropriate federal district court
10. This "kick-out" provision ensures that prolonged administrative delays do not deny the complainant access to judicial relief
11. The complainant is entitled to a **jury trial** on the merits of the retaliation claim in federal court

## Remedies

### Available Relief
An employee prevailing on a Section 806 claim is entitled to all relief necessary to make the employee whole, including:

#### Reinstatement
- Reinstatement to the same position with the same seniority status the employee would have had but for the retaliation
- If reinstatement is not feasible (e.g., the relationship is irreparably damaged), front pay may be awarded as a substitute

#### Back Pay with Interest
- Compensation for all lost wages and benefits from the date of the adverse action to the date of reinstatement or judgment
- Includes regular pay, overtime, bonuses, commissions, stock options, retirement contributions, health insurance, and all other employment benefits
- Interest calculated at the rate applicable to underpayment of taxes under 26 U.S.C. Section 6621

#### Compensatory Damages
- Damages for emotional distress, pain and suffering, reputational harm, and other non-economic injuries resulting from the retaliation
- No statutory cap on compensatory damages under Section 806

#### Litigation Costs
- Reasonable **attorneys' fees**
- Expert witness fees
- **Litigation costs** including filing fees, deposition costs, and other reasonable expenses

### Burden of Proof
- **Employee's burden**: The employee must demonstrate by a preponderance of the evidence that (1) the employee engaged in protected activity, (2) the employer knew about the protected activity, (3) the employee suffered an adverse employment action, and (4) the protected activity was a **contributing factor** in the adverse action
- **Employer's defense**: If the employee meets the contributing factor standard, the employer may avoid liability only by demonstrating by **clear and convincing evidence** that it would have taken the same adverse action in the absence of the protected activity

## Dodd-Frank Act Extensions (Section 922)

### SEC Whistleblower Bounty Program
The Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act of 2010 (Section 21F of the Exchange Act) created an SEC whistleblower bounty program that complements SOX Section 806:

#### Bounty Amount
- **10% to 30%** of monetary sanctions collected by the SEC in any judicial or administrative action where sanctions exceed **$1,000,000**
- The percentage is determined by the SEC based on factors including significance of the information, degree of assistance, and the SEC's programmatic interest in deterring violations

#### Enhanced Anti-Retaliation (Dodd-Frank Section 922)
- Extends the filing deadline for retaliation claims to **6 years** from the date of retaliation (or 3 years after the employee knew or should have known of the violation, whichever is earlier, but no later than 10 years)
- Provides for **double back pay** in retaliation cases
- Creates a private right of action in federal court (no requirement to exhaust OSHA administrative remedies)
- Protects individuals who report directly to the SEC, even without internal reporting first

#### Interaction with SOX Section 806
- An employee may pursue both a SOX Section 806 administrative complaint and a Dodd-Frank retaliation claim simultaneously
- The Dodd-Frank program provides additional financial incentives for reporting to the SEC
- SOX Section 806 remains the primary vehicle for employees who report internally or to law enforcement agencies other than the SEC

## Relationship to Audit Committee Whistleblower Procedures [SOX-SEC-301]

Section 806 protections work in concert with the audit committee complaint procedures required by Section 301. Employees who use the Section 301 anonymous reporting channels to report accounting or auditing concerns are engaging in protected activity under Section 806. The audit committee must ensure that:
- Complaint procedures are widely publicized and accessible
- Confidentiality of complainants is maintained
- No retaliatory action is taken or tolerated against complainants
- Complaints are investigated and resolved in a timely manner

## Compliance Checklist

- [ ] Establish and publicize whistleblower reporting channels (hotline, web portal, email)
- [ ] Ensure reporting channels allow confidential, anonymous submissions
- [ ] Train all managers and supervisors on prohibited retaliatory actions
- [ ] Include anti-retaliation policies in the company's code of ethics and employee handbook
- [ ] Implement procedures to investigate retaliation complaints promptly and independently
- [ ] Maintain documentation of all whistleblower reports, investigations, and outcomes
- [ ] Coordinate with audit committee on complaint receipt and tracking [SOX-SEC-301]
- [ ] Ensure legal counsel reviews all proposed adverse actions against employees who have engaged in protected activity within the prior 180 days
- [ ] Monitor OSHA and DOL determinations and respond within required timelines
- [ ] Train HR personnel on the contributing factor/clear and convincing evidence burden of proof framework
- [ ] Review and update anti-retaliation policies annually to reflect regulatory changes
- [ ] Consider voluntary participation in the SEC's whistleblower bounty program awareness initiatives
