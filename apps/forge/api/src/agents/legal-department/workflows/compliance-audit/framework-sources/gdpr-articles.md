# GDPR — Key Compliance Requirements

Paraphrased requirement summaries for RAG ingestion. Each section represents
a distinct compliance area chunked at article boundaries for retrieval quality.

---

## Article 5 — Principles of Data Processing

Organizations must process personal data lawfully, fairly, and transparently. Data must be collected for specified, explicit, and legitimate purposes and not further processed in a manner incompatible with those purposes. Personal data must be adequate, relevant, and limited to what is necessary. Data must be accurate and kept up to date. Personal data must be kept in a form that permits identification for no longer than necessary. Data must be processed with appropriate security measures including protection against unauthorized or unlawful processing, accidental loss, destruction, or damage.

Key requirements:
- Lawfulness, fairness, and transparency in all processing activities
- Purpose limitation — data collected only for specified legitimate purposes
- Data minimization — only collect what is necessary
- Accuracy — keep data up to date, rectify inaccuracies without delay
- Storage limitation — define and enforce retention periods
- Integrity and confidentiality — appropriate technical and organizational security measures

---

## Article 6 — Lawful Basis for Processing

Every processing activity must have a documented legal basis. The six lawful bases are: consent of the data subject, performance of a contract, compliance with a legal obligation, protection of vital interests, performance of a task carried out in the public interest, and legitimate interests pursued by the controller or a third party.

Key requirements:
- Document the legal basis for each processing activity
- Where relying on legitimate interests, conduct and document a balancing test
- Where relying on consent, ensure it meets the conditions in Article 7
- Be able to demonstrate which lawful basis applies to each processing operation

## Article 7 — Conditions for Consent

When processing is based on consent, the controller must be able to demonstrate that the data subject has consented. Consent requests must be presented clearly and in plain language, distinguishable from other matters. The data subject has the right to withdraw consent at any time, and withdrawal must be as easy as giving consent. When assessing whether consent is freely given, account must be taken of whether performance of a contract is conditional on consent to processing not necessary for that contract.

Key requirements:
- Ability to demonstrate consent was given
- Consent request presented in clear, plain language
- Consent clearly distinguishable from other terms and conditions
- Right to withdraw consent at any time
- Withdrawal must be as easy as giving consent
- Consent must be freely given, not bundled with unrelated contract terms

## Article 9 — Processing of Special Categories of Data

Processing of data revealing racial or ethnic origin, political opinions, religious or philosophical beliefs, trade union membership, genetic data, biometric data for identification, health data, or data concerning sex life or sexual orientation is prohibited unless a specific exception applies. Exceptions include explicit consent, employment and social security obligations, vital interests, legitimate activities of certain bodies, data made public by the subject, legal claims, substantial public interest, health or social care purposes, public health, and archiving or research purposes.

Key requirements:
- Identify any special category data in processing activities
- Document the specific exception that permits processing
- Implement additional safeguards for special category data
- Where relying on explicit consent, ensure heightened consent requirements are met

## Articles 12-14 — Transparency and Information Obligations

Controllers must provide information about data processing in a concise, transparent, intelligible, and easily accessible form using clear and plain language. At the time data is obtained from the data subject, the controller must provide: identity and contact details of the controller, contact details of the data protection officer, purposes and legal basis, recipients or categories of recipients, details of international transfers, retention period, data subject rights, right to withdraw consent, right to lodge a complaint, whether provision of data is a statutory or contractual requirement, and the existence of automated decision-making including profiling.

Key requirements:
- Privacy notice provided at the time of data collection
- Information presented in clear, plain, accessible language
- All required elements included in the privacy notice
- Separate notice required when data obtained from third parties (Article 14)
- Information provided within one month when data not obtained from the subject

## Articles 15-22 — Data Subject Rights

Data subjects have the right of access to their personal data and information about its processing. They have the right to rectification of inaccurate data. They have the right to erasure (right to be forgotten) under certain conditions. They have the right to restrict processing in certain circumstances. Controllers must notify recipients of any rectification, erasure, or restriction. Data subjects have the right to data portability in a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format. Data subjects have the right to object to processing based on legitimate interests or public interest, including profiling. Data subjects have rights related to automated individual decision-making, including profiling that produces legal or similarly significant effects.

Key requirements:
- Process for handling access requests within one month
- Ability to rectify inaccurate personal data
- Process for handling erasure requests and determining applicable grounds
- Ability to restrict processing when requested
- Notification of recipients when data is rectified, erased, or restricted
- Ability to export personal data in a portable format
- Process for handling objections to processing
- Safeguards for automated decision-making with significant effects

## Article 25 — Data Protection by Design and Default

The controller must implement appropriate technical and organizational measures designed to implement data protection principles effectively and integrate necessary safeguards into processing. This applies both at the time of determining the means for processing and at the time of processing itself. By default, only personal data necessary for each specific purpose should be processed, applying to the amount of data collected, the extent of processing, the period of storage, and accessibility.

Key requirements:
- Privacy impact considered at the design stage of systems and processes
- Data protection principles built into system architecture
- Default settings configured to minimize data collection and processing
- Access to personal data limited to those who need it for processing
- Regular review of privacy-by-design measures

## Articles 28-29 — Processor Obligations

Processing by a processor must be governed by a contract or legal act that sets out the subject matter, duration, nature, and purpose of processing, the type of personal data, categories of data subjects, and obligations and rights of the controller. The processor must process data only on documented instructions from the controller, ensure persons authorized to process have committed to confidentiality, take all required security measures, assist the controller with data subject requests, assist with breach notification and impact assessments, delete or return all personal data after the end of services, and make available all information necessary to demonstrate compliance.

Key requirements:
- Written data processing agreement with all processors
- Agreement includes all required contractual terms
- Processor processes data only on documented instructions
- Processor implements appropriate security measures
- Sub-processor engagement requires prior authorization
- Processor assists controller with compliance obligations

---

## Articles 32-34 — Security and Breach Notification

Controllers and processors must implement appropriate technical and organizational measures to ensure a level of security appropriate to the risk, including pseudonymization and encryption, the ability to ensure ongoing confidentiality, integrity, availability, and resilience of systems, the ability to restore availability and access to data in a timely manner, and a process for regularly testing and evaluating security measures.

In case of a personal data breach, the controller must notify the supervisory authority within 72 hours of becoming aware of it, unless the breach is unlikely to result in a risk to individuals. When the breach is likely to result in a high risk to individuals, the controller must communicate the breach to the affected data subjects without undue delay.

Key requirements:
- Risk-based security measures including encryption and pseudonymization
- Ability to ensure confidentiality, integrity, availability, and resilience
- Business continuity and disaster recovery capabilities
- Regular testing and evaluation of security measures
- Breach detection and response procedures
- Ability to notify supervisory authority within 72 hours
- Process for communicating high-risk breaches to affected individuals
- Breach documentation and record-keeping

## Articles 35-36 — Data Protection Impact Assessment

Where processing is likely to result in a high risk to individuals, the controller must carry out a data protection impact assessment before processing begins. This is required for systematic and extensive evaluation of personal aspects based on automated processing including profiling, large-scale processing of special categories of data, and systematic monitoring of publicly accessible areas on a large scale. The assessment must include a description of the processing and its purposes, an assessment of necessity and proportionality, an assessment of risks to individuals, and the measures to address those risks.

Key requirements:
- Process for identifying high-risk processing activities
- DPIA conducted before high-risk processing begins
- DPIA includes all required elements (description, necessity assessment, risk assessment, mitigation measures)
- Consultation with supervisory authority when DPIA indicates high residual risk
- DPIA reviewed and updated when processing changes

---

## Articles 37-39 — Data Protection Officer

Controllers and processors must designate a data protection officer where processing is carried out by a public authority, where core activities consist of large-scale systematic monitoring of individuals, or where core activities consist of large-scale processing of special categories of data. The DPO must be provided with resources necessary to carry out their tasks and maintain expert knowledge. The DPO must be involved in all issues relating to the protection of personal data, report to the highest management level, and not receive instructions regarding the exercise of their tasks.

Key requirements:
- Assessment of whether DPO appointment is required
- DPO has expert knowledge of data protection law and practices
- DPO provided with necessary resources
- DPO involved in all data protection matters
- DPO reports to highest management level
- DPO operates independently without instructions on task exercise
- DPO contact details published and communicated to supervisory authority

---

## Articles 44-49 — International Data Transfers

Transfers of personal data to third countries or international organizations may only take place if the controller and processor comply with the conditions for transfer. Transfers may be based on an adequacy decision by the European Commission, appropriate safeguards such as standard contractual clauses or binding corporate rules, or specific derogations such as explicit consent, contractual necessity, or important public interest.

Key requirements:
- Identify all international data transfers
- Document the transfer mechanism for each transfer
- Where relying on standard contractual clauses, ensure they are properly executed
- Where relying on binding corporate rules, ensure approval by supervisory authority
- Conduct transfer impact assessments where required
- Implement supplementary measures where standard safeguards are insufficient

---

## Articles 82-84 — Liability and Penalties

Any person who has suffered material or non-material damage as a result of an infringement has the right to receive compensation from the controller or processor. Administrative fines for infringements can be up to 10 million euros or 2 percent of annual worldwide turnover for certain violations, and up to 20 million euros or 4 percent of annual worldwide turnover for more serious violations including violations of the basic principles for processing, data subject rights, and international transfer conditions.

Key requirements:
- Awareness of potential liability for data protection violations
- Understanding of the two tiers of administrative fines
- Documentation and compliance measures to mitigate risk of penalties
- Liability allocation in processor agreements
