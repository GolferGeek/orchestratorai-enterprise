# GDPR Article 7 — Conditions for Consent

## Article 7(1) — Demonstrating Consent

Where processing is based on consent, the controller shall be able to demonstrate that the data subject has consented to processing of their personal data. The burden of proof rests entirely with the controller.

Demonstration requirements:
- Maintain records of when and how consent was obtained
- Record the specific information provided to the data subject at the time of consent
- Record the mechanism used to obtain consent (e.g., online form, written document, verbal with recording)
- Retain evidence that the consent was freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous
- Ensure records are sufficient to withstand regulatory scrutiny

## Article 7(2) — Written Declarations

If the data subject's consent is given in the context of a written declaration which also concerns other matters, the request for consent must be:

- Presented in a manner clearly distinguishable from the other matters
- Presented in an intelligible and easily accessible form
- Written using clear and plain language

Any part of a consent declaration that infringes the GDPR is not binding. Consent buried in terms and conditions, or combined with other unrelated agreements, does not meet this requirement.

## Article 7(3) — Right to Withdraw Consent

The data subject has the right to withdraw consent at any time. Requirements:

- Withdrawal must be as easy as giving consent — if consent was given with a single click, withdrawal must not require calling a phone number or writing a letter
- The data subject must be informed of the right to withdraw before giving consent
- Withdrawal does not affect the lawfulness of processing based on consent before withdrawal
- Controllers must implement technical mechanisms to facilitate easy withdrawal
- Upon withdrawal, processing must cease unless another lawful basis applies

## Article 7(4) — Freely Given Consent

When assessing whether consent is freely given, utmost account shall be taken of whether the performance of a contract, including the provision of a service, is conditional on consent to the processing of personal data that is not necessary for the performance of that contract.

Consent is NOT freely given when:
- There is a clear imbalance between the data subject and the controller (e.g., employer-employee, public authority-citizen)
- Consent is bundled — the data subject cannot consent to one processing operation without consenting to others
- Consent is a precondition — service is refused if consent is not given for non-essential processing
- There is detriment for refusing — the data subject faces negative consequences for declining consent
- Granularity is not offered — consent is all-or-nothing rather than purpose-specific

## Recital Guidance

Recital 32 clarifies that consent should be given by:
- A clear affirmative act establishing freely given, specific, informed, and unambiguous agreement
- A written statement, including by electronic means, or an oral statement
- Ticking a box when visiting a website, choosing technical settings, or another statement or conduct clearly indicating acceptance
- Silence, pre-ticked boxes, or inactivity do NOT constitute consent

Recital 42 clarifies that for consent to be informed, the data subject should be aware of at least:
- The identity of the controller
- The purposes of each processing operation for which consent is sought

Recital 43 clarifies that consent is presumed not freely given when:
- It does not allow separate consent for different processing operations
- The performance of a contract is made dependent on consent despite not being necessary for performance

## Enforcement

Violations of consent conditions are subject to the higher tier of administrative fines under Article 83(5)(a): up to EUR 20,000,000 or 4% of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher.
