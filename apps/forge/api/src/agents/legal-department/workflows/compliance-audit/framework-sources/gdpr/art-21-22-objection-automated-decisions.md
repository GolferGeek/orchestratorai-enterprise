# GDPR Articles 21-22 — Right to Object and Automated Decision-Making

## Article 21 — Right to Object

### Article 21(1) — General Right to Object

The data subject has the right to object, on grounds relating to their particular situation, at any time to processing of personal data concerning them which is based on:
- Article 6(1)(e) — public interest or official authority
- Article 6(1)(f) — legitimate interests

This includes profiling based on those provisions. Upon objection, the controller shall no longer process the personal data unless the controller demonstrates compelling legitimate grounds for the processing which override the interests, rights, and freedoms of the data subject, or for the establishment, exercise, or defense of legal claims.

The burden of proof shifts to the controller — the controller must demonstrate "compelling" grounds, not merely "legitimate" grounds. This is a higher threshold than the Article 6(1)(f) balancing test.

### Article 21(2) — Direct Marketing

Where personal data is processed for direct marketing purposes, the data subject has the right to object at any time to processing of personal data concerning them for such marketing, which includes profiling to the extent that it is related to such direct marketing.

This right is absolute — there are no grounds on which the controller can refuse. Once a data subject objects to direct marketing, processing must cease immediately.

### Article 21(3) — Effect of Direct Marketing Objection

Where the data subject objects to processing for direct marketing purposes, the personal data shall no longer be processed for such purposes.

### Article 21(4) — Information Requirement

At the latest at the time of the first communication with the data subject, the right to object must be explicitly brought to the attention of the data subject and presented clearly and separately from any other information.

### Article 21(5) — Information Society Services

In the context of the use of information society services, the data subject may exercise their right to object by automated means using technical specifications (e.g., browser do-not-track signals, privacy preference signals).

### Article 21(6) — Research and Statistics Exception

Where data is processed for scientific or historical research purposes or statistical purposes under Article 89(1), the data subject has the right to object on grounds relating to their particular situation, unless the processing is necessary for the performance of a task carried out for reasons of public interest.

## Article 22 — Automated Individual Decision-Making, Including Profiling

### Article 22(1) — General Prohibition

The data subject has the right not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing, including profiling, which produces legal effects concerning them or similarly significantly affects them.

"Legal effects" include decisions about:
- Credit applications (approval, rejection, terms)
- Employment (hiring, promotion, termination)
- Insurance (coverage decisions, premium calculations)
- Government benefits (eligibility determinations)

"Similarly significantly affects" includes decisions about:
- Automated pricing affecting access to essential services
- Targeted advertising that manipulates behavior
- Automated content moderation affecting freedom of expression

### Article 22(2) — Exceptions

The prohibition does not apply if the decision is:

#### (a) Contract Performance
Necessary for entering into, or performance of, a contract between the data subject and a controller. The decision must be genuinely necessary — not merely convenient.

#### (b) Authorized by Law
Authorized by Union or Member State law to which the controller is subject and which also lays down suitable measures to safeguard the data subject's rights, freedoms, and legitimate interests.

#### (c) Explicit Consent
Based on the data subject's explicit consent.

### Article 22(3) — Safeguards

In the cases referred to in Article 22(2)(a) and (c), the controller shall implement suitable measures to safeguard the data subject's rights, freedoms, and legitimate interests, at least the right to:
- Obtain human intervention on the part of the controller
- Express their point of view
- Contest the decision

### Article 22(4) — Special Category Data

Decisions referred to in Article 22(2) shall not be based on special categories of personal data under Article 9(1), unless Article 9(2)(a) (explicit consent) or 9(2)(g) (substantial public interest) applies and suitable measures to safeguard the data subject's rights, freedoms, and legitimate interests are in place.

### Profiling Definition

Profiling means any form of automated processing of personal data consisting of the use of personal data to evaluate certain personal aspects relating to a natural person, in particular to analyze or predict aspects concerning that natural person's:
- Performance at work
- Economic situation
- Health
- Personal preferences
- Interests
- Reliability
- Behavior
- Location
- Movements

## Implementation Requirements

Organizations using automated decision-making must:
- Identify all processes involving solely automated decisions with significant effects
- Implement human review mechanisms for each identified process
- Provide meaningful information about the logic of automated decisions in privacy notices
- Enable data subjects to request human intervention
- Document the safeguards implemented
- Conduct DPIAs under Article 35 for systematic profiling with significant effects

## Enforcement

Violations of Articles 21-22 are subject to the higher tier of administrative fines under Article 83(5)(b): up to EUR 20,000,000 or 4% of total worldwide annual turnover, whichever is higher.
